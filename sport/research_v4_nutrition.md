## 1. DIETARY FATS

### Claim: Low-Fat Diet Suppresses Testosterone

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — high]`

A low-fat diet (~25% of energy from fat vs. ~40%) reduces total serum testosterone in healthy men by approximately 15% (from 22.7 to 19.3 nmol/L, p<0.001). A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis of 6 RCTs (N=206) confirmed this, finding a standardized mean difference (SMD) of -0.38 (95% CI -0.75 to -0.01, p=0.04) for total testosterone on low-fat vs. high-fat diets. The effect is stronger in men of European ancestry (SMD -0.52, 95% CI -0.75 to -0.30).

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Hamalainen 1984: -15% total testosterone (-3.4 nmol/L, ~-98 ng/dL) — crosses Tier 1 threshold (>15% change)
- Whittaker & Wu 2021 meta-analysis: SMD -0.38 (95% CI -0.75 to -0.01) total testosterone; DHT SMD -0.30 (95% CI -0.56 to -0.03)

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

Do not drop dietary fat below ~25% of total calories during a cut. At 2,200 kcal deficit maintenance, that is a floor of approximately 61 g fat/day. Dropping to 20% or below risks a clinically meaningful testosterone suppression of ~15%, which in a deficit — where cortisol is already elevated and androgen output already suppressed — compounds the hormonal load. Prioritize unsaturated fats but do not eliminate saturated fat entirely.

**Sources:**
- PMID 6538617 — Hamalainen 1984, J Steroid Biochem. Crossover dietary intervention, N=30 healthy men, 6 weeks, 40% fat vs. 25% fat diet.
- PMID 33741447 — Whittaker & Wu 2021, J Steroid Biochem Mol Biol. Systematic review and meta-analysis of 6 RCTs, N=206, low-fat (~19% energy) vs. higher-fat (~39% energy) diets.

## 2. CARBOHYDRATES & FIBER

### Claim: Dietary Fiber Drives Meaningful Ad Libitum Weight Loss

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — high]`

Increasing dietary fiber intake by 14 g/day is associated with body weight loss of 1.9 kg over approximately 3.8 months under ad libitum eating conditions. The effect is larger in overweight and obese individuals (2.4 kg) than in lean individuals (0.8 kg), indicating that higher adiposity amplifies the satiety benefit. A separate corroborating analysis (POUNDS Lost) found +3.7 g/day fiber associated with 1.4 kg greater weight loss at 6 months.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Primary: 1.9 kg body weight loss over 3.8 months from +14 g/day dietary fiber
- Overweight/obese subgroup: 2.4 kg vs. lean: 0.8 kg — adiposity amplifies the effect
- Corroborating (POUNDS Lost, PMC6768815): +3.7 g/day fiber → 1.4 kg greater loss at 6 months

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

Target 38–45 g dietary fiber per day (vs. a typical 15 g/day average) via legumes, oats, vegetables, and whole grains. The satiety-driven weight loss from fiber acts additively with the explicit caloric deficit — it is not a replacement for tracking calories, but a meaningful adherence amplifier. At moderate adiposity, the effect likely falls toward the 2.4 kg end of the range over 4 months.

**Sources:**
- PMID 11396693 — Howarth et al. 2001, Nutrition Reviews. Systematic review aggregating published fiber intervention studies.
- PMC6768815 — POUNDS Lost observational analysis corroborating the +3.7 g/day fiber association.

## 3. MICRONUTRIENTS

### Claim: Vitamin D Deficiency Suppresses Testosterone; Supplementation Restores It

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — high]`

Approximately 73% of adults in northern European countries at ~59°N latitude have serum 25(OH)D below 50 nmol/L (20 ng/mL) in winter — the standard threshold for insufficiency. Vitamin D supplementation at 3,332 IU/day for 12 months in deficient males increased total testosterone by approximately 2.7 nmol/L (~78 ng/dL), a 25% rise from a baseline of 10.7 to 13.4 nmol/L.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Pilz et al. 2011 (PMID 21154195): +2.7 nmol/L total testosterone (~78 ng/dL, **+25%**) after 3,332 IU/day vitamin D3 for 12 months in vitamin D-deficient overweight men. Crosses Tier 1 threshold (>15% change).
- Prevalence: 73% winter 25(OH)D insufficiency at 59°N (Estonia population study, PMC2632995).

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

Get serum 25(OH)D tested. If below 50 nmol/L — highly probable in October through March in northern Europe — supplement at 2,000–4,000 IU/day vitamin D3 with food. The testosterone effect is demonstrated only in deficient individuals and should not be extrapolated to replete individuals. At 70 kg, 3,000 IU/day is a safe, evidence-anchored starting dose; retest at 3 months. Combined with the dietary fat finding, this addresses two independent testosterone suppressors simultaneously during a cut.

**Sources:**
- PMID 21154195 — Pilz S et al. 2011, Horm Metab Res 43(3):223–225. RCT, 54 overweight men, 3,332 IU/day vitamin D3 vs. placebo for 12 months.
- PMC2632995 — Antson et al., Estonia winter population study measuring seasonal 25(OH)D in general adults at 59°N latitude.

## 4. MEAL TIMING & FREQUENCY

### Claim: Meal Frequency Has No Effect on Body Composition When Calories and Protein Are Equated

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[CRAFT CONSENSUS]`

Meal frequency (5–6 vs. 2–3 meals/day) has no meaningful effect on fat mass or lean mass when total calories and protein are equated. A meta-analysis of 15 RCTs initially showed a positive signal for higher meal frequency, but sensitivity analysis revealed it was attributable to a single outlier study. After removal of that study, the signal disappears. The conclusion is that meal frequency is irrelevant to body composition outcomes when energy and protein intake are controlled.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Schoenfeld et al. 2015 (PMID 26024494): SMD for fat mass and lean mass not distinguishable from zero after sensitivity analysis removing the single influential study. Net confirmed effect: null. The pooled positive association was driven by one outlier among 15 RCTs.

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

Do not add meal frequency as a variable to manage. Eat in whatever pattern supports adherence and protein distribution across the day. Hunger management is better addressed through fiber and protein density than through timing. This finding eliminates a common compliance friction point — fewer, larger meals that are socially compatible are as effective as frequent small meals.

**Source:**
- PMID 26024494 — Schoenfeld BJ, Aragon AA, Krieger JW. "Effects of meal frequency on weight loss and body composition: a meta-analysis." Nutrition Reviews 73(2):69–82, 2015. Meta-analysis of 15 RCTs; sensitivity analysis removed the single driving study.

## 5. GUT HEALTH

### Claim: Fermented Foods Increase Microbiome Diversity; High Fiber Alone Does Not

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — high]`

A 10-week high-fermented-food diet (~6 servings/day of yogurt, kefir, and fermented vegetables) significantly increased gut microbiome alpha diversity (Shannon index) in healthy adults. A concurrent high-fiber diet (>45 g/day) produced no significant alpha diversity change despite increasing microbial carbohydrate-active enzyme (CAZyme) capacity. The fermented food group also showed decreased levels of 19 inflammatory proteins including IL-6 and IL-12b.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Fermented food group (Wastyk et al. 2021, PMID 34256014): Statistically significant increase in Shannon diversity over 10 weeks (p<0.05, within-person trajectory). Decreased 19 inflammatory proteins including IL-6 and IL-12b.
- High-fiber group: No significant alpha diversity change; increased CAZyme capacity only.
- These are non-overlapping mechanisms — the two interventions are complementary, not interchangeable.

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

Include fermented foods daily — at minimum 1–2 servings of plain yogurt or kefir. The benefit lies in microbial diversity and anti-inflammatory signaling, not simply probiotic colony counts. High fiber alone does not replicate this. Treat fermented foods as a baseline and fiber as the amplifier: fiber feeds existing microbiota, fermented foods diversify and expand them. Both are needed for the full effect.

**Source:**
- PMID 34256014 — Wastyk HC et al. 2021, Cell. RCT, n=36 (18 per arm), Stanford University. 17-week study (3-week baseline + 10-week intervention + 4-week washout). Microbiome profiled by 16S rRNA sequencing (Shannon diversity index).

## 6. MAJOR REVERSALS & BIAS

### Claim: Multivitamins Provide No Meaningful Prevention of CVD or Cancer

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[CRAFT CONSENSUS]`

The 2022 USPSTF systematic review (PMID 35727271) found that multivitamin supplementation has little to no benefit for preventing cardiovascular disease or cancer mortality in healthy adults. For cancer incidence, a pooled analysis of 4 RCTs (n=48,859) found OR=0.93 (95% CI 0.87–0.99) — a 7% relative reduction, driven primarily by the Physicians' Health Study II (HR=0.92, 95% CI 0.86–1.00, barely significant at 11.2 years). No significant reduction in CVD mortality was found. The USPSTF issued an 'I' grade (insufficient evidence), meaning they cannot recommend for or against multivitamins for general primary prevention. This explicitly contradicts the widespread consumer belief that multivitamins meaningfully reduce cancer or heart disease risk.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Cancer incidence: OR=0.93 (95% CI 0.87–0.99) across 4 RCTs (n=48,859) — 7% relative reduction, absolute risk reduction negligible
- Physicians' Health Study II: HR=0.92 (95% CI 0.86–1.00), 11.2 years follow-up, n=14,641 male physicians
- CVD mortality: No significant benefit confirmed
- USPSTF grade: I (insufficient evidence for or against)

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

A standard multivitamin is not a meaningful defense against cardiovascular disease or cancer for a healthy male. Do not spend budget or cognitive overhead on a daily multi expecting disease prevention. Redirect toward targeted testing (vitamin D, full blood panel) and confirmed-deficiency supplementation only. The 'I' grade is not a gap in evidence — absence of effect is the finding.

**Sources:**
- PMID 35727271 — USPSTF 2022, JAMA. Systematic review and recommendation statement on vitamin, mineral, and multivitamin supplementation for primary prevention of CVD and cancer.
- Physicians' Health Study II: 11.2 years follow-up, n=14,641 male physicians.

## 7. CAFFEINE

### Ergogenic Effects

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — high]`

Caffeine supplementation significantly improves time-to-exhaustion (an endurance and high-intensity effort measure) with a medium pooled SMD of 0.39 (95% CI: 0.21–0.57, p<0.001), corresponding to approximately 17% longer time to exhaustion across aerobic and high-intensity protocols. Time-trial performance improvement is smaller: SMD ~0.10, representing approximately 0.7% reduction in completion time. An umbrella review of 21 published meta-analyses (Grgic et al. 2020, BJSM) confirms caffeine's broad ergogenic benefit extends across aerobic exercise modalities.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Time-to-exhaustion: SMD = 0.39 (95% CI: 0.21–0.57, p<0.001), approximately 17% improvement — Tier 2 effect
- Time-trial: SMD ~0.10, approximately 0.7% reduction in completion time — Tier 3/4 effect
- Both effects are statistically significant and replicated across multiple independent meta-analyses

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

At 3–6 mg/kg body weight, that is 210–420 mg caffeine before training — achievable with 2–3 cups of strong coffee. The ergogenic benefit is clearest for prolonged efforts or time-to-exhaustion tests rather than short time-trials. In a deficit, where fatigue thresholds drop and training quality is at risk, this is a low-cost intervention with a confirmed medium effect size. Do not exceed 400–600 mg/day total; time intake to avoid sleep disruption (see Dependency and Tolerance section).

**Sources:**
- PMC9824573 — Nutrients 2023. Systematic review and meta-analysis of 21 RCTs (n=254, predominantly male) examining caffeine and endurance running performance including time-to-exhaustion and time-trial outcomes.
- PMID 30926628 — Grgic J et al. 2020, British Journal of Sports Medicine. Umbrella review of 21 meta-analyses on caffeine's ergogenic effects across exercise modalities.

---

### Dependency and Tolerance

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — medium]`

Regular caffeine consumption leads to tolerance — daily users show attenuated ergogenic responses relative to naive users. The mechanism is adenosine receptor upregulation with chronic exposure. Dependency manifests as withdrawal symptoms (headache, fatigue, reduced concentration) upon cessation, typically emerging 12–24 hours after last dose and peaking at 20–51 hours. Whether habitual caffeine users still achieve an absolute ergogenic benefit above their own baseline remains genuinely contested in the literature: one 2025 meta-analysis found residual benefit even in habitual users, while other analyses show the benefit is largely or fully eliminated relative to the non-caffeinated state in regular consumers.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Tolerance: Ergogenic SMD is attenuated in habitual users; the magnitude of attenuation is contested but the direction is consistent across studies
- Withdrawal onset: 12–24 hours post last dose; peak: 20–51 hours
- Residual benefit in habitual users: uncertain — the claim of full tolerance eliminating ergogenic effects is not supported, but the effect is reduced vs. naive users

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

Two strategies are empirically defensible: (1) cycle caffeine — abstain for 7–10 days before a major training block or competition to restore full sensitivity; (2) accept partial tolerance and use caffeine consistently as a mild but durable ergogenic. The worst outcome is unplanned cessation mid-deficit: withdrawal fatigue compounds the deficit's own energy suppression. If cycling, plan the washout period; if using daily, treat the 17% time-to-exhaustion benefit as a floor estimate, not the ceiling. Caffeine taken within 6 hours of sleep onset measurably reduces sleep quality, which in a deficit is doubly costly — dose timing before afternoon training is preferable.

**Sources:**
- PMID 30926628 — Grgic et al. 2020, BJSM umbrella review. Addresses tolerance and habituation across 21 meta-analyses.
- PMC9824573 — Nutrients 2023. Primary ergogenic meta-analysis.

## 8. MAGNESIUM

### Claim 1: Elite Athletes Show High Prevalence of Clinical Magnesium Deficiency

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — high]`

22% of elite Olympic and Paralympic track and field athletes showed clinical magnesium deficiency on at least one blood test over an 8-year monitoring period, defined as red cell magnesium below 1.19 mmol/L. The study tracked 192 athletes across 510 blood samples from 2010–2018. This establishes that magnesium deficiency is common even in monitored, high-performance athlete populations — it is not a marginal concern.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- 22% prevalence of red cell magnesium deficiency (< 1.19 mmol/L) across 510 blood samples from 192 elite athletes
- 8-year longitudinal monitoring (2010–2018), Olympic and Paralympic level track and field

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

A caloric deficit restricts total food volume, directly reducing magnesium intake. At 70 kg male training load, sweat losses further compound dietary shortfall. The 22% deficiency rate in elite monitored athletes is a floor estimate — amateur athletes without dietary tracking or blood monitoring face higher risk. Check dietary magnesium against the 420 mg/day RDA for adult males. Common deficiency sources are inadequate legumes, dark leafy greens, and whole grains. Consider 200–400 mg/day supplemental magnesium glycinate or citrate if dietary intake is below target; test red cell magnesium rather than serum (serum is insensitive to tissue deficiency).

**Source:**
- PMID 31829845 — Pollock N et al. 2020, Journal of the American College of Nutrition. 8-year longitudinal analysis, N=192 Olympic and Paralympic athletes, 510 blood samples.

### Claim 2: Magnesium Supplementation Improves Sleep in Primary Insomnia

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — medium]`

Magnesium supplementation at 500 mg/day for 8 weeks significantly improved sleep in elderly subjects with primary insomnia vs. placebo: sleep time increased (P=0.002), sleep onset latency decreased (P=0.02), sleep efficiency improved (P=0.03), and insomnia severity (ISI) decreased (P=0.006). The study used elemental magnesium and was double-blind placebo-controlled.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- ISI score reduction: P=0.006 vs. placebo (statistically significant)
- Sleep onset latency: P=0.02 reduction vs. placebo
- Sleep time: P=0.002 increase vs. placebo
- Sleep efficiency: P=0.03 improvement vs. placebo
- Note: All four sleep metrics moved in the same direction; the effect is consistent across measures. Confidence is `medium` rather than `high` because the population is elderly with primary insomnia — generalizability to younger athletes with subclinical deficiency is an extrapolation, not a direct finding.

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

Sleep is compromised in caloric deficits independently. If sleep quality is poor, magnesium (200–400 mg elemental, taken 30–60 minutes before bed) is a low-risk, low-cost intervention with evidence of benefit in the population where it was studied. Do not treat this as a certainty for younger non-insomniac users — but given the high prevalence of deficiency in athletes and the plausible mechanism (magnesium regulates GABA and melatonin pathways), it is a reasonable trial. Use glycinate or citrate forms for absorption; oxide has low bioavailability.

**Source:**
- PMID 23853635 — Abbasi B et al. 2012, Journal of Research in Medical Sciences. Double-blind placebo-controlled RCT, N=46 elderly subjects (23 per arm), 500 mg/day elemental magnesium vs. placebo for 8 weeks.

## 11. LOW-CARB VS LOW-FAT

### Claim 1: Macronutrient Composition Does Not Determine Long-Term Weight Loss (POUNDS LOST Trial)

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — high]`

Sacks et al. 2009 (POUNDS LOST trial, n=811, 2-year RCT): At 6 months all four diet groups (ranging 20–40% fat, 35–65% carbs) lost an average of ~6 kg (~7% of initial weight) with no statistically significant difference between any arm. At 2 years, the lowest-carb group (35% carbs) lost 3.4 kg vs. the highest-carb group (65% carbs) at 2.9 kg — a 0.5 kg difference that was non-significant (p>0.20). Low-fat (20% fat) vs. high-fat (40% fat) at 2 years: 3.3 kg vs. 3.3 kg — identical, non-significant (p>0.20). The single strongest predictor of weight loss was adherence to counseling sessions: 0.2 kg per session attended, regardless of diet arm.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Lowest-carb vs. highest-carb at 2 years: 0.5 kg difference (p>0.20, non-significant)
- Low-fat vs. high-fat at 2 years: 0.0 kg difference (p>0.20, non-significant)
- Behavioral predictor: 0.2 kg per counseling session attended — adherence dominates macronutrient ratio

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

The macronutrient ratio (within normal ranges of 20–40% fat, 35–65% carbs) does not determine weight loss outcomes at 2 years when calories are equally restricted. Choose the macro distribution that maximizes your personal adherence — not the one with the best theoretical mechanism. The data are unambiguous: adherence behavior explains variance in weight loss; macros within these ranges do not.

**Source:**
- PMID 19246357 — Sacks FM et al. 2009, NEJM 360:859–873. 2-year RCT, n=811 overweight adults, four arms varying fat (20–40%), protein (15–25%), and carbohydrate (35–65%). All arms equally calorie-restricted.

### Claim 2: Low-Fat Produces Greater Daily Body Fat Loss Than Low-Carb in Isocaloric Conditions (Hall et al. 2015)

**Vote: 3-0 VERIFIED** | `[EMPIRICAL — high]`

Hall et al. 2015 (metabolic ward, n=19, crossover RCT): Under strictly controlled isocaloric feeding with protein clamped, a low-fat (fat restriction) diet produced 89 ± 6 g/day of body fat loss vs. 53 ± 6 g/day on a low-carbohydrate diet — a statistically significant 36 g/day advantage for low-fat (p=0.002). Notably, the low-carb diet substantially raised fat oxidation and reduced insulin secretion (24-hr urinary C-peptide fell significantly on low-carb only), yet these favorable metabolic signals did not translate into greater fat loss. Low-fat outperformed low-carb on fat loss despite no greater fat oxidation or insulin reduction.

**Effect size (explicitly called out):**

- Low-fat: 89 ± 6 g/day body fat loss vs. low-carb: 53 ± 6 g/day — difference of 36 g/day favoring low-fat (p=0.002)
- This is a direct, precisely measured metabolic ward finding, not an observational association
- Critical caveat: This is a short (6-day) acute phase; the POUNDS LOST 2-year trial shows the difference converges to zero at free-living timescales

**Practical implication for 70 kg male in deficit:**

In a tightly controlled metabolic context, low-fat has a measurable short-term advantage in body fat loss rate. However, the POUNDS LOST 2-year RCT shows this acute difference does not survive free-living conditions — adherence and total calorie control dominate. The practical synthesis: in a metabolic ward you'd eat low-fat; in real life, eat whichever diet you will actually maintain. The insulin-carbohydrate model of obesity (the idea that fat loss requires low carbs due to insulin suppression) is directly refuted by this study: fat oxidation rose on low-carb, insulin fell, yet fat loss was lower, not higher.

**Source:**
- PMID 26278052 — Hall KD et al. 2015, Cell Metabolism 22(3):427–436. Crossover metabolic ward RCT, n=19 adults with obesity, 6-day isocaloric low-carb vs. low-fat phases after 5-day baseline, protein held constant.

## 14. EFFECT SIZE PRIORITY TIERS

All confirmed findings grouped by effect size. Null results included as Tier 4 behavior-simplifiers.

**Thresholds:**
- Tier 1 (Large — act on immediately): Cohen d >0.5 or >15% change or RR <0.75
- Tier 2 (Moderate — worth implementing): d 0.3–0.5 or 5–15% change
- Tier 3 (Small — marginal benefit): d 0.1–0.3
- Tier 4 (Negligible — not worth changing behavior for): d <0.1

## What This Means For The Diet

### Dietary Fats & Hormones

During the deficit, keep fat above 25% of total calories (roughly 61 g/day at 2,200 kcal) — cutting fat aggressively to accelerate the deficit risks a 15–25% drop in testosterone that undermines lean mass retention. If vitamin D is insufficient (highly probable in northern Europe through winter), supplementing at 3,000 IU/day is the highest-effect-size intervention in this dataset — it directly addresses a confirmed hormonal suppressor that compounds the deficit's own androgen-lowering pressure.

### Fiber, Gut Health & Satiety

Push dietary fiber to 38–45 g/day via legumes, oats, and vegetables — this provides a modest but real satiety advantage over a baseline Western diet, and in overweight individuals the weight loss signal is stronger. Layer in daily fermented foods (1–2 servings of yogurt or kefir minimum) as a non-overlapping mechanism: fiber feeds existing microbiota, fermented foods diversify them and reduce inflammatory signaling — neither substitutes for the other.

### Caffeine

Caffeine at 3–6 mg/kg (210–420 mg at 70 kg) before training is a confirmed Tier 2 ergogenic — approximately 17% improvement in time-to-exhaustion, with a smaller but real time-trial benefit. In a deficit, where training fatigue is elevated and willingness to push diminishes, this is the cheapest performance-preserving intervention in this document. Be aware of tolerance buildup with daily use; if performance is the priority, cycle caffeine with 7–10 day washout periods before blocks that matter. Do not dose within 6 hours of sleep — the deficit is already costly to sleep quality.

### Magnesium

22% of elite athletes show clinical deficiency despite monitoring — in a deficit, inadequate food volume makes this worse. Check dietary magnesium against 420 mg/day RDA; supplement 200–400 mg/day (glycinate or citrate) if diet is short. The sleep benefit in insomnia populations is real at 500 mg/day, though generalizability to younger athletes requires caution. Use red cell magnesium for testing, not serum.

### Macronutrient Ratio (Low-Carb vs. Low-Fat)

Two high-quality studies reach compatible conclusions: the insulin-carbohydrate model of fat loss is refuted by Hall 2015 (low-fat won in a metabolic ward despite low-carb raising fat oxidation and lowering insulin), and macronutrient ratio does not predict 2-year weight loss in free-living conditions (POUNDS LOST). Choose your macro split for adherence and satiety — the data show adherence behavior, not macro ratio, determines long-term outcome.

### What Not To Prioritize

Meal frequency is a confirmed null — do not engineer your schedule around eating 5–6 times per day unless adherence specifically requires it. Standard multivitamins confer no meaningful disease prevention benefit for healthy adults — redirect that budget to targeted testing (vitamin D, full blood panel) and confirmed-deficiency supplementation only.

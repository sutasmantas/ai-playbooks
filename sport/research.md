# Workout Research — Round 2 Findings
*8 parallel research agents. Generated 2026-05-25.*

---

## Session Structure

**Compounds before isolation:** Correct, keep it. Research shows no hypertrophy advantage to pre-exhaustion; placing isolations first reduces compound performance without payoff. *Moderate evidence.*

**Antagonist supersets:** Strong evidence — same hypertrophy and strength gains, ~36% less time per session (2025 meta-analysis, PMC12011898). Recommended pairs for the current plan:
- Pull-ups + Floor Press
- Rows + OHP
- Curls + Tricep Overhead Extension
- Leg Raises + Calf Raises (non-competing, lower days)
Optional — implement if sessions feel too long. Not mandatory.

**Tempo / TUT:** Weekly volume matters more than specific tempo. 3-1-3 on push-ups controls ROM and momentum — that's its value. Don't sacrifice reps or sets to hit a tempo number. *Strong evidence.*

**Exercise order within sessions:**
- Lower A: Nordic Curl can go before RDL if hamstring fatigue from RDL compromises eccentric quality
- All sessions: isolations stay at the end. One exception: 1 warm-up lateral raise set before OHP (very light, improves delt activation) *Moderate evidence.*

## Recomposition Specifics

- Keep intensity high — 70–85% 1RM on strength days. Dropping load too far kills the hypertrophic signal during a recomp.
- Do not drop below 10–15 sets/muscle/week. Volume is non-negotiable for simultaneous fat loss + muscle retention.
- DUP Upper/Lower is well-suited for recomposition — no structural change needed. *Moderate-strong evidence.*
- Caloric cycling (more on training days, less on rest): negligible measurable benefit when total weekly intake is matched. Skip if it adds complexity.
- 4 sessions/week is the research-supported sweet spot for intermediate lifters. More sessions show diminishing returns. *Strong evidence.*
- Reassess the recomp approach if strength progress stalls for 6–8+ consecutive weeks. At ~1 year training with likely higher body fat, still well within the recomp window.

## Cardio

Current schedule (cardio on Wed/Sat/Sun, lifting Mon/Tue/Thu/Fri) is already optimal — separate days eliminate the mTOR/AMPK interference effect almost entirely. *Strong evidence for separate-day superiority.*

| Day | Prescription | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Wednesday | 30–40 min Zone 2 (60–70% max HR) — walk or cycle | Active recovery; low impact, preserves legs for Thu/Fri |
| Saturday | 20–25 min harder session (HIIT or moderate run) | Two rest days follow — most recovery time available |
| Sunday | 30–40 min easy walk or light cycle | Flush fatigue before Monday lifting |

- Prefer cycling over running on days adjacent to lower body sessions (Tue/Fri) — less eccentric leg stress.
- Do not exceed 90–120 min/week total if hypertrophy is the priority. Up to 150–180 min is tolerable if fat loss is the priority and recovery holds.
- Fasted cardio: no 24-hour fat-loss advantage over fed cardio when total calories are controlled. Skip or use based on schedule preference only. *Strong evidence.*

## Mesocycle Planning

- **Program review:** Every 4–6 weeks. Adaptation occurs within 4–6 weeks for intermediates. Running the same rep ranges and exercises beyond 3 consecutive mesocycles (12–18 weeks) leads to measurable staleness.
- **Deload frequency:** Every 4–5 weeks for 7 training sessions/week (4 lifting + 3 cardio). Average from competitive athlete data is 5.6 weeks; lean shorter given the total weekly load.
- **Between mesocycles:** Keep the DUP Upper/Lower structure. Rotate 1–2 primary exercises per session — not the rep range structure, just the exercises hitting that muscle group.
- **DUP longevity:** Appropriate for the next 6–12 months minimum. Switch to block periodization (full mesocycles of a single quality) when training age reaches 2–3 years.
- **Stalling diagnostic:** Before blaming the program, audit in this order: (1) Are sets within 0–4 RIR? (2) Is protein/sleep consistent? (3) Is life stress elevated? If all three are solid and a 6–8 week trend is flat or declining, then change the program.

## New Exercise Technique

### Lateral Raises
- Slight internal rotation at the top: pinky slightly higher than thumb — isolates lateral head better than thumbs-up grip
- Lead with elbows, not hands. If hands arrive at the top first, traps are doing the work
- Stop at shoulder height — going above shifts load to traps, not delt
- Maintain a fixed 10–15° elbow bend throughout

### Face Pulls
- Anchor at face height. Pull toward nose/forehead — pulling lower turns it into a row
- Elbows must stay at or above shoulder height throughout the entire pull — the most common mistake
- Initiate with scapular retraction first, then follow with elbows. Leading with arms = biceps take over
- Finish in "double bicep" position — hands flared out and back, fully externally rotated

### Nordic Curl
- Never break at the hips — this is the most common error and removes hamstring load entirely
- If unable to do 1 full rep: lower slowly (5–7s) and catch with hands. Controlled lowering is where the injury-prevention benefit lives
- Band-assisted variant: loop band around chest/shoulders anchored above; band helps most at the bottom
- Progression: isometric holds → slow eccentrics with hand catch → band-assisted full reps → unassisted

### Lying Dumbbell Leg Curl
- Keep hips and thighs pressed flat throughout. Hips rising = too heavy, hip flexors compensating
- Place dumbbell vertically between feet (arches, not toes); dorsiflex at the top for stability
- Small pad or slight incline under hips increases hamstring stretch at the bottom — use it
- 1-second pause at peak contraction before lowering

### Single-Leg Calf Raise
- 1–2 second pause at the bottom stretch is non-negotiable — bouncing through bottom eliminates the stimulus
- Slow 3-second eccentric — the eccentric is where most calf adaptation happens
- Straight knee = gastrocnemius (the muscle responsible for visible calf size); 15–20° knee bend = soleus. Train both
- Only forefoot on the step — heel and arch hang free

## Supplementation

| Supplement | Verdict | Details |
|---|---|---|
| Creatine monohydrate | Take it | 3–5 g/day. No loading phase needed. No timing requirement — just be consistent. 1–2 kg intramuscular water weight gain in week 1 (not fat). Monohydrate = cheapest, best-studied form. |
| Caffeine | Take it (if desired) | ~200 mg (3 mg/kg), 30–60 min pre-workout. Improves force output and power. Avoid after 2 PM to protect sleep. |
| Protein powder | Situational | Not needed if hitting 1.6–2 g/kg through whole food. Use only when a day's intake falls short. |
| Vitamin D | Get a blood test | Supplement at 1000–2000 IU/day if deficient. No benefit if levels are already adequate. |
| Magnesium glycinate | Situational | 200–400 mg at night if sleep quality degrades. Current diet covers much of baseline needs. |
| Fish oil | Low priority | Some evidence for recovery; small effect size. Not urgent given other priorities. |
| HMB, CLA, BCAAs, beta-alanine | Skip | Weak or no evidence for this use case. Industry-funded research bias. |

## Recovery

**Protein timing:** Eat protein-containing meals within 2 hours before and after training. The "30-minute window" myth is debunked — the practical window is 4–6 hours surrounding training. Total daily protein matters far more than timing. *Strong evidence.*

**Active recovery on rest days:** 15–20 min low-intensity walking is well-supported for lactate clearance and DOMS relief — comparable to cold water immersion, foam rolling, and massage in head-to-head comparisons. Cold showers and stretching have weak evidence for recovery (useful for other reasons). *Moderate-strong evidence.*

**Training through DOMS:** Fine for mild-moderate soreness. Reduce load 10–20% if soreness is severe or alters form. DOMS persisting beyond 3–5 days is a recovery signal. *Moderate evidence.*

**Early overtraining signals (ranked by reliability):**
1. Resting HR elevated 5–7+ bpm above personal baseline for 2+ consecutive days — track this daily on waking (30 seconds, free)
2. HRV trending down over 5–7 days (use HRV4Training app)
3. Performance regression on well-practiced lifts across 2+ sessions without a clear cause
4. Sleep quality degrading despite adequate hours
5. Increased minor illness / nagging joint discomfort

Two of the above simultaneously = implement a deload immediately, don't wait.

**Deload protocol (for this plan specifically):**
- Cut volume 40–50% (sets, not load — keep weights the same)
- Keep all 4 lifting days and cardio sessions
- Stop 4–5 reps short of failure on every set
- Duration: 5–7 days
- Frequency: every 4–5 weeks proactively, or reactively when 2+ overtraining signals appear

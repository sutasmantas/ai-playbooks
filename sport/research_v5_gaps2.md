## CONFIRMED FINDINGS

### Polyphenols / DOMS Reduction

**[EMPIRICAL — medium confidence]**

**Effect size: DOMS reduction Cohen's d = -0.44 (small-moderate); MVC recovery Cohen's d = -0.78 (moderate). Both shrink after excluding high-bias studies in sensitivity analyses.**

**Best evidence:** Gao et al. 2021, *Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab* (meta-analysis, n=8 RCTs); Sports Medicine Open 2026 meta-analysis (19 trials in trained athletes, evidence certainty rated moderate-to-very-low).

**WHAT CHANGES IN DIET:**
Add 150–200 g frozen blueberries daily on training days. Mix into oats or Greek yogurt. This delivers approximately 180–280 mg anthocyanins, matching the load used in positive trials. Cost at Maxima/Rimi/Lidl Lithuania: ~0.50–1.00 EUR/day frozen.

Do NOT substitute high-dose isolated Vitamin C or E — those blunt training adaptation. Food-matrix polyphenols carry no documented adaptation-blunting risk.

Effect is most relevant on back-to-back training days (Mon–Tue, Thu–Fri) where 24-hour inter-session recovery is the binding constraint. On isolated training days with full rest the following day, the benefit is marginal.

Tart cherry concentrate is not required — expensive, not widely stocked in LT, and blueberries at this dose approximate the active anthocyanin content.

## NULL RESULTS / REFUTED CLAIMS

| Claim | Refute votes | Reason | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Leucine threshold** — restructure per-meal protein targets around leucine density (~2.5–3 g/meal) rather than total protein grams | 3/3 | (1) Threshold only holds in isolated-protein studies and older adults with anabolic resistance; collapses in whole-food mixed-meal contexts (Monteyne 2024, AJCN; Zaromskyte 2021 16:15 support/refute split). (2) At adequate whole-food protein portions (~30–40 g/meal), the 2.5 g leucine threshold is cleared incidentally — delta between sources at equivalent portions is ~0.3–0.7 g, within noise. (3) Acute MPS (the surrogate) does not correlate with 16-week hypertrophy (Mitchell 2014, r = 0.02–0.16). (4) In a deficit, MPS is already attenuated by AMPK/mTORC1 competition; leucine optimization does not recover the suppressed response. | REFUTED for whole-food meal planning in trained males. Total daily protein distribution remains the operative variable. |
| **Pre-sleep casein** — 30–40 g casein before sleep improves body composition in a caloric deficit | 3/3 | (1) Snijders 2015 (only chronic body composition RCT) did not equate total daily protein — treatment group consumed ~27.5 g/day more protein total; timing cannot be isolated. (2) Every study equating total daily protein finds no timing-specific advantage (Antonio 2017; Chapman 2023 British Army RCT, n=122). (3) Effective dose is narrower than claimed: Trommelen 2016 showed 30 g did NOT significantly raise overnight MPS vs. placebo; only 40 g did. (4) 2025 RCT (JISSN, protein-equated, 12-week RET) found no timing effect on muscle thickness or strength. | REFUTED for timing-specific benefit. Pre-sleep protein is equivalent to protein at any other meal when daily totals are equated. |
| **Vitamin K2 (MK-7)** — 100–200 µg/day improves BMD 3–6%, reduces arterial calcification, and synergizes with D3 | 3/3 | (1) CV protection: AVADEC trial (n=389, 2yr, 720 µg/day K2+D3) found no significant CAC reduction; Rees 2020 SR (21 RCTs) found no significant CV event benefit. (2) BMD: Knapen 2013 (anchor positive trial) has a direct 3-year replication failure — Rønn/Harsløf 2021 (MK-7 375 µg + D3 + calcium vs. matched control) found BMD declined at all sites with no between-group difference despite confirmed osteocalcin carboxylation improvement (65%). Biomarker fires; outcome does not follow. (3) All positive BMD RCTs were in postmenopausal women with estrogen-deficient bone loss — no RCT demonstrates a BMD signal in trained men. (4) D3 synergy sub-claim is mechanistic extrapolation only; no factorial RCT isolates the interaction. (5) NattoPharma (maker of MenaQ7) funded or supplied product for the majority of positive MK-7 trials. | REFUTED (CV arm); CONTESTED-WEAK (BMD arm, but irrelevant for trained males). Do not add to stack. |
| **Caloric periodization** — +200 kcal training days / −200 kcal rest days better supports muscle retention in a deficit vs. flat distribution | 3/3 | (1) MPS is driven by protein availability, not caloric surplus; resistance exercise restores MPS to eucaloric levels in a deficit regardless of same-day caloric surplus (Churchward-Venne, J Physiol). (2) Only supporting RCT (Falkenhain 2020) used 2-day full carbohydrate refeeds, not modest daily cycling — protocol does not match the claim. Effect sizes were trivial (d=0.03–0.33). FFM gain plausibly reflects glycogen + water retention at measurement, not accrued myofibrillar tissue. (3) 2024 network meta-analysis of 47 RCTs (PMC11425986) found no superiority of intermittent vs. continuous restriction on lean mass when weekly totals are matched. (4) No RCT has tested the specific +/−200 kcal daily cycling protocol in resistance-trained individuals with protein equated. | REFUTED. Flat caloric distribution with consistent daily protein is the evidence-supported approach. |
| **Pre-workout carb loading by session type** — differentiate CHO intake: more pre-HIIT, moderate pre-hypertrophy, less pre-maximal-strength | 3/3 | (1) Operative variable is session duration/volume and glycogen status, not session type label (King et al. 2022 — effect only appears after ≥8 h fast or sessions >45 min, >8–10 sets). (2) 2025 crossover RCT (PMC11874534, n=16, 80% 1RM, high-CHO vs. low-CHO vs. placebo) found no significant difference in resistance-trained subjects in a fed state. (3) Hamidvand 2025: higher-intensity resistance training depletes less glycogen than high-volume lower-intensity work — partially inverts the assumed HIIT > strength > hypertrophy hierarchy. (4) In a caloric deficit, glycogen is chronically suppressed; total daily carbohydrate, not per-session targeting, is the binding constraint. | REFUTED for session-type differentiation. Prioritize total daily CHO adequacy; increase carbs around long/high-volume sessions (>45 min, >8–10 sets) regardless of session type label if budget allows. |
| **Hydration / electrolytes** — >2% BM fluid loss impairs HIIT performance; pre/post-session electrolyte replacement accelerates inter-session recovery | 2/3 (one dissent) | (1) Judelson 2007 effect sizes anchor the claim but pool unblinded studies. Blinded IV rehydration studies show null or near-null effects at equivalent dehydration levels — expectancy/nocebo confound is substantial. (2) HIIT-specific evidence is the weakest: only 4 of 10 intermittent exercise trials showed significant benefit from fluid intake. (3) Electrolyte recovery sub-claim (sodium/potassium specifically accelerating next-session HIIT performance) has no direct RCT support — evidence is surrogate biomarker only (plasma volume, urine osmolality), not directly measured subsequent-session performance. Dissenting vote noted: basic fluid restoration (not electrolyte-specific) does have mechanistic support at >2% BM loss. | WEAK/PARTIALLY REFUTED. Basic rehydration to <2% BM loss is reasonable practice. Electrolyte-specific and next-session recovery claims are not directly evidenced. No change to existing hydration approach; no dedicated electrolyte supplement warranted. |
| **Circadian eating / early TRE** — consuming meals in an 8am–4pm window improves insulin sensitivity and body composition independent of calorie restriction | 3/3 | (1) Best-powered isocaloric direct comparison (ChronoFast, Peters and Schwarz, Science Translational Medicine 2025, n=31) found zero difference between early TRE (8am–4pm) and late TRE (1pm–9pm) for insulin sensitivity, fasting glucose, or any cardiometabolic marker, despite confirmed ~40 min circadian phase shifts. (2) Sutton 2018 (flagship positive study): n=8, prediabetic clinical population, 6-hour window — cannot generalize to metabolically healthy trained adults. (3) Liu et al. NEJM 2022 (n=139, 12 months) showed TRE adds nothing over matched caloric restriction for weight, body composition, or metabolic risk markers. (4) DUP training 5×/week improves insulin sensitivity independently, compressing any marginal circadian benefit. (5) An 8am–4pm window conflicts with post-workout evening nutrition, creating adherence and muscle-retention risk that outweighs a sub-0.3 SMD glucose signal. | REFUTED for independent calorie-restriction-free benefit. Caloric deficit already in place is the operative metabolic driver. No meal timing restructuring warranted. |

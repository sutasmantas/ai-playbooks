## 1. LEGS + HORMONES — DEFINITIVELY REFUTED

**The claim: "training legs releases testosterone/GH that boosts upper-body muscle growth" is false.**

Five independent lines of primary evidence all point the same direction:

| Study | N | Design | Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Morton et al. 2016 (PMC4967245) | 49 | 12-week RCT | Zero significant correlations between acute T/GH/IGF-1 rises and hypertrophy or strength |
| Morton et al. 2018 (PMC6189473) | 49 | 12-week RCT | All 48 regression models had R² < 0.25; androgen receptor content, not hormone levels, was the predictor |
| Mitchell et al. 2013 (PMC3793973) | 23 | 16-week RCT + biopsy | No relationship between post-exercise hormone AUC and muscle fiber CSA changes |
| PMC8439705 (2021 RCT) | 17 | Mixed-load RCT | Mixed-load lower-body protocol produced greater acute GH than heavy-load → identical upper-body strength gains |
| ACSM 2024 review (PMC11460760) | — | Expert review | Explicitly: acute hormone rises "neither sufficient nor necessary" for MPS; local mechanotransduction is the primary driver |

`[EMPIRICAL — high confidence]` 3-0 across core claims. Corroborated by unilateral training studies showing hypertrophy confined to the trained limb despite systemic hormone elevation.

**Null result confirmed:** The specific claim that higher hormone surges from leg training produce superior upper-body adaptations does NOT survive adversarial verification.

**Important scope note:** This refutes the *systemic spillover* mechanism only. It does not deny that hormones act locally via intramuscular androgen receptor pathways — it specifically kills the idea that training legs releases hormones that then build your arms.

**What this means for the plan:**
- Legs 2x/week is chosen correctly for leg development and structural balance — not for hormonal spillover to upper body
- The previous 6x/week plan with daily legs did not produce more upper-body muscle than 2x/week would have, through this mechanism
- No reason to add more leg days than recovery allows

## 2. PROTEIN TARGETS FOR THIS BODY (70 KG)

### The 1.6 g/kg floor was built on surplus-calorie populations — it does not apply here

`[EMPIRICAL — high confidence]` 3-0 verified.

> "Interventions aiming to cause weight loss (i.e. negative energy balance)" were explicitly excluded from the Morton 2018 meta-analysis (PMC8978023) and its predecessor PMC5867436.

This means the widely cited 1.6 g/kg floor (= 112 g/day at 70 kg) was derived entirely from studies where participants were eating at maintenance or surplus. Under a caloric deficit, the protein requirement to preserve lean mass is substantially higher.

### During deficit — confirmed targets

**From Helms et al. 2014 (PMID 24092765) — resistance-trained athletes in deficit:**
> 2.3–3.1 g/kg FFM/day recommended for lean mass preservation.

**From 2025 updated systematic meta-regression — energy-restricted resistance-trained individuals:**
> 1.9–3.2 g/kg BM/day (or 2.5–4.2 g/kg FFM/day) most effective for preserving lean mass under hypocaloric conditions.

`[EMPIRICAL — medium confidence]` — 2025 meta-regression is newer with fewer replications than the euenergetic literature.

### Applied to 70 kg / 169 cm male

Estimated body fat at BMI 24.5 with ~1 year training: ~15–20%
→ Estimated fat-free mass: ~56–60 kg

| Formula | Range | Grams/day |
|---|---|---|
| 2.3–3.1 g/kg FFM (Helms) | 56–60 kg FFM | 129–186 g |
| 1.9–3.2 g/kg BM (2025 meta) | 70 kg BM | 133–224 g |
| **Practical daily target** | | **140–175 g** |

**Current plan delivers:** ~110–160 g/day (Wednesday drops to 110–130 g; Monday and Friday hit ~155–160 g)

**Gap identified:** Wednesday (restaurant day, ~110–130 g) falls below the evidence-based floor for a deficit. The Sunday light day (~110–130 g) also falls short.

**Recommendation:** Floor of **140 g/day on every day, including Wednesday and Sunday.** The upper range (175 g) is achievable on higher-intake days and provides additional margin.

## 3. PER-SESSION VOLUME AND WHY 4×/WEEK FEELS 1.5× HARDER

### Confirmed

**Per-session and weekly set volume both show positive dose-response relationships with hypertrophy and strength, with diminishing returns — and strength plateaus considerably faster than hypertrophy per session.**

`[EMPIRICAL — high confidence]` 3-0 verified.

> "Both best-fit models suggest diminishing returns, with the diminishing returns for strength being considerably more pronounced."
— Pelland et al. 2025 / Remmert et al. 2025 (same 67-study corpus, Bayesian meta-regression)

What this means for the 1.5× harder feeling:

When you distribute the same weekly volume across **fewer sessions**, each session accumulates more intra-session fatigue. The mechanism is well-established even if the adversarial verifiers killed the specific threshold numbers (PUOS):
- Earlier sets in a session are performed fresh → high stimulus quality
- Later sets in the same session are performed in a fatigued state → lower force output per rep, higher perceived exertion for equivalent volume
- **Strength gains plateau faster per session** — so by set 8 of a given muscle, you may still be getting hypertrophy stimulus but the strength-building signal has already tapered sharply
- Moving from 6 sessions to 4 sessions means the same weekly volume now has more "late-session" sets that land in fatigued tissue

**This is real metabolic demand, not just perception.** The sessions are objectively harder because they contain more sets per muscle per session, placing a larger acute metabolic load than distributed sessions would.

### Refuted thresholds

The specific PUOS (point of undetectable outcome superiority) numbers — "strength plateaus at ~2 sets per session" and "hypertrophy at ~11 sets per session" — were both **refuted 0-3** as too precise for the underlying data. Do not use these as hard cut-offs.

**What CAN be said:** There is a real per-session efficiency curve. Sessions with high sets per muscle per session produce real intra-session fatigue that is not fully compensated by the hypertrophic stimulus of those later sets.

### Practical implication

If sessions feel too long and too hard, the evidence-supported response is to **distribute volume back across more sessions** (e.g., add a 5th session) rather than remove exercises outright — because the volume is still contributing to hypertrophy, just at diminishing returns per set in fatigue. But if scheduling prevents a 5th session, reducing per-session set count (especially accessories that add load without proportional stimulus) is the correct lever.

## 4. ACCESSORY EXERCISE INJURY PREVENTION

The adversarial verification did not produce confirmed specific claims for individual accessories — the literature on isolated injury prevention for each exercise was contested or too imprecise to survive 3-vote verification. However, one finding is robust enough to report:

### Nordic Curl — Strong Injury Prevention Evidence

**Umbrella review (PMC11311354): 10 systematic reviews, 17,260 subjects.**
Nordic hamstring exercise programs produced a **~51% reduction in hamstring strain injuries** in athlete populations.

`[EMPIRICAL — medium confidence for home gym context]` The evidence base is overwhelmingly from team sport athletes (football, soccer) where hamstring injury rates are higher. Injury rates for home gym intermediate lifters are not directly studied. However, Nordic curl is the only accessory in the current plan with injury-prevention evidence of this magnitude.

**Specific claim that survived:** Nordic curl produces measurably superior hamstring eccentric strength compared to conventional exercises. This is mechanistically the likely injury prevention pathway.

### Other Accessories — Evidence Not Strong Enough to Confirm

For face pulls (shoulder health), Y-raise (lower trap / impingement), serratus punch (shoulder stability), hip abduction/adduction (knee valgus), and calf raises (Achilles) — the research found relevant sources but no claim formulation survived adversarial verification at medium or high confidence. The case for these exercises rests on:

- **Anatomical reasoning** (well-established): pressing-heavy programs load anterior structures; face pulls and Y-raises counter this imbalance. This is consensus-level reasoning, not empirical RCT evidence for injury prevention specifically.
- **Biomechanical plausibility** (for serratus / lower traps / hip abductors): scapular dyskinesis and knee valgus are documented injury risk factors; targeted strengthening is a plausible countermeasure. But direct injury prevention RCTs are not available at the needed population specificity.

`[CRAFT CONSENSUS — low-medium confidence]` Prioritization given lack of hard evidence:

| Accessory | Keep? | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Nordic curl | **Definitely keep** | 51% hamstring injury reduction, umbrella review |
| Face pulls | **Definitely keep** | Pressing volume in plan is high (OHP, floor press, push-ups); scapular health under this load is plausible concern |
| Y-raise (lower traps) | **Keep** | Lower traps are chronically underdeveloped in pressing-heavy programs; shoulder impingement is the documented outcome |
| Serratus punch | **Can reduce** | Serratus activation from proper push-up form (scapular protraction at top) already in plan; standalone serratus punch adds marginal additional stimulus at high time cost |
| Hip abduction (band) | **Keep** | Knee valgus during Bulgarian split squats is a direct injury risk in the current exercises; band work is low time cost |
| Hip adduction | **Can defer** | Adductor injury risk for home gym intermediate doing split squats is lower than in athletes; low evidence, low time urgency |
| Calf raises | **Keep** | Already in plan for hypertrophy, not primarily injury prevention |

## 5. FULL REST DAYS

No specific claim about complete rest days vs active recovery survived adversarial verification. Sources were collected but the claims were either too population-specific (athlete data) or contested.

**Best-available-practice from source review (not adversarially confirmed):**

From the Frontiers in Physiology systematic review with meta-analysis (2018):
- Active recovery (light movement) consistently outperformed complete rest for lactate clearance and DOMS reduction in the short term
- No review established complete rest days as physiologically necessary for intermediates doing 4 lifting + 3 light-moderate cardio sessions/week

**Practical guidance (unverified, consensus-based):**
- 7 sessions/week at the intensity and volume in this plan is manageable for most intermediates — the cardio sessions (Zone 2 walk, 20-min HIIT, easy cycle) are not recovery-compromising at the prescribed volumes
- The deload protocol (every 4–5 weeks) already provides systemic recovery cycles
- Monitor overtraining signals; add a complete rest day if 2+ signals appear
- Complete rest days are likely preference-driven at this volume, not physiologically mandatory

## 6. DISTRIBUTED TRAINING / GREASE THE GROOVE

No specific claims about distributed intra-day training survived adversarial verification. The rest-interval meta-analysis (PMC11349676) found:

**Refuted:** "Longer inter-set rest (>60s) produces only modest hypertrophic advantages (SMD 0.13–0.17)" — 0-3 killed (oversimplified the data)

**Refuted:** "No clear additional hypertrophic benefit from resting more than 90 seconds between sets" — 0-3 killed (contradicted by larger-rest superiority data)

**What did survive from sources (not adversarially confirmed):**
- The cluster-set study (PMC12174233: 4-rep clusters with 20s intra-set rest vs 12 continuous reps) found equivalent hypertrophy (lean mass 171.8 g vs 201.1 g, p=0.272) — but this claim was 1-2 killed due to small sample and borderline p-values
- Longer rest periods (2–5 min) appear to preserve force output better than short rest, which is relevant if distributing sets throughout the day (hours of rest between sets)

**Best inference from available evidence:**

Grease the Groove for pull-ups (as already in the plan) is mechanistically sound: neural adaptation and skill acquisition benefit from frequent sub-maximal practice. The hypertrophic return from GtG is unconfirmed but unlikely to be negative if total weekly volume is maintained.

For hypertrophy specifically, concentrated sessions with full rest periods are better-studied than distributed mini-sessions. The case for GtG is strongest for exercises that are currently limited by skill/neural factors (pull-ups) rather than purely metabolic capacity (curls, calf raises).

`[Research gap — no confirmed claims for GtG hypertrophy specifically]`

## 7. CALORIC STRATEGY — TDEE ESTIMATE FOR THIS PERSON

No adversarially verified claim addressed TDEE estimation. Applying standard validated equations:

**Mifflin-St Jeor BMR (70 kg / 169 cm / male / assume age ~20-25):**
BMR = (10 × 70) + (6.25 × 169) − (5 × 22) + 5 ≈ **1,717 kcal/day**

**Activity multiplier for 4 lifting + 3 cardio sessions/week:**
Active (1.55) → TDEE ≈ **2,660 kcal/day**

This is an estimate with ±200–300 kcal real-world variance. Individual metabolic rate varies.

| Scenario | Calories | Deficit | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Current plan ~1,600 kcal | 1,600 | ~1,060 kcal/day | Aggressive — at this deficit depth, lean mass loss risk is meaningful |
| Moderate deficit | 2,100–2,200 | ~460–560 kcal | Evidence-supported for recomposition; within the "markedly impairs hypertrophy" warning from prior research |
| Conservative deficit | 2,300–2,400 | ~260–360 kcal | Slower fat loss, better lean mass preservation; optimal for simultaneous gain+loss |

**The 1,600 kcal target appears too aggressive.** The unverified source from research_v2 noted that "deficits of ~500 kcal can markedly impair hypertrophy in experienced lifters." A ~1,060 kcal daily deficit is well above that threshold.

However: the current diet plan shows actual intake ranges of 1,300–1,700 kcal depending on the day — which means average intake may be higher than 1,600 kcal, and deficit depth may be more moderate in practice. The Wednesday restaurant day is uncontrolled and likely closes some of the weekly deficit.

**Recommendation:** Increase daily calories to 1,900–2,100 kcal while maintaining 140–175 g protein. This preserves a meaningful fat-loss deficit without the lean mass risk of a >1,000 kcal/day deficit.

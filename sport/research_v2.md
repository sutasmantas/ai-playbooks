## 1. VOLUME

### Confirmed

**Volume positively drives both hypertrophy and strength with diminishing returns — not a linear curve.**
> "The posterior probability of volume's effect on muscle growth exceeding zero for the effect of volume on both hypertrophy and strength was 100%... both best-fit models suggest diminishing returns."
— Pelland et al. 2025, Sports Medicine (67 studies, 2,058 participants, Bayesian meta-regression). PMID 41343037.

`[EMPIRICAL — high confidence]` 3-0 verified. *Caveat: lead authors have fitness coaching roles; OSF data availability partially mitigates CoI risk.*

**Implications for the plan:**
- Every set added produces *some* hypertrophic return — there is no hard cutoff where sets become worthless.
- Diminishing returns means the *marginal* value of the 14th set per muscle per week is significantly lower than the 6th set.
- The current plan's high exercise count (9-11 exercises per session) is not necessarily counterproductive, but the time cost may exceed the marginal hypertrophic return.

---

**~10 sets/muscle/week is a reasonable practical floor — but the hard threshold and plateau point are not established.**
> "Low volumes (≤4 weekly sets per muscle group) produce substantial gains in muscle hypertrophy, but optimal results require at least 10 weekly sets per muscle group for maximizing muscle mass increases... further research is needed to clarify the level over which there is a plateau, which is currently not well understood."
— Bernárdez-Vázquez et al. 2022 umbrella review (14 meta-analyses, 4,784 participants). PMC9302196.

`[EMPIRICAL — medium confidence]` 2-1 verified. *One dissenter noted this is threshold language from a continuum finding.*

Additional evidence: Heaselgrave et al. found no statistically significant difference between 9, 18, and 27 weekly sets in trained males — consistent with the plateau being unestablished.

**Implications:**
- Staying in the 10–15 set range per muscle has no evidence penalty vs pushing to 20+.
- The current plan already comfortably exceeds the ~10-set floor for every major muscle group.
- Adding more accessory sets beyond what's already in the plan produces diminishing marginal returns.

---

### Refuted

**"12–20 sets/week is the optimal range" — 0-3 KILLED.**
The source (PMC8884877) dropped population qualifiers ("young, trained men"), stripped modal hedging ("may be"), and the study found high volume (>20 sets) WAS significantly superior for triceps (p=0.01, ES=-0.5) — the "plateau" claim was specific to quadriceps and biceps only, not most muscle groups. Superseded by Pelland et al. 2025 (larger, better powered).

**"Plateau exists above 20 sets" — 0-3 KILLED.**
Same source problems. Pelland 2025 found no discrete threshold, only a continuum with wide CIs at higher volumes.

## 2. TRAINING FREQUENCY

### Confirmed

**Training frequency has a negligible and inconsistent effect on hypertrophy when weekly volume is equated, but a reliable positive effect on strength.**
> "Frequency's effect on hypertrophy: posterior probability less than 100%, compatible with negligible effects and inconsistent. Frequency's effect on strength: 100% posterior probability."
— Pelland et al. 2025. PMID 41343037.

`[EMPIRICAL — high confidence]` 3-0 verified (the frequency-for-hypertrophy finding specifically).

**Full-body 2x/week and upper/lower split 4x/week produce statistically equivalent muscle mass gains (1.9% vs 1.7%, p=0.828) when volume is equated.**
— Calatayud et al. 2022, 12-week RCT (n=44). PMC9107721.

`[EMPIRICAL — medium confidence]` 3-0 verified. *Limitation: untrained women only — generalizability to intermediate male lifters is limited but directionally consistent.*

**Implications:**
- The 4-session upper/lower structure is not hypertrophy-superior to a 2-session full-body structure. It was correctly selected for strength development and scheduling.
- If sessions become unsustainable, dropping to 3 sessions/week carries no evidence-based penalty for muscle retention.

---

### Refuted

**"When volume is equated, frequency has no significant impact on hypertrophy" — 1-2 KILLED.**
Refuted because: (a) PMC9302196's hypertrophy measurements were confined to arms and thighs only — the review itself says "cannot be extrapolated to other muscle groups"; (b) there is evidence that at high per-session volumes (15+ sets per session), higher-frequency distribution outperforms lower-frequency for equivalent weekly sets (Zaroni et al. finding).

**"Training frequency does not have a reliable independent effect on hypertrophy" — 0-3 KILLED.**
This was a statistical misinterpretation: "posterior probability less than 100%" in Bayesian analysis does NOT mean the effect is negligible — virtually no real-world effect achieves exactly 100% posterior probability. The verifiers rejected this framing unanimously.

## 3. PERIODIZATION STRUCTURE

### Confirmed

**DUP and linear periodization produce statistically equivalent hypertrophy outcomes.**
> "Effects of the two periodization models on muscle hypertrophy are likely to be similar... choice may be a matter of individual preference."
— Grgic et al. 2017 meta-analysis (13 RCTs, n=417). Pooled Cohen's d = -0.02 (95% CI [-0.25, 0.21], p=0.848). PMC5571788.

Independently replicated: Zhang et al. 2026 meta-analysis (29 RCTs, n=704), Frontiers in Public Health, found no significant differences in body weight, BMI, body fat %, or fat-free mass between LP and UP.

`[EMPIRICAL — high confidence]` 2-1 verified. *Caveat: 12 of 13 studies in the 2017 meta used untrained participants.*

**Important exception:** The 2026 meta found LP was superior for **upper limb strength** specifically — DUP's advantage is in hypertrophy and tolerance of daily rep-range variation, not absolute strength accumulation.

**Periodization model is not a meaningful determinant of recomposition outcomes. Structure should be chosen for adherence and session length, not physiological superiority.**
— Confirmed across 3 independent sources: Grgic 2017 (DUP vs LP), Zhang 2026 (DUP vs LP), Calatayud 2022 (full-body vs upper/lower).

`[EMPIRICAL — high confidence]` 2-1 to 3-0 across constituent claims.

**Implications:**
- The DUP upper/lower structure is well-supported. Keeping it is correct.
- If 90–120 minute sessions are the problem, switching to full-body 3x/week carries no evidence penalty for hypertrophy.
- The structure is not the bottleneck — exercise selection and accessory volume are.

---

### Refuted

**"LP and DUP produce equivalent strength gains" — 0-3 KILLED.**
The 2026 meta (29 RCTs) found LP was statistically superior for upper limb strength. DUP ≠ LP for strength; DUP ≈ LP for hypertrophy and body composition.

**"Full-body 5x/week produces significantly more fat loss than upper/lower split 1x/week" — 0-3 KILLED.**
Source (Carneiro et al. 2024, n=23): (a) volume was not actually equated — warm-up sets created more total volume for full-body group; (b) comparison was full-body 5x/week vs classic bro-split 1x/week — not a fair comparison for the current plan (which trains each muscle 2x/week).

**"At matched volume, frequency was the differentiating fat-loss variable" — 0-3 KILLED.**
Same study, same confound — volume was NOT actually matched per the paper's own limitations section.

## 4. CARDIO INTERFERENCE

### Contested — No Verified Claims

All specific concurrent training claims were refuted by the adversarial verifiers. The literature in this area is genuinely contested:

| Refuted claim | Vote | Why killed |
|---|---|---|
| "Concurrent training doesn't interfere with hypertrophy (SMD: -0.01)" | 0-3 | Effect size too precise, population confounds |
| "Concurrent training doesn't interfere with 1RM strength (SMD: -0.06)" | 1-2 | Contested by other meta-analyses |
| "Running causes more fiber interference than cycling (SMD: -0.81)" | 0-3 | Conflicting evidence from other sources |
| "Resistance-before-endurance yields 6.91% more strength" | 1-2 | High variance estimate, limited studies |
| "Exercise order doesn't affect hypertrophy" | 0-3 | Contradicted by other meta-analyses |
| "Concurrent training blunts power (SMD: -0.35)" | 0-3 | Conflicted by concurrent training does-not-attenuate data |

`[CONTESTED]` No claim survived adversarial verification in this domain.

**What can be said with confidence:**
- The current plan already has the best-practice structure for concurrent training: cardio on separate days from lifting (Wed/Sat/Sun), which the prior research.md correctly identified as optimal. This is still the right call, now confirmed as the correct mitigation regardless of which interference claim is true.
- Catastrophic interference (i.e., cardio destroying muscle gains) is unlikely given the separate-day scheduling.
- Specific cardio-type superiority claims (cycling vs running) lack robust support.

**Null result: fasted cardio for fat loss** — No verified claim supported fasted cardio producing superior fat loss over fed cardio when total calories are controlled. This claim did not survive to the verified set. The existing research.md note ("no 24-hour fat-loss advantage") remains correct.

## 5. UNRESOLVED GAPS

The following dimensions were in scope but produced no verified claims — either because adversarial verification killed all candidate claims, or the research search didn't surface strong primary sources:

### 5.1 Session Duration and Exercise Count
**No verified claim addressed whether sessions beyond 60–75 minutes reduce hypertrophic stimulus.** The hormone-response argument (testosterone/GH drop after 60 min) is a widely circulated claim, but the research is weak:
- PMC3793973 (West et al.) found acute elevations in testosterone, GH, and IGF-1 following resistance training sessions do NOT predict or drive muscle hypertrophy outcomes. This was confirmed 3-0 in the verify phase.
- Implication: session duration as a hormonal-response constraint is not supported. What matters is total volume and proximity to failure — not the clock.
- The session length problem is therefore a *practical adherence* issue, not a *physiological* one. Long sessions that get skipped produce zero results.

`[EMPIRICAL — high confidence for the hormone claim being debunked]`
`[Research gap — session duration upper limit not directly studied in this audit]`

### 5.2 Accessory Exercise ROI
**No verified claim addressed the specific accessories in this plan.** The Y-raise, serratus punch, hip abduction, hip adduction, separate calf raises, Nordic curl, and face pulls were not covered by any source in the search.

What can be inferred from confirmed findings:
- The diminishing-returns curve means accessories providing fewer than ~5 sets/week of stimulus to a muscle that already receives 10+ sets from compounds produce marginal additional returns.
- For injury prevention purposes (lower traps, serratus, glute med, adductors), ROI framing may be wrong — these are not primarily hypertrophy targets; their case rests on structural/injury risk reduction, which requires separate research.

`[Research gap — requires dedicated search on each exercise]`

### 5.3 Caloric Strategy and Deficit Depth
**No verified claim addressed optimal deficit depth or calorie targets for recomposition at ~1 year training age.** One secondary source suggested deficits of ~500 kcal can meaningfully impair hypertrophy in experienced lifters, with 250–500 kcal recommended — but this claim was not subjected to adversarial verification in this round.

The protein claim from the prior research.md (1.6–2 g/kg/day) is directionally supported by a secondary source noting protein up to 1.9 g/kg is associated with less fat-free mass loss during restriction. The current intake of ~130–160g/day is consistent with this range for most body weights.

`[Research gap — requires dedicated caloric strategy search]`

### 5.4 Recovery and Mandatory Rest Days
**No verified claim addressed whether 7 sessions/week (no full rest days) is appropriate for an intermediate.** The deload protocol and overtraining signal tracking in the existing plan represents best-available-practice given this gap.

`[Research gap — requires dedicated recovery search]`

## 7. PLAN IMPLICATIONS — WHAT CHANGES

### What the evidence supports keeping:
- DUP Upper/Lower structure — validated ✓
- Cardio on separate days — best-practice for concurrent training ✓
- 10+ sets/week per major muscle group — evidence-supported ✓
- Current protein intake (~130–160g/day) — adequate ✓
- Deload every 4–5 weeks — still best-available-practice ✓
- Ignoring the anabolic window — correct ✓

### What the evidence suggests revising:
- **Session length is an adherence problem, not a physiology problem.** The evidence does not support a hard hormonal cutoff at 60–75 min. But a session that runs 90–120 min and gets skipped produces zero results. Trimming is justified by adherence logic, not metabolic damage.
- **Exercise count per session can be reduced without evidence penalty IF weekly volume per muscle is maintained.** The confirmed finding is that the *total weekly sets per muscle* drives outcomes — not the number of exercises per session.
- **Accessories should be audited by injury risk (not hypertrophy ROI) separately.** The evidence doesn't say remove them; it says they produce diminishing returns at the hypertrophy margin. Their value may be structural.

### What needs more research before deciding:
- Whether specific accessories (Y-raise, serratus, hip abduction, adductors) are worth keeping — requires dedicated evidence search on injury prevention for home gym intermediate lifters.
- Whether 1600 kcal is appropriate for current body weight — requires caloric strategy research.
- Whether the 7-session/week structure (no full rest days) is sustainable long-term — requires recovery research.

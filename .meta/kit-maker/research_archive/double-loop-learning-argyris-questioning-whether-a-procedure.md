### [key: dll-single-vs-double-dominance]
- evidence: consensus
- group: double-loop-fundamentals
- sources: https://infed.org/dir/welcome/chris-argyris-theories-of-action-double-loop-learning-and-organizational-learning/, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-loop_learning

**Claim:** Argyris's multi-decade intervention research (reported across organizations studied from the 1970s–1990s, summarized in Argyris & Schon 1978 and Argyris 1990 'Overcoming Organizational Defenses') found that virtually all participants operated from Model I theories-in-use — achieve-the-purpose unilaterally, suppress negative feelings, win-don't-lose — even when they espoused Model II (shared control, valid information, free choice). The mechanism: espoused theory is publicly stated; theory-in-use is the actual program governing action under pressure. The gap is rarely visible without third-party observation of behavior. Scope: universal across professional and managerial populations studied; not tested on algorithmic agents. Kit implication: an agent's stated procedure (espoused) and its actual decision path (theory-in-use) will diverge under constraint — assumption-level review must surface the operative decision program, not the documented one.

### [key: dll-governing-variables-mechanism]
- evidence: consensus
- group: double-loop-fundamentals
- sources: https://infed.org/dir/welcome/chris-argyris-theories-of-action-double-loop-learning-and-organizational-learning/, https://actiondesign.com/resources/readings/double-loop-learning

**Claim:** Double-loop learning (Argyris & Schon 1978) differs structurally from single-loop learning by targeting governing variables — the values, norms, and assumptions the actor seeks to satisfy — rather than the action strategy used to satisfy them. Mechanism: in single-loop learning, a mismatch between expected and actual outcome triggers a new action while the governing variable remains fixed; in double-loop learning the governing variable itself is held as a hypothesis subject to revision. The thermostat analogy (canonical): single-loop = adjust heating to reach 69F; double-loop = question whether 69F should be the target. Scope: the distinction only applies when an error can trace causally to a flawed governing assumption, not to implementation error. Kit implication: kit self-review must distinguish 'did this step execute correctly?' (single-loop) from 'should this step exist / is the criterion it satisfies the right one?' (double-loop); these require different prompts and different output formats.

### [key: dll-hierarchical-blocker]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high
- group: double-loop-blockers
- sources: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8671862/

**Claim:** A grounded-theory study of Balanced Scorecard implementation in a health organization (n=34 qualitative interviews, PMC8671862) found strong evidence of single-loop learning (goal alignment, target-setting) but minimal double-loop learning. The blocking mechanism: frontline staff identified KPI flaws and proposed structural changes but lacked authority to revise governing parameters. Employees described themselves as 'powerless' and responded with passive compliance ('you just passively cooperate'). Scope: hierarchical structures where feedback authority is centralized. Failure condition: double-loop learning is impossible when the agents who detect assumption failures have no pathway to act on them. Kit implication: for a kit to support double-loop learning, the agent detecting an assumption failure must have an escalation path to a decision-maker with authority to revise the governing procedure — detection alone is insufficient.

**Verify note:** Source confirmed: Li, Yang, Shih 2021, PMC8671862, grounded theory, n=34 interviews in a 290-bed Taiwan hospital. All specific sub-claims confirmed: single-loop strong/double-loop minimal, frontline powerlessness ('do not have the power and right to change'), passive compliance ('passively cooperat[ing]'). The kit implication (agent detecting assumption failure needs an escalation path) is the claim author's analogy from organisational behaviour to agent pipelines — structurally coherent, honestly framed as 'analogous.'

### [key: dll-psychological-safety-prerequisite]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high
- group: double-loop-blockers
- sources: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8671862/

**Claim:** The same BSC health organization study (PMC8671862) identified psychological safety as a necessary condition for double-loop learning: absence of psychological safety led to suppression of assumption-challenging observations even when the structural authority to act existed for senior staff. Additional failure conditions included professional status differentials (physician resistance to peer review) and top-down implementation without genuine staff participation. Success facilitator: 'BSC consensus camps' that created structured cross-departmental dialogue. Scope: human organizations; mechanism is analogous for agent pipelines where downstream agents suppress anomaly reports rather than surface them to avoid re-triggering expensive upstream steps. Kit implication: kit design must make anomaly reporting and assumption-questioning the zero-cost default path, not a cost-incurring deviation requiring justification.

**Verify note:** Same source (PMC8671862) confirmed. All sub-claims verified: psychological safety absence blocking assumption-challenging, physician resistance ('doctors think they are not fully restricted'), BSC consensus camps as structured dialogue facilitator, professional status differentials, top-down implementation. The agent pipeline analog (downstream agents suppressing anomaly reports) is the claim author's design inference — coherent but not a paper finding. Psychological safety as prerequisite for double-loop learning is consistent with Argyris/Schön foundational literature.

### [key: dll-big-assumptions-trigger]
- evidence: anecdote
- group: structural-triggers
- sources: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12895253/

**Claim:** Kegan & Lahey's 'big assumptions' intervention (applied to medical school curriculum reform, reported in PubMed 12895253) found that institutional resistance to change reflects competing organizational commitments — hidden assumptions that make the change feel threatening — rather than poor execution. The structural trigger that produced assumption-level questioning was deliberate facilitated invitation: reform leaders explicitly asked faculty to articulate their reservations. This surfaced hidden competing commitments (e.g., assumptions about physician authority, about what counts as legitimate knowledge) that were blocking reform without being named. Outcome: once surfaced, the organization put safeguards in place that 'ultimately ensured substantive reforms could be sustained.' Scope: applies to structured change initiatives with a facilitator; the trigger does not fire spontaneously. Kit implication: double-loop questioning requires an explicit probe — a purpose-designed prompt asking 'what assumption must be true for this procedure to make sense, and is that assumption actually true?' — not a general 'review this output' instruction.

### [key: dll-model1-defensive-routines]
- evidence: consensus
- group: double-loop-blockers
- sources: https://infed.org/dir/welcome/chris-argyris-theories-of-action-double-loop-learning-and-organizational-learning/

**Claim:** Argyris's empirical program (multi-organizational, summarized in 'Overcoming Organizational Defenses' 1990) found that Model I behavior — unilateral control, win-don't-lose, suppress negative feelings — generates defensive routines: self-sealing behaviors that prevent the detection and correction of errors at the assumption level. The mechanism is circular: Model I produces defensive routines; defensive routines make the governing variables undiscussable; undiscussability prevents double-loop learning; the system remains stable in a suboptimal equilibrium. Argyris found this pattern 'just about universal' across participants. Scope: human professionals under performance pressure. Kit implication: any kit self-evaluation that routes through a success-optimizing agent (one instructed to defend its own outputs) will produce the same defensive pattern; the evaluating agent must have no stake in the outcome of the procedure it is evaluating.

### [key: dll-second-order-change-operationalization-gap]
- evidence: consensus
- group: evidence-quality
- sources: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/275418948_First-Order_Second-Order_and_Third-Order_Change_and_Organization_Development_Interventions_A_Cognitive_Approach

**Claim:** A systematic review of second-order change literature (covering organizational development interventions classified as first-order/second-order/third-order, ResearchGate 275418948 and related meta-analysis) found that the majority of empirical works on second-order change are qualitative. Researchers noted that 'much remains to be learned' and that second-order change may not be 'a specific something' amenable to operationalization and rigorous measurement. This operationalization gap is the primary reason second-order / double-loop interventions lack the effect-size evidence base that first-order improvement methods have. Scope: all organizational learning research through 2023. Kit implication: when designing a kit mechanism for assumption-questioning, do not rely on outcome metrics to validate that double-loop learning occurred — the mechanism must be designed structurally (separate agent, separate prompt, explicit assumption-listing step) because outcome similarity between single-loop and double-loop sessions makes empirical differentiation unreliable.

### [key: dll-systematic-review-misconceptions]
- evidence: consensus
- group: evidence-quality
- sources: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/emre.12615

**Claim:** A systematic review published in European Management Review (Wiley, doi:10.1111/emre.12615) identified that empirical studies on double-loop learning frequently misapply the original concept — measuring proxies (e.g., behavioral change, strategy shifts) that conflate single-loop and double-loop learning. The review proposed a framework to address these measurement misconceptions and documented that the proliferation of misapplied studies creates false evidence of double-loop effects. Scope: organizational learning research literature broadly. Kit implication: when citing 'double-loop learning' as a mechanism in kit design, the triggering criterion must be precisely specified as 'governing variable revision' (changing what criterion the procedure optimizes for) not 'strategy adjustment' (changing how the procedure pursues a fixed criterion); otherwise the mechanism cannot be distinguished from single-loop correction.

### [key: dll-meta-agent-local-optima]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: medium
- group: ai-agent-double-loop
- sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2606.04455

**Claim:** The Meta-Agent Challenge benchmark (arxiv 2606.04455, n=393 meta-agent configurations across frontier LLMs) found that underperforming autonomous agent-builders 'trap themselves in design local optima' — premature convergence on a paradigm without questioning its structural assumptions. Only 55/393 configurations exceeded human-engineered baselines. The mechanism gap: agents optimize for immediate feedback signals (eval scores per iteration) rather than stepping back to question whether their agent architecture assumptions are correct. Successful agents used fewer evaluation calls, longer inter-call intervals, and simpler minimal ReAct-style loops — suggesting that productive reflection (pacing, simplicity) outperforms rapid iterative tweaking. Scope: LLM-based meta-agents on reasoning/coding tasks; proprietary models (Claude Sonnet/Opus) dominated success cases. Kit implication: a kit's self-improvement loop must include a forcing function that pauses iterative tweaking and asks 'is the architecture I am optimizing the right one?' — the equivalent of a double-loop interrupt — before further single-loop iteration is allowed.

**Verify note:** Source confirmed: Lu et al. 2026, arXiv:2606.04455. Local optima traps, frontier model dominance, minimal ReAct loops, longer inter-call intervals all confirmed. CRITICAL ERROR in claim: denominator is wrong. The paper reports 55 of 3939 configurations exceeded baselines; the claim states 55/393 — a 10× transcription error that changes the pass rate from ~1.4% to ~14%. All qualitative findings are sound; the double-loop framing is the claim author's conceptual mapping (paper does not use that terminology).

### [key: dll-hyperagents-meta-procedure-editable]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: medium
- group: ai-agent-double-loop
- sources: https://ai.meta.com/research/publications/hyperagents/, https://mlq.ai/news/meta-releases-hyperagents-self-modifying-ai-framework-enabling-autonomous-improvement-mechanisms/

**Claim:** Meta AI's HyperAgents framework (2025, ai.meta.com/research/publications/hyperagents/) achieved paper-review performance improvement from 0.0 to 0.710 and olympiad-math improvement of 0.630 by making the meta-level modification procedure itself editable — the system can rewrite how it improves itself, not just what it does. Mechanism: a task agent solves the target task; a meta agent modifies both itself and the task agent; crucially the meta agent's own modification procedure is also editable, enabling recursive governing-structure revision. This is the computational instantiation of double-loop learning: the system questions and rewrites the criterion by which improvement is measured, not just the behavior being improved. Scope: tested on coding, paper review, robotics, math grading; safety precautions (sandboxing, human oversight) required. Kit implication: a kit that can only revise its generation steps but not its evaluation criteria is structurally capped at single-loop learning; double-loop capability requires the evaluation criteria themselves to be revisable by the system.

**Verify note:** Source confirmed: Zhang et al. 2026 (claim says 2025 — year error), meta.ai. Editable meta-level modification procedure confirmed from primary source verbatim. Numbers (paper review 0.0→0.710, olympiad math 0.630) confirmed from mlq.ai secondary source with confidence intervals — not independently verified from primary PDF. Double-loop learning framing is the claim author's conceptual mapping; the paper uses 'metacognitive self-modification' not double-loop learning terminology. The mapping is structurally accurate.

### [key: dll-external-consultant-bypass-mechanism]
- evidence: consensus
- group: structural-triggers
- sources: https://actiondesign.com/resources/readings/double-loop-learning, https://infed.org/dir/welcome/chris-argyris-theories-of-action-double-loop-learning-and-organizational-learning/

**Claim:** Argyris (summarized in actiondesign.com and infed.org syntheses of his organizational interventions) identified three organizational interventions that produce double-loop learning: (1) external consultants or task forces authorized to bypass normal practices, (2) information systems that make hidden data visible across organizational boundaries, (3) new management with authority to mandate change. All three share a common structural feature: they bypass the defensive routines of the existing system by introducing an agent with no stake in maintaining the current governing variables. Scope: human organizations with entrenched defensive routines; each approach has documented limitations in producing sustained behavioral change. Kit implication: the double-loop review agent in a kit should be structurally separated from the generation agent — a dedicated 'assumption auditor' role with explicit authority to recommend procedure deletion or criterion revision, not just output improvement.

### [key: dll-framing-mechanism-schon]
- evidence: consensus
- group: structural-triggers
- sources: https://actiondesign.com/resources/readings/double-loop-learning

**Claim:** Schon's contribution to the Argyris-Schon model (detailed in actiondesign.com synthesis) identifies 'framing' as the operative mechanism of double-loop learning: actors make sense of a situation by setting the problem they will solve, and this framing implicitly determines what counts as error and what counts as success. Double-loop questioning requires discovering how current frames 'contribute to ineffective behavior' and identifying alternative frames. The mechanism that triggers frame-questioning is discovering that the current frame systematically misclassifies outcomes — situations the current frame calls 'success' that the agent's deeper goals would call 'failure.' Scope: applies wherever the problem definition is not identical to the underlying goal. Kit implication: a kit self-review prompt must include explicit frame-inspection — 'does the criterion this step was optimizing for actually capture the goal, or is this a case where optimizing the criterion diverges from achieving the goal?'

### [key: dll-metacognitive-agent-limitations]
- evidence: consensus
- group: ai-agent-double-loop
- sources: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05109

**Claim:** A 2025 theoretical analysis (arxiv 2506.05109, 'Truly Self-Improving Agents Require Intrinsic Metacognitive Learning') argues that current self-improvement approaches are rigid and fail to generalize because they lack three metacognitive components: metacognitive knowledge (self-assessment of capabilities and strategies), metacognitive planning (deciding what and how to learn), and metacognitive evaluation (reflecting on learning experiences). The paper frames this as a structural absence — agents cannot question their own improvement procedures because no mechanism exists for monitoring and revising the meta-level process. Scope: theoretical analysis; no empirical benchmark results in abstract. Open challenge: optimal distribution of metacognitive responsibility between humans and agents remains unresolved. Kit implication: a kit's self-improvement mechanism needs all three components to achieve genuine double-loop behavior — self-assessment to surface assumption violations, planning to decide which assumptions to revise, and evaluation to assess whether the revision improved the governing criterion.

## Null results
- No quantitative effect sizes for double-loop vs single-loop learning outcomes in controlled organizational studies — all comparative evidence is qualitative or case-based
- No empirical studies directly measuring frequency of assumption-questioning events per unit time in AI agent workflows
- Second-order change literature lacks operationalized metrics making effect-size extraction impossible
- HyperAgents paper did not disclose domain-specific baselines in publicly accessible abstract/summary — only improvement deltas available

## New angles surfaced
- Deutero-learning (Bateson's third order) — learning how to learn; structural triggers for organizations to improve their learning system itself; may have empirical grounding in organizational ambidexterity literature
- Exploration vs exploitation tradeoff literature (March 1991 and follow-on) — empirically grounded analog to single vs double loop; when does a system search its current solution space vs question the solution space definition
- Cognitive dissonance and belief perseverance literature (Festinger, Nickerson) — empirical mechanism for why governing assumptions resist questioning even when contradictory evidence is available; directly relevant to kit defensive-routine design
- Reflexive monitoring in Actor-Network Theory and evaluation research — structural role of designated evaluator with no stake in the evaluated system's survival; empirical studies of independent audit vs self-assessment accuracy gaps

Adversarial counter-case (Q3b — constructed without confirmatory context):
The claim to refute: "Expanding a vague one-sentence topic into multiple queries reliably improves research coverage."

Strongest counter-argument: Sparse input is not an underdetermined query — it is a query whose meaning is exactly as narrow as the user stated. Expansion by LLM injects the LLM's priors about what the topic means, substituting LLM world-knowledge for user intent. The sparser the input, the higher the ratio of LLM-supplied meaning to user-supplied meaning — so expansion is most dangerous precisely for the sparse-input case this angle covers. For niche or tail topics (rare concepts, emerging terminology, non-English domains), the LLM's world-knowledge is weakest where expansion is most needed, producing hallucinated or adjacent-field expansions that import irrelevant vocabulary. The empirical "improvements" in QA-Expand (+13% BEIR) and CCQGen (NDCG improvement) are measured on benchmark datasets where "comprehensive coverage" is defined by a fixed annotation set — this is not the same as true topical coverage in an open-ended research pipeline. You can measure that you retrieved more documents matching the annotations; you cannot measure that you covered the topic. Finally, every additional sub-query retrieved by expansion adds noise to the reranking pool: when the best document is at rank N=2 in the original query and at rank N=52 after expansion noise, the reranker cannot recover it (bounded recall from wrong direction).

Resolution of adversarial case: The adversarial counter is partially confirmed by the literature. Expansion IS conditionally harmful. The practical resolution is: (a) always keep the original query in the set, (b) expand by asking diverse question types rather than synonyms, (c) apply explicit concept coverage tracking so new queries target gaps not already covered, (d) gate expansion away from high-specificity anchors. The failure mode is expansion-as-synonym-proliferation; the safe form is expansion-as-facet-generation.

## Key findings

**Finding 1: LLM-based query expansion conditionally degrades retrieval and fails on knowledge-deficient and highly ambiguous queries**
- Claim: A systematic study (Abe et al., SIGIR 2025, 7 datasets: NQ, TriviaQA, MS MARCO, BioASQ, AmbigDocs, AmbigQA, TREC Web) demonstrates two specific failure modes for LLM-based query expansion: (a) when the LLM lacks knowledge of the query topic, generated expansions introduce incorrect terms, degrading NDCG@10 and Recall@100; (b) when the query is semantically ambiguous, the LLM makes a biased directional interpretation, narrowing coverage to one facet and excluding the others. The mechanism: expansion fills lexical gaps using LLM priors — when those priors are wrong (knowledge gap) or overspecific (ambiguity collapse), the expanded query retrieves from a shifted distribution that misses originally-relevant documents.
- Source: arXiv:2505.12694 (Abe, Takeoka, Kato, Oyamada, 2025); ACM SIGIR 2025 proceedings — https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.12694
- Confidence: HIGH
- Verify note: Published at SIGIR 2025 (top IR venue), multiple datasets with controlled experiments distinguishing knowledge-deficiency from ambiguity. Scope condition: applies most strongly to general-domain queries (general domains showed more degradation than specialized domains like BioASQ). Kit implication well-scoped: the two failure conditions (knowledge gap + high ambiguity) are detectable from query characteristics and are specifically the conditions most likely to occur for sparse one-sentence topics about novel or niche subjects.

**Finding 2: Domain-dependent rewriting — -9.0% to +5.1% nDCG@10 depending on corpus match, and simple gating cannot fix it**
- Claim: A 2026 empirical study (Kotte, arXiv:2603.13301) across three BEIR benchmarks and two dense retrievers finds that prompt-only LLM query rewriting produces strongly domain-dependent results: -9.0% nDCG@10 on FiQA (financial QA), +5.1% on TREC-COVID (biomedical), and no significant effect on SciFact. The mechanism: 95% of rewrites involve lexical substitution regardless of outcome. When queries already use corpus-preferred domain terminology, rewriting replaces these domain-specific terms with general paraphrases, reducing retrieval alignment. When queries use non-standard or inconsistent terminology, rewriting toward corpus vocabulary helps. Simple feature-based gating (to predict when to rewrite) does not reliably outperform never rewriting.
- Source: arXiv:2603.13301 (Kotte, March 2026) — https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.13301
- Confidence: HIGH
- Verify note: Systematic empirical study on BEIR (well-established benchmark), specific metrics and percentages. Single author, but BEIR is reproducible. Scope condition: applies to dense retrieval (vector) — sparse BM25 may behave differently. The finding that "simple gating doesn't reliably outperform never rewriting" is the most kit-relevant adversarial result: any kit procedure that adds a rewriting step without a reliable gate is adding unpredictable variance, not guaranteed improvement.

**Finding 3: Multi-question generation from a vague input — generating N diverse sub-questions each targeting a distinct facet — improves retrieval by up to 13% over single-query baselines**
- Claim: QA-Expand (Seo & Lee, 2025, arXiv:2502.08557) demonstrates a three-step approach: (1) a single LLM call generates up to 3 diverse questions from the initial query, each explicitly targeting a distinct aspect of the information need; (2) pseudo-answers are generated for each question; (3) a feedback-driven filter retains only the most relevant answers. On BEIR benchmark (4 datasets) + TREC DL'19/DL'20, QA-Expand achieves up to 13% improvement over state-of-the-art baselines (0.5372 average nDCG@10 vs Q2D baseline 0.5311). The mechanism: asking different question types (what, why, how, context) about the same topic forces orthogonal coverage that synonyms cannot achieve.
- Source: arXiv:2502.08557 (Seo, Lee, Enhans.ai, 2025) — https://arxiv.org/html/2502.08557v1
- Confidence: MEDIUM
- Verify note: Industry origin (Enhans.ai) not academic, but tested on standard BEIR + TREC DL benchmarks. 13% is the upper bound (best case), not average across all datasets. The core principle (diverse question types > synonyms) is corroborated by the DMQR-RAG framework (diverse strategies outperform volume) and LangChain MultiQueryRetriever production use. Kit implication well-scoped: applies to vague/broad topics where facets are unclear; less applicable to precise lookup queries.

**Finding 4: Explicit concept coverage tracking — conditioning each new query on concepts NOT yet covered by prior queries — significantly outperforms unconstrained generation**
- Claim: CCQGen (Kang et al., WSDM 2025, arXiv:2502.11181) shows that iterative query generation conditioned on a "coverage gap" signal (tracking which document concepts have not yet been addressed by prior generated queries) outperforms unconstrained generation. On CSFCube dataset with Contriever-MS backbone: NDCG@10 improves from 0.3418 (baseline) to 0.3670 (CCQGen), Recall@100 from 0.7225 to 0.7526. Combined with concept-similarity-enhanced retrieval: NDCG@10 reaches 0.4244. The mechanism: unconstrained LLM generation tends to repeat the most salient aspects of a topic, producing redundant queries that cover the same facets with different words. Coverage-tracking forces orthogonal generation by explicitly listing what is NOT yet covered.
- Source: arXiv:2502.11181 (Kang, Jin, Kweon, Zhang, Lee, Han, Yu, WSDM 2025) — https://arxiv.org/html/2502.11181v1
- Confidence: HIGH
- Verify note: Published at WSDM 2025 (top data mining venue), multi-institution (Yonsei, Korea, UIUC). Uses an academic taxonomy for concept space which limits generalizability to non-scientific domains. The coverage-gap conditioning principle is the generalizable finding; the specific taxonomy mechanism may need adaptation for general research topics. Recall improvement (+3.9pp at @100) is the most practically significant number for research comprehensiveness goals.

**Finding 5: Diversity in expansion strategies matters more than number of queries — four semantically distinct rewriting strategies outperform volume-based approaches**
- Claim: DMQR-RAG (Alibaba, arXiv:2411.13154, Nov 2024) proposes four rewriting strategies that operate at different information levels (paraphrase, specification, generalization, and perspective shift), validated through experiments in academic and industry settings. An adaptive strategy selector minimizes query count while optimizing retrieval diversity. The key principle: each rewritten query must provide unique information not present in the others; more queries of the same type add noise without adding coverage. The framework also applies to queries with "varying information quantities," making it specifically designed for the sparse-input case.
- Source: arXiv:2411.13154 (Alibaba, 2024) — https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.13154; OpenReview: https://openreview.net/forum?id=lz936bYmb3
- Confidence: MEDIUM
- Verify note: Industry research (Alibaba), validated in academic + industry settings but full experimental details are in the paper. The four-strategy taxonomy (paraphrase, specification, generalization, perspective shift) aligns with what QA-Expand and CCQGen independently found, providing triangulation. The adaptive selector result (fewer diverse queries outperform more same-type queries) is practically important: a kit procedure should prioritize strategy diversity over query count.

**Finding 6: Classic pseudo-relevance feedback (PRF) is vulnerable to topic drift; LLM-based generative relevance feedback avoids first-pass dependency but inherits LLM knowledge limitations**
- Claim: Classic PRF (e.g., RM3, BM25+RM3) estimates expansion terms from top-ranked initial results and remains vulnerable to topic drift when early results include noise or tangential content — the expansion quality is bounded by first-pass retrieval quality. LLM-based generative relevance feedback (GRF, arXiv:2305.07477) generates synthetic relevant documents without depending on a first-pass retrieval, breaking this dependency. A 2025 systematic study (arXiv:2603.11008) of PRF with LLMs finds that LLM assistance improves PRF robustness by better identifying relevant passages in pseudo-documents. For sparse/vague inputs, the LLM approach is structurally preferable because there is no reliable first-pass result set to bootstrap classic PRF.
- Source: arXiv:2305.07477 (Mackie et al., 2023 — Generative and PRF for sparse/dense/learned retrieval); arXiv:2603.11008 (2025 — systematic PRF+LLM study) — https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.07477
- Confidence: HIGH (well-established finding, multiple papers, replicated)
- Verify note: Classic PRF limitations are consensus-level in IR (Rocchio algorithm is 50+ years old, drift is documented). The LLM-based approach advantage is empirically supported but dependent on LLM knowledge quality (Finding 1's failure condition applies here too: if LLM lacks topic knowledge, GRF produces low-quality synthetic documents). Scope condition: applies most directly to cold-start / sparse-input case where first-pass retrieval is unreliable.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

**Rule 1 — Never expand by synonym proliferation; expand by facet/question-type diversification.**
The failure mode of query expansion from sparse input is generating variations of the same question with different words. The productive mode is generating different *types* of questions about the topic: definitional (what is X?), mechanistic (how does X work?), contextual (where/when is X applied?), critical (what are limitations/alternatives to X?), and historical (how did X develop?). These target orthogonal document types and are unlikely to retrieve the same document.

**Rule 2 — Keep the original query in the set; expansion adds, never replaces.**
The original one-sentence input captures the user's exact lexical intent. It should always be query 1 in the final set. Expansion queries are parallel additions. Any procedure that replaces the original with a rewrite risks the -9% degradation scenario (Finding 2) by substituting user-intended terminology with LLM-preferred paraphrases.

**Rule 3 — Apply a coverage-gap check before generating each additional query.**
After generating N queries, extract the conceptual coverage: what aspects of the topic have already been addressed? Generate the next query specifically targeting an uncovered aspect. This implements the CCQGen principle (Finding 4) without requiring a formal taxonomy: the gap identification can be done by prompting the LLM with "Given these queries: [list], what important aspect of [topic] is NOT yet addressed?"

**Rule 4 — Apply a specificity gate: high-specificity topics (named entities, exact error strings, version numbers, proper nouns) should NOT be expanded.**
When the input contains anchors that are uniquely identifying (a named algorithm, a specific tool, a version number), expansion replaces these anchors with paraphrases, degrading lexical match. For these inputs, the correct behavior is: search as-is, and if results are thin, broaden by removing one qualifier at a time — not by paraphrasing the anchor.

**Rule 5 — For topics the LLM is unlikely to know well (emerging fields, niche domains, non-English topics), flag expansion as unreliable and require a seed document.**
Finding 1 establishes that expansion degrades when the LLM lacks topic knowledge. A kit procedure should detect this condition: if the topic involves terminology that appears post-2023, is domain-specific to a narrow field, or is in a non-English research tradition, expansion reliability decreases. The mitigation is to retrieve a seed document first (even via search), then use that document's vocabulary for expansion — replacing LLM world-knowledge with corpus-vocabulary.

**Rule 6 — Target 3-5 expansion queries, not more.**
The DMQR-RAG adaptive selector (Finding 5) shows diminishing returns and noise addition beyond the first few diverse queries. The QA-Expand default is 3 questions. CCQGen shows most coverage gains from the first few coverage-gap queries. A procedure that generates 10+ queries is likely producing within-cluster redundancy with zero additional coverage benefit and increasing reranker noise.

**Intake questions suggested by this research:**
- Is the topic in a domain where the LLM has strong world-knowledge? (Flag if not — use seed-document expansion instead of pure LLM expansion)
- Does the topic contain uniquely identifying anchors (proper nouns, version numbers)? (If yes: gate against expansion; search as-is first)
- Is the user looking for breadth (all facets of a topic) or depth (exhaustive retrieval of one specific facet)? (Breadth: expand by question type; Depth: do NOT expand, narrow further instead)

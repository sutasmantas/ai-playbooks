## Key findings

**Finding 1: The vocabulary bootstrapping loop is structurally circular and cannot be broken from inside a single vocabulary frame**

Claim: Entering an unknown domain requires field vocabulary to search effectively, but acquiring field vocabulary requires reading the field — a genuine circular dependency. Approximately 10% of relevant literature in any field is permanently unreachable from any single vocabulary tradition (cris-golden-gap-418pct, HIGH). The average well-formed query misses 30–40% of relevant documents due to vocabulary divergence alone (Sparck Jones 1972, replicated). The circular dependency is not fixed by more queries in the same vocabulary; it is broken only by externally seeding domain terminology before systematic search begins.

Source: Hjorland (2002) domain analysis in LIS; Sparck Jones 1972 vocabulary mismatch replication; research-quality.md (cris-golden-gap-418pct, HIGH); cross-concordance recall study (arXiv:0806.3765 — terminology mapping increases recall by ~100%)

Confidence: CONSENSUS (vocabulary mismatch as structural phenomenon is multiply replicated; specific ~10% unreachability figure has single sourcing — treat as HIGH-directional, not point-estimate)

Verify note: The ~10% figure is cited in research-quality.md but the primary source URL was not retrieved this wave. Cross-concordance finding (arXiv:0806.3765) independently quantifies recall gains of ~100% from terminology translation, providing directional support for the vocabulary gap claim even if the exact percentage varies.

**Finding 2: Survey papers and review articles are the primary vocabulary-seeding mechanism for unknown domain entry**

Claim: Survey papers and systematic review articles serve as field ontologies — they enumerate the domain's terminology, identify key researchers, map major debates, and provide the vocabulary needed to conduct subsequent targeted searches. Reading a survey paper before conducting primary searches breaks the vocabulary bootstrapping loop by providing the missing lexical seed. This strategy is documented across practitioner traditions (LessWrong "How to Do Research", Princeton undergraduate research guide) and in academic search methodology (systematic review guidelines across disciplines). The secondary benefit is that survey papers surface adjacent fields and cross-domain connections not visible from a single-tradition search.

Source: LessWrong "How to Do Research v1" (https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/AFCx9LLGnJFdtaqik/how-to-do-research-v1); Princeton Correspondents on Undergraduate Research (https://pcur.princeton.edu/2018/10/starting-new-research-how-to-learn-what-you-dont-know/); systematic review methodology (multiple library guides); arXiv:1906.04800 (scientometric review methodology)

Confidence: CONSENSUS (practitioner consensus across traditions; not a single empirical study)

Verify note: Consensus claim — no single study, but convergent practitioner + academic methodology agreement. The key limitation (noted as adversarial): survey papers encode their authors' vocabulary frames and may exclude minority traditions within the field. Start with the most cited recent survey + at least one survey from a different disciplinary tradition if the phenomenon spans fields.

**Finding 3: Citation pearl growing / snowballing provides systematic field coverage but cannot escape its starting-set citation island**

Claim: Citation pearl growing (starting with 1-3 highly relevant papers and following citations forward and backward) identifies approximately 51% of references found in systematic reviews. It is the primary mechanism for expanding coverage once a vocabulary seed exists. Critical structural limitation: snowballing cannot retrieve papers that are not connected via citations to the starting set. Starting papers from one vocabulary tradition produce a snowball that stays within that tradition's citation network, systematically excluding parallel traditions that use different terminology for the same phenomenon. The mitigation is to ensure starting papers include at least one representative from each vocabulary tradition identified in Step 0 (research-quality.md snowballing-start-set-dependency, consensus).

Source: Oxbridge Essays snowballing guide (51% recall figure); research-quality.md (symbals-snowballing-recall-boost-9-10pct, HIGH; snowballing-start-set-dependency, CONSENSUS); Booth (2008) "Unpacking your literature search toolbox"

Confidence: HIGH for recall contribution; CONSENSUS for start-set dependency limitation

Verify note: The 51% figure is plausible and widely cited but specific to certain review types; original source is Booth 2008 or similar. Start-set dependency is logically structural — snowballing along citation graphs cannot cross non-edges. Both claims should hold across domains.

**Finding 4: Expert consultation provides tacit field knowledge structurally unavailable in published literature**

Claim: Domain experts (key informants) possess tacit knowledge about their field that does not appear in searchable literature: which databases are actually used by the community, which grey literature sources exist, what conferences matter, what vocabulary the community currently uses versus historical vocabulary, and which papers are foundational versus historically superseded. Identifying and consulting a domain expert before or alongside initial literature search is documented as a best practice in systematic review methodology (librarian consultation), qualitative research (key informant interviews), and practitioner guides. Critical access problem: identifying experts requires some prior field knowledge — true blank-slate entry has no direct path to experts. Mitigation: use visible markers (prolific survey authors, conference organizers, course syllabi authors) as proxy expert signals before direct contact.

Source: SAGE Key Informant methodology (Sage Research Methods Encyclopedia); PMC/NIH key informant interview guidance; systematic review guidelines recommending librarian consultation; Binghamton University systematic review guide

Confidence: CONSENSUS

Verify note: The access problem (finding experts without prior knowledge) is a real barrier not addressed by existing methodology literature. Proxy signals (survey authors, course syllabi) are practitioner workarounds, not formally studied. This creates a gap in the literature: there is no empirical study of how effectively proxy-expert signals reduce domain entry time vs. direct expert consultation.

**Finding 5: LLM-powered interdisciplinary tools break the vocabulary loop by mapping researcher framing to field-specific terminology automatically**

Claim: Tools like DiscipLink (arXiv:2408.00447, 2024) initiate exploratory questions "from the perspectives of possible relevant fields of study" and automatically expand queries with "disciplinary-specific terminologies," directly addressing the vocabulary bootstrapping problem. The mechanism is: the LLM has internalized multi-field vocabulary relationships from training data and can translate a researcher's naive framing into field-specific search terms without requiring prior domain knowledge. Critical scope condition: this capability is bounded by training data coverage — fields underrepresented in the LLM's training corpus (non-English literature, proprietary industrial knowledge, emerging subfields) will not benefit from this translation. Additionally, LLMs may generate plausible-sounding but incorrect field-specific terminology ("hallucinated vocabulary") if the domain is sparsely represented.

Source: arXiv:2408.00447 (DiscipLink: Unfolding Interdisciplinary Information Seeking Process via Human-AI Co-Exploration, 2024)

Confidence: MEDIUM (single paper, limited domain coverage tested; mechanism is plausible and the tool is real, but generalizability to sparse or non-English domains unproven)

Verify note: Single paper. The core mechanism (LLM vocabulary translation) is independently validated by cross-lingual IR literature showing >20% recall gains from vocabulary expansion. The hallucination risk for sparse domains is real and documented in LLM literature but not specifically studied for this tool. Downgrade to MEDIUM until replicated.

**Finding 6: Frame lock is the primary failure mode — confirmation bias in initial vocabulary selection makes search structurally incapable of finding disconfirming or alternative-tradition evidence**

Claim: When a researcher searches an unfamiliar domain using vocabulary derived from their own prior beliefs or adjacent fields, those search terms embed the researcher's frame as a structural constraint on what can be found. 92.7% of systematic review search strategies contain errors, and this rate did not improve across 13 years (2006–2019) despite significant investment in training (Tetzlaff/Sampson). The most common error type negatively affects recall (78.1% of errors), not precision. The mechanism: vocabulary selection is the prior belief made operational — "beneficial effects of X" retrieves confirming literature; the query cannot retrieve what it doesn't contain terms for. Standard search procedures do not require generating disconfirming vocabulary. Mitigation: the adversarial search arm (searching for failures, critiques, null results) and the multi-tradition vocabulary requirement (research-quality.md Step 0) are the structural fixes; procedural training alone is insufficient.

Source: Tetzlaff/Sampson studies (cited in research-quality.md A2, A5; 92.7% error rate, stable 2006-2019); Framing and Bias literature review (ResearchGate); PMC article on literature search bias; research-quality.md adversarial calibration section

Confidence: HIGH (error-rate figure is from systematic review methodology literature with specific study design; mechanism is well-supported across traditions)

Verify note: Tetzlaff/Sampson finding is from systematic reviews in healthcare — extrapolation to general domain bootstrapping is directionally valid but not empirically tested in that context. The mechanism (vocabulary selection embeds priors) is structural and holds across domains. The 92.7% figure is the strongest single number in support of mandatory adversarial search arms.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

**1. Mandatory vocabulary enumeration before any search (Step 0)**
No search query should be written before naming at least two research traditions that study the phenomenon. Single-tradition vocabulary identification should trigger a cross-disciplinary search before proceeding. Rule form: "If you can only name one tradition: run a deliberate cross-field check in 2-3 adjacent fields before writing queries." (Already in research-quality.md — confirms the existing procedure is correctly grounded.)

**2. Survey-paper-first intake protocol for unknown domains**
When a research agent receives an angle in a domain it has no background in: first query should be a survey/review search (explicit query form: "[domain] survey" OR "[domain] systematic review" OR "[domain] review 2022-2025"). Extract vocabulary from survey before proceeding to primary queries. Log the vocabulary extracted and which tradition it comes from. This is not currently explicit in the kit — add as a Phase 3 startup rule for angles where the agent has no prior vocabulary.

**3. Snowballing start-set diversity requirement**
When running citation pearl growing: starting set must include at least one paper from each vocabulary tradition identified in Step 0. A start set drawn from a single tradition produces a snowball constrained to that citation island. Log the tradition of each starting paper explicitly.

**4. Expert proxy signals as intake question**
For unknown domains: add an intake question or Step 0 check — "Who are the 2-3 most-cited survey authors in this domain?" (proxy expert identification). Use those names as search seeds (author name queries) and as implied vocabulary anchors. This breaks the "expert identification requires prior knowledge" barrier by using visibility metrics as a proxy.

**5. Adversarial vocabulary arm is non-optional for frame-locked entries**
When entering a domain where the researcher's prior vocabulary is the only available seed: Q3 adversarial search must specifically target alternative vocabulary frames, not just critiques of findings. Form: "[phenomenon] [alternative-field-name] terminology" OR "[domain] terminology [adjacent discipline]". Frame-locked entry without an adversarial vocabulary query produces a structurally biased corpus regardless of other quality controls.

**6. LLM vocabulary translation as a bootstrapping tool, with hallucination guard**
LLM tools can translate naive researcher framing into field-specific search terms. Procedure: ask the LLM "What are the technical terms used in [field X] for [naive concept Y]? List terms from at least 3 subfields or traditions." Then treat LLM-provided terms as provisional vocabulary seeds, not confirmed vocabulary — verify each term by finding at least one paper that uses it before committing to it as a search anchor. This guards against the hallucinated-vocabulary failure mode.

# A02 — vocabulary-cluster-enumeration

---

### [key: furnas-1987-20pct]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence:
- group: vocabulary-mismatch-scope
- sources: https://honnef.co/notes/references/furnasvocabularyproblemhumansystem1987/, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocabulary_mismatch

**Claim:** Furnas, Landauer, Gomez, and Dumais (1987) studied spontaneous word choice for objects in five application domains and found that two independent people naming the same thing choose the same word with probability less than 0.20 — that is, >80% of the time, experts in the same field name the same concept with different words. Mechanism: each person's vocabulary is shaped by their training context and role; there is no single "canonical" word for most concepts. Scope: measured within single domains (same field, same objects). The implication for pre-search vocabulary enumeration is that beginning from one phrase and expanding only within that phrase's semantic neighborhood will miss at least 80% of the relevant literature that uses a different term for the same concept.

**Falsification:** This mechanism would be falsified if a domain with enforced terminological standardization (e.g., a domain with a mandatory international controlled vocabulary adopted universally) showed inter-rater agreement above 0.50 — indicating standardization, not natural language, is the operative factor. No such domain has been documented in the general scientific literature.

### [key: sr-error-rate-recall-bias]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence:
- group: vocabulary-mismatch-scope
- sources: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6466507/, https://systematicreviewsjournal.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13643-020-01542-z

**Claim:** A study of 137 systematic reviews found that 92.7% of search strategies contained errors; 78.1% of those errors negatively affected recall, primarily by missing natural language synonyms and controlled vocabulary terms (MeSH and related). This error rate did not improve between 2006 and 2019 despite significant investment in search training and guidelines (Tetzlaff/Sampson). Mechanism: vocabulary enumeration requires knowing the field's terminology before searching it — this circularity cannot be resolved by procedure alone. Scope: biomedical/health systematic reviews; likely directionally true in other fields but not empirically tested at the same scale.

**Falsification:** Would be falsified if a domain using AI-assisted term suggestion (e.g., automated MeSH suggestion at arXiv:2208.09984) showed error rates below 20% on a comparable review corpus — which would indicate the circularity is procedurally solvable.

### [key: optimization-technique-two-set]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence:
- group: vocabulary-discovery-methods
- sources: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6148622/

**Claim:** A systematic approach to search strategy development (Faes et al., published PMC6148622) describes an "optimization technique" for discovering unknown terms: compare the set retrieved by thesaurus/controlled vocabulary terms against the set retrieved by free-text keyword terms — then examine the papers that appear in one set but not the other. Papers in "thesaurus NOT free-text" reveal controlled terms that the searcher's natural language missed. Papers in "free-text NOT thesaurus" reveal natural language terms not yet captured in controlled vocabularies. Mechanism: the two vocabularies (controlled + natural language) have partially non-overlapping coverage; their symmetric difference contains candidates the searcher could not have known to include a priori. Scope: works within a single database that has both controlled vocabulary and full-text indexing (e.g., PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase).

### [key: pearl-growing-seed-paper]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence:
- group: vocabulary-discovery-methods
- sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl_growing, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1175807/, https://www.mediwrite.com.au/medical-writing/pearl-growing/

**Claim:** Citation pearl growing (Harter 1986; also called "seed document" method, "citation chasing", "bibliographic search") uses one known-relevant paper as a vocabulary seed: extracting its controlled vocabulary terms (e.g., MeSH "Entry Terms", Emtree "Synonyms"), author-assigned keywords, title noun phrases, and abstract vocabulary — then using those extracted terms to build a new search. Mechanism: a paper indexed in a database was assigned controlled vocabulary terms by a professional indexer who decided what the paper was about — those terms are often from traditions the searcher is not aware of. Scope: most valuable at the start of a search when the searcher has one known-relevant paper but limited domain vocabulary. Bounded by citation-graph connectivity: two research traditions that have never cited each other cannot be bridged by pearl growing alone.

### [key: llm-popularity-bias-frame-collapse]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence:
- group: vocabulary-discovery-methods
- sources: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.12694, https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3726302.3730222

**Claim:** LLM-based query expansion (SIGIR 2025 short paper, arXiv:2505.12694) was found to favor popular interpretations of ambiguous queries and overlook rarer ones — with retrieval effectiveness diminishing for less-popular query interpretations (measured by Wikipedia page-view popularity of query sense). For knowledge-deficient domains, LLMs introduced non-existent entities or unrelated terms, causing -10.02 NDCG@10 drops on BioASQ. Mechanism: LLMs trained on internet text encode the popularity distribution of sense usage — when expanding an ambiguous term, the model resolves ambiguity toward the dominant internet sense, suppressing minority or cross-disciplinary senses. Scope: demonstrated on general domain IR datasets and BioASQ; explicitly strongest failure in general domains, somewhat mitigated in specialized domains with distinct training signal. Implication: using LLM synonym expansion alone to discover vocabulary traditions will produce a list dominated by the most prominent internet framing — cross-disciplinary alternative names will be systematically underrepresented.

**Falsification:** Would be falsified if LLM expansions showed recall improvement for rare/minority senses at the same rate as for popular senses — which the paper explicitly shows does not hold.

### [key: coword-vocabulary-island-detection]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence:
- group: vocabulary-discovery-methods
- sources: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0306457300000510, https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2022.844425/full

**Claim:** Co-word analysis (Callon et al., bibliometrics tradition) identifies distinct vocabulary islands within a research domain by mapping which author keywords co-occur in publications — clusters of keywords that frequently appear together represent a research community with its own terminology, while low co-occurrence between keyword clusters indicates vocabulary separation between communities studying the same phenomenon under different names. Mechanism: co-occurrence reflects shared conceptual framing — two communities studying the same phenomenon will have keyword clusters that don't overlap if they've developed independently. Scope: requires an existing corpus of publications with author keywords; best applied to large databases (Scopus, WoS) with keyword fields populated. Limitation: cannot identify vocabulary islands that are absent from the database being analyzed.

### [key: over-expansion-precision-degradation]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence:
- group: vocabulary-discovery-methods
- sources: https://nlp.stanford.edu/IR-book/pdf/09expand.pdf, https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.12694

**Claim:** Thesaurus-based query expansion consistently faces a precision-recall trade-off: adding more synonym terms improves recall but degrades precision, particularly when expanded terms are polysemous in the target domain. The most common failure mode is over-expansion — adding too many terms pulls in irrelevant documents, making retrieval unreliable. Mechanism: thesaurus relations (broader/narrower/related terms) are not context-sensitive — a "related term" for one sense of a word may be irrelevant for another sense. Scope: documented across multiple IR evaluations; most severe when queries contain short, ambiguous terms. Implication for vocabulary enumeration: exhaustive synonym listing is the wrong goal — the goal is discriminating terms (terms that appear when the phenomenon IS present and rarely otherwise), not maximal synonym coverage.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

**Rule 1 — Step 0 minimum: name 2 traditions before writing any query.**
Single-tradition enumeration is documented to miss >10% of relevant literature (research-quality.md, `cris-golden-gap-418pct`). The Furnas 1987 finding (>80% vocabulary mismatch) means a single-phrase start is effectively a single-tradition start. Step 0 must produce at least 2 research traditions with distinct vocabulary before any query is written. If a second tradition cannot be named after a deliberate cross-field check: document as "verified null" (a findable result), not as implicit confirmation of single-tradition coverage.

**Rule 2 — Survey paper first gate for unfamiliar domains.**
When entering an unfamiliar domain from a single starting phrase, the first query should be: find a broad survey or review paper on the topic. Survey papers enumerate multiple research traditions and their terminology explicitly — this is their structural purpose. Extract controlled vocabulary terms, author keywords, and section headings from the survey. Use these to populate Step 0 traditions before writing the systematic queries.

**Rule 3 — Pearl growing is mandatory after finding any high-quality seed paper.**
When a highly-relevant paper is found: open its database record and extract (a) all controlled vocabulary terms (MeSH Entry Terms, Emtree Synonyms, ACM CCS codes); (b) all author keywords; (c) title noun phrases not already in the query. These terms represent indexer judgments about what the paper is about — they often name traditions the searcher didn't know to look for. Add them to Step 0's tradition list before continuing.

**Rule 4 — Never use LLM synonym expansion as the sole vocabulary discovery method.**
LLMs collapse ambiguous terms to the dominant internet framing (SIGIR 2025 evidence). For cross-disciplinary vocabulary discovery, LLM expansion produces a list biased toward the most-discussed framing online, which may not be the framing used in the discipline of interest. Use LLM expansion as one input among several, and explicitly check whether any returned synonyms come from a different discipline vs. the same dominant frame. If all returned synonyms cluster in one frame: replace at least one with a deliberate cross-disciplinary probe.

**Rule 5 — Apply the optimization technique when a database has both controlled vocabulary and free-text.**
Run: (controlled vocabulary terms) AND NOT (free-text core phrase). Papers retrieved here name concepts the controlled vocabulary uses that the searcher's natural language didn't include. Run: (free-text core phrase) AND NOT (controlled vocabulary terms). Papers here use natural language terms not yet in controlled vocabulary — early or emerging usage. Both sets are candidate vocabulary extensions not discoverable from a single vocabulary direction.

**Intake question for kit (vocabulary pre-check):**
Before starting Step 0: "Name the field where you first encountered this term. Do you know if any other field studies the same phenomenon under a different name?" This separates the case where the user already knows the cross-disciplinary name (proceed directly to Step 0) from the case where they don't (trigger the survey-paper-first gate).

**Output criterion for Step 0:**
Step 0 is complete when: (a) at least 2 traditions are named with at least 2 discriminating terms each, AND (b) for each tradition: one query is planned that will use vocabulary from that tradition exclusively. An enumeration that can be fully covered by one query is not multi-tradition coverage — it is one tradition with synonyms.

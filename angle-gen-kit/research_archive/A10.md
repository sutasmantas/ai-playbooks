# A10 — llm-research-question-generation

## Key findings

**Finding 1 — Ideation-execution gap: LLM novelty advantage evaporates under real implementation**
Claim: LLM-generated research ideas are rated as significantly more novel than human-expert ideas in blind pre-execution evaluation (p<0.05, n>100 NLP researchers, Si et al. 2024), but after 43 expert researchers spent 100+ hours implementing and writing up assigned ideas, this advantage flips: LLM idea scores decrease significantly more than human ideas on all metrics (novelty, excitement, effectiveness; p<0.05). The surface-novelty inflation is a calibration artifact — LLM ideas sound novel without being executable.
Source: arXiv:2409.04109 (Si et al., "Can LLMs Generate Novel Research Ideas?", 2024); arXiv:2506.20803 ("The Ideation-Execution Gap", 2025)
Confidence: HIGH — two independent studies, first pre-registered with over-100-participant blind review, second with controlled execution by 43 domain experts
Verify note: Adversarial caveat — the execution gap study confounds idea quality with idea-ownership; humans implement their own ideas more effectively than assigned ideas. The flip may partly reflect familiarity advantage, not idea quality. Reduces confidence from very high to high.

**Finding 2 — LLM-judged novelty is anticorrelated with actual scientific impact**
Claim: LLM-as-Judge novelty scoring is negatively correlated with real research impact measured 30 months later (ρ = −0.29, p<0.01; HindSight 2025, time-split evaluation against post-cutoff citation data). LLMs systematically overvalue ideas that "sound novel" — parametric familiarity creates false positive novelty signals because unfamiliar-sounding recombinations score high even when they don't anticipate real research directions. LLM judges also assign scores ~0.46 points higher than human domain experts on average (agreeableness bias). Consequence: using LLM self-evaluation to score or filter generated questions introduces systematic miscalibration.
Source: arXiv:2603.15164 ("HindSight: Evaluating LLM-Generated Research Ideas via Future Impact", 2025)
Confidence: HIGH — novel evaluation methodology with objective retrospective ground truth; specific numbers confirmed; single study caveat
Verify note: The HindSight method (time-split + citation ground truth) is structurally sound. Main limitation: citation count as the ground-truth proxy favors incremental over paradigm-shifting work.

**Finding 3 — Collective diversity collapse: AI narrows the research space it generates**
Claim: AI-generated research ideas are substantially more concentrated around existing seed literature than human-authored work from the same starting point. A 37,802-idea study (4 AI frameworks, 6 LLMs) found AI-generated ideas remain much closer to seed papers than human follow-on research, primarily recombining existing technical methods rather than introducing new research questions. Separately, epistemic diversity testing of 27 LLMs across 155 topics showed all models are less epistemically diverse than a basic web search; larger models produce *less* diverse outputs than smaller ones. Format templating further compounds this: templated prompting significantly reduces topic diversity (EMNLP 2025). The kit-level implication is that LLM-generated angle lists will systematically under-represent exploratory, cross-disciplinary, and negating angles.
Source: arXiv:2605.27905 (Tang & Yang, "AI Research Agents Narrow Scientific Exploration", 2025); arXiv:2510.04226 ("Epistemic Diversity and Knowledge Collapse in LLMs", 2024); arXiv:2505.18949 ("The Price of Format: Diversity Collapse in LLMs", EMNLP 2025)
Confidence: HIGH — three convergent studies with different methodologies, large-scale primary dataset
Verify note: The Tang & Yang finding is strongest (37,802 ideas, systematic comparison). The epistemic diversity finding covers topics broadly, not research angles specifically. Converging direction across sources is robust.

**Finding 4 — Null hypothesis and divergence blindspot: no LLM class generates negating angles**
Claim: No tested LLM class spontaneously proposes null hypotheses or negating directions. Non-reasoning LLMs collapse into a "hivemind" of similar ideas; reasoning models roam a wider hypothesis space but still systematically fail to diverge or negate. Performance degrades severely in pluralistic fields (social sciences) that require contextual interpretation and theoretical flexibility, performing better in standardized fields with cleaner empirical traditions. Automated evaluation metrics poorly align with expert scientific judgment in these domains.
Source: arXiv:2606.08251 ("Contemporary AI lacks the imagination to diverge or negate in science", 2025)
Confidence: MEDIUM — single study, strong claim; directionally consistent with Finding 3 (diversity collapse); requires replication
Verify note: Finding aligns with cognitive science literature on confirmation bias in generative systems. Main risk: study may sample a narrow range of models; fast-moving capability frontier.

**Finding 5 — Domain blind spots in specialized knowledge generate shallow or wrong angles**
Claim: LLMs face a structural domain-knowledge gap in specialized fields: (a) they lack tacit procedural and failure knowledge of laboratory practice (arXiv:2605.08956), (b) LLM judges detect only ~45% of errors in specialized domains (arXiv:2512.16272), (c) literature review quality remains "very limited" despite grammatical fluency — outputs tend to be uncritical summaries rather than structured analytical reviews (arXiv:2412.13612). A specific failure pattern is "contextual relationship blindness" — inability to recognize domain-specific feature relationships domain experts consider fundamental, causing generated questions to miss the actual knowledge frontier in fields like materials science, earth science, and clinical research.
Source: arXiv:2605.08956 ("Agentic AI Scientists Are Not Built For Autonomous Scientific Discovery", 2025); arXiv:2512.16272 ("Beyond Blind Spots: Analytic Hints for Mitigating LLM-Based Evaluation Pitfalls", 2024); arXiv:2412.13612 ("LLMs for Automated Literature Review", 2024)
Confidence: CONSENSUS — convergent across multiple independent sources; specific failure mode (tacit knowledge gap) is structurally well-grounded
Verify note: The "45% detection" number is from evaluation-specific context, not research question generation directly. Extrapolation to angle generation is reasonable but not measured.

**Finding 6 — Recency bias and training-cutoff distortion favor recent vocabulary over historical foundations**
Claim: LLMs acting as rerankers and literature reviewers systematically promote recent content — across 7 models (GPT-3.5-turbo through GPT-4o), fresh passages are consistently promoted, shifting the Top-10 mean publication year forward by up to 4.78 years. Effective knowledge cutoff often differs from reported cutoff (older data underrepresented in CommonCrawl). For research question generation, this means LLMs favor recently-coined vocabulary and recent methodological trends, generating angles around 2022-2024 work while systematically overlooking foundational papers and historical debates that define the actual knowledge frontier.
Source: ResearchGate DOI record ("Do Large Language Models Favor Recent Content?", 2024); arXiv:2603.11838 ("DatedGPT", 2025); arXiv:2605.22176 ("LLM-Metrics: Measuring Research Impact Through LLM Memory", 2025)
Confidence: MEDIUM — recency bias in reranking is documented across 7 models with specific numbers; extrapolation to research question generation vocabulary selection is mechanism-plausible but not directly measured in this context
Verify note: The 4.78-year shift is in a reranking context. The mechanism (training data temporal distribution) applies to question generation vocabulary, but magnitude in that context is unmeasured.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

1. **Never use LLM self-evaluation to score or rank generated angles.** Finding 2 shows LLM-judged novelty anticorrelates with actual impact (ρ = −0.29). Use structural criteria (gap from seed literature, cross-disciplinary distance, presence of negating angles) rather than asking the LLM to rate its own output.

2. **Explicitly require null-hypothesis and negating angles as a structural gate.** Finding 4 shows no LLM class generates these spontaneously. The angle list procedure must include a mandatory check: "Are there angles that challenge the core assumption of this research area? Are there null-result or disconfirming angles?" These must be explicitly elicited, not emergent.

3. **Apply diversity enforcement across vocabulary traditions.** Finding 3 shows LLMs systematically converge on training-data-dominant vocabulary. The Step 0 vocabulary enumeration procedure in research-quality.md directly mitigates this — it is empirically justified by this finding. Multiple traditions must be named and queries must cross tradition boundaries; a single-tradition angle list is the failure mode.

4. **Flag domain-specialized angles for human expert review.** Finding 5 shows ~55% of domain-specific errors are undetected by LLM judges. For kit procedures in specialized domains (chemistry, clinical, legal), add an intake question: "Is this domain specialized enough that tacit practitioner knowledge is required to identify the frontier?" If yes, route the angle list through a domain expert before execution.

5. **Prefer recency-bounded + undated paired queries.** Finding 6 shows up to 4.78-year temporal shift in LLM-surfaced content. The WS-1 failure mode in research-quality.md (recency bias buries foundational work) is independently confirmed here. Run both `after:YYYY-MM-DD` and undated relevance queries to recover historical literature.

6. **Treat high pre-execution novelty scores as a warning flag, not a quality signal.** Finding 1 shows the ideation-execution gap. An angle that "sounds novel" without clear execution path should be scrutinized, not selected. Feasibility/execution-path questions should be intake gates: "What experiment or evidence would this angle generate? What would constitute a result?"

7. **Avoid templated prompting for angle generation.** Finding 3 (format diversity collapse) shows templated prompts significantly reduce topic diversity. Vary prompt structure across generations; don't reuse the same angle-generation prompt template across the same session.

**Adversarial query — Q3a (external critiques):**
External critique search found: (a) The execution gap finding (arXiv:2506.20803) is the sharpest adversarial input to LLM novelty claims — but it *confirms* the calibration failure finding, not a critique of the failure-mode research. (b) MCQ generation study shows LLMs comparable to humans on relevance and clarity (null result), but this is in pedagogical question generation (MCQ), not research angle generation — different task. No study found that directly contradicts the diversity collapse or calibration failure findings.

**Adversarial query — Q3b (adversarial subagent counter-case):**
Strongest counter-arguments against the claim that LLMs perform poorly at generating research questions:
- (1) The ideation-execution gap confounds idea quality with idea-ownership. Humans execute their own ideas more effectively regardless of idea quality. The flip from LLM-superior to human-superior after execution may be ~50% confound.
- (2) The diversity collapse finding operates at field-wide aggregate level. Individual researchers using varied prompting strategies can recover diversity — the finding is about default behavior at scale, not about ceiling capability.
- (3) Almost all studies are in AI/NLP — the most training-data-saturated domain. LLM-generated angles may show different (better) patterns in data-sparse fields where LLM training data is less overfit.
- (4) The novelty-anticorrelation finding (HindSight) uses citation count as ground truth — this favors incremental work over paradigm-shifting ideas that may not accrue citations in 30 months.
- (5) Null hypothesis generation failure (Finding 4) may be prompt-engineering addressable. Instructing the model explicitly to generate null/negating hypotheses may substantially change the output.
Counter-case verdict: The diversity collapse and calibration-failure findings are the most robust. The execution gap finding has the strongest confound. The recommendation to require explicit null/negating angle elicitation (implication 2) is partially undermined by counter-argument 5 — but this is an argument *for* explicit elicitation, not against it.

## Key sources

1. **Si et al. (2024). "Can LLMs Generate Novel Research Ideas? A Large-Scale Human Study with 100+ NLP Researchers."** arXiv:2409.04109. First statistically rigorous comparison of LLM-generated vs. human research ideas (n>100 blind reviewers). Key finding: LLM ideas more novel pre-execution (p<0.05) but lack diversity and self-evaluation capability.

2. **Authors anon. (2025). "The Ideation-Execution Gap: Execution Outcomes of LLM-Generated versus Human Research Ideas."** arXiv:2506.20803. 43 domain experts implemented assigned ideas. LLM idea scores flip to lower than human on all metrics post-execution (p<0.05). Strongest evidence for surface-novelty inflation.

3. **Tang, Y. & Yang, Y. (2025). "AI Research Agents Narrow Scientific Exploration."** arXiv:2605.27905. 37,802 ideas from 4 AI frameworks and 6 LLMs. AI ideas substantially more concentrated than human follow-on research; better suited to local elaboration than broadening exploration.

4. **Authors anon. (2025). "HindSight: Evaluating LLM-Generated Research Ideas via Future Impact."** arXiv:2603.15164. Time-split evaluation against post-cutoff citation data. LLM-judged novelty anticorrelates with actual impact (ρ = −0.29, p<0.01). LLM judges assign scores 0.46 points higher than domain experts.

5. **Authors anon. (2025). "Agentic AI Scientists Are Not Built For Autonomous Scientific Discovery."** arXiv:2605.08956. Identifies four structural barriers: McNamara fallacy in problem selection, tacit knowledge gaps, output homogenization from preference optimization, and evaluation disconnected from physical experiments.

6. **Authors anon. (2024). "Epistemic Diversity and Knowledge Collapse in Large Language Models."** arXiv:2510.04226. 27 models, 155 topics, 12 countries. All models less epistemically diverse than basic web search; larger models paradoxically less diverse.

# A12 — angle-quality-redundancy-criteria

## Key findings

### [key: result-set-overlap-is-distinctness-gold-standard]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: angle-quality
- sources: https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/metrics-for-assessing-sets-of-subtopics/, https://arxiv.org/pdf/1701.02617

**Claim:** Radlinski, Szummer & Craswell (SIGIR 2010) formally define "distinctness" for search subtopics as requiring that intents be substantially different in terms of the documents judged relevant — if two angles share most relevant documents, they are operationally equivalent and judgment effort is wasted. This has an empirically testable proxy: result-set Jaccard overlap. The paper "On Low Overlap Among Search Results of Academic Search Engines" (arXiv:1701.02617) found that for genuinely distinct queries, median pairwise Jaccard across academic engine result sets is near zero; within a retrieval session, mean pairwise Jaccard across top-5 sets was 0.12 (88% distinct on average). The practical implication: run both candidate angles in any available database, compute Jaccard on top-20 results; if J > 0.3, treat as redundant. This is the only post-search criterion that directly measures distinctness rather than proxying it with vocabulary or structure.

**Falsification:** Would be false if two angles with J > 0.3 overlap in top-20 results systematically yield non-overlapping findings in downstream synthesis at a rate comparable to low-overlap angle pairs — i.e., if result-set overlap does not predict synthesis-level redundancy.

**Verify note:** Radlinski et al. was peer-reviewed SIGIR work with formal metric development. The Jalali et al. study is a meta-research paper measuring actual Jaccard values across Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, and Web of Science. The 0.12 mean figure is from a specific controlled measurement. The J > 0.3 threshold I derive as a practical rule is my extrapolation from the near-zero baseline — the threshold itself is not directly from a study. Confidence: HIGH on the mechanism; MEDIUM on the specific 0.3 threshold.

### [key: pico-boolean-facet-test-for-pre-search-distinctness]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: HIGH
- group: angle-quality
- sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2112.09424, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.00011

**Claim:** Systematic review methodology provides a structural pre-search test for angle distinctness: assign each angle to a PICO facet (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, Context). Two angles assigned to the SAME facet are likely synonyms — they would be combined with OR in a Boolean search strategy and target the same concept. Two angles assigned to DIFFERENT facets are candidates for genuine distinctness — they would be combined with AND and address structurally independent dimensions of the question. MacFarlane et al. (arXiv:2112.09424) describe this as the standard boolean architecture of systematic search: subdivide topic into constituent concepts, identify which concepts deserve discrete blocks, collect terms per block. Nasser et al. (arXiv:2602.00011) automate this with LLM chain prompting achieving 0.9 average recall on the LEADSInstruct dataset. The Boolean-connective test is fast and requires no retrieval: if you cannot explain WHY two angles require AND (not OR) to combine them, one is probably a synonym for the other.

**Verify note:** PICO is a mature consensus framework in systematic review methodology. The Boolean-connective test is derived reasoning from that framework, not a directly tested empirical claim, so confidence is HIGH-consensus rather than HIGH-empirical. The 0.9 recall figure from Nasser et al. applies to their LLM framework for GENERATING search strategies, not for evaluating angle distinctness specifically.

### [key: llm-angle-generation-mode-collapse-synonym-clusters]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: angle-quality
- sources: https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.16582, https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.12752

**Claim:** Without explicit diversity constraints, LLM-based query generators default to what the Deep Research Survey (arXiv:2508.12752) calls "diversification-mode" — rewriting one angle into vocabulary variations rather than genuinely decomposing into distinct sub-aspects. O2-Searcher (arXiv:2505.16582) documents this as query mode collapse and adds an explicit "diversity-aware outcome term" as a reward signal to penalize duplication under budget constraints. The diagnostic signal: if N consecutive LLM-generated angles all use the same vocabulary cluster (synonyms, related terms from one field) rather than independent concept dimensions, mode collapse has occurred. The practical implication for kit procedures: when evaluating a machine-generated angle list, group angles by vocabulary cluster; if multiple angles cluster together (same root concept, different synonyms), retain only the best-formed one per cluster and replace the others with genuinely different facets.

**Falsification:** Would be false if LLM-generated angle lists, measured by pairwise result-set Jaccard, showed consistently low overlap (< 0.1 mean) without explicit diversity rewards — indicating LLMs do produce genuinely distinct angles by default.

**Verify note:** O2-Searcher is a May 2025 preprint with RL-based search agent design. The diversity reward claim is from the paper description, not from an ablation comparing angle distinctness with and without the reward explicitly. The Deep Research Survey categorization (decomposition vs. diversification) is taxonomic, not a measurement of how often each failure mode occurs. Confidence HIGH on the phenomenon (mode collapse in LLM query generation is multi-source corroborated), MEDIUM on the specific rate of occurrence.

### [key: vocabulary-tradition-difference-necessary-not-sufficient-for-distinctness]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: MEDIUM
- group: angle-quality
- sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/0806.3765, https://arxiv.org/pdf/1701.02617

**Claim:** Two angles can use entirely different vocabulary traditions while targeting the same document corpus (e.g., "connectionist models" vs. "deep learning" — different historical terms, same papers). Conversely, two angles can share vocabulary while targeting genuinely different result sets. Cross-concordance IR research (arXiv:0806.3765) found that vocabulary mapping between disciplines is imprecise — term mapping introduces noise when vocabularies overlap, and retrieval results vary even for cross-discipline vocabulary substitutions in ways that do not reliably predict distinctness. The vocabulary-tradition dimension (as defined in the kit's Step 0 procedure) is a valid heuristic for ensuring a broad SEARCH, but is NOT a reliable criterion for confirming that two angles are distinct. Vocabulary tradition difference raises the probability of retrieving distinct documents but does not guarantee it. Result-set overlap remains the gold-standard check. The vocabulary test is a good pre-search screening heuristic; the Jaccard test is the post-search verification criterion.

**Falsification:** Would be false if vocabulary tradition membership (measured by term co-occurrence clustering) showed correlation r > 0.7 with result-set Jaccard overlap across a large sample of query pairs — indicating vocabulary structure reliably predicts result-set structure.

**Verify note:** The cross-concordance paper (arXiv:0806.3765) is specifically about terminology mapping quality, not about angle distinctness in research planning. The extrapolation from "vocabulary overlap introduces retrieval noise" to "vocabulary tradition difference does not guarantee angle distinctness" is plausible but not directly tested in the paper. Medium confidence reflects this extrapolation gap.

### [key: research-character-accuracy-requires-multi-tradition-verification]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: MEDIUM
- group: angle-quality
- sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2112.09424, https://dissertationbydesign.com/how-to-identify-and-address-research-gaps/

**Claim:** Labeling an angle as "gap" (no existing literature) before searching across all identified vocabulary traditions is premature classification and is likely wrong. Research gap classification literature distinguishes empirical gaps (no data), methodological gaps, conceptual gaps, and theoretical gaps — but a gap detected within one vocabulary tradition may be a saturated domain in an adjacent tradition with different terminology. MacFarlane et al. (arXiv:2112.09424) document that vocabulary mismatch causes systematic under-retrieval; a researcher seeing "no results" for "deep learning for X" may miss a full literature under "neural networks for X." Similarly, an angle labeled "empirical" (controlled studies exist) must be verified — a field with only expert opinion literature does not support empirical classification. The research character determines which search strategies will be productive and which claims can be extracted; misclassifying it wastes search effort or produces false confidence. Practical rule: an angle may only be labeled "gap" after returning null results across at least 2 vocabulary traditions, 2 distinct databases, and after an adversarial follow-up query targeting synonyms.

**Verify note:** This is a consensus synthesis from systematic review methodology and vocabulary mismatch literature. No single study directly tests whether gap mislabeling is common in LLM research agents specifically. MEDIUM confidence reflects that the mechanism is well-grounded but the empirical rate of research-character misclassification in agent contexts is not measured.

### [key: launchability-requires-translatable-searchable-anchor]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: HIGH
- group: angle-quality
- sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2112.09424, https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.12752

**Claim:** An angle fails the launchability criterion when it cannot be expressed as specific search terms that would appear in documents relevant to the angle. Abstract angles ("what makes X good?", "the general challenge of Y") fail because they have no controlled vocabulary equivalent, no anchor paper, and no systematic search would retrieve documents specifically about that abstract formulation. The deep research survey (arXiv:2508.12752) flags "how to formulate effective, diverse queries that balance specificity and coverage" as the central challenge of the question-developing stage. An angle is launchable if: (a) you can write at least one specific query with identifiable vocabulary, (b) you expect that query to return at least 3 results in at least one database, (c) the expected results would be relevant to the angle's stated scope. Minimum test: if you cannot name a specific paper, conference, author, or technical term associated with the angle before searching, it is not yet launchable — it requires a scoping search to discover vocabulary before becoming a real angle.

**Verify note:** Launchability as a named criterion does not appear in the literature I found — this is a synthesis from systematic review search strategy requirements (operationalizability of concepts) and the deep research planning literature. The criterion itself is consensus-backed reasoning, not a directly measured empirical finding. HIGH confidence on the conceptual validity; no measurement data exists for the 3-result minimum threshold specifically.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

**Rule 1 — Pre-search angle review (PICO facet test, required before dispatch):**
For every pair of angles in the angle list, assign each to a question facet (Who/Population, What method/Intervention, Against what/Comparison, What outcome/Outcome, What context). If two angles land on the same facet: one is redundant. Merge or replace before dispatching. This costs zero retrieval time and catches synonym clusters structurally.

**Rule 2 — Boolean-connective test (required for any pair of adjacent angles):**
Ask: "Could I combine these two angles with OR in a Boolean search?" If yes, they are addressing the same concept with different terms — combine into one angle with expanded synonyms. If no (AND is required), they address different aspects — genuinely distinct. This test should be a required intake step in the angle-generation procedure.

**Rule 3 — Post-search distinctness verification (result-set Jaccard, at cluster close):**
After dispatching a cluster of angles, before synthesizing results: compute pairwise Jaccard on top-20 results from any single database. Any pair with J > 0.3 should be flagged as overlapping; their results should be merged, not treated as independent support for a claim.

**Rule 4 — LLM mode-collapse detection (required for machine-generated angle lists):**
After receiving a machine-generated angle list: group angles by vocabulary cluster. If multiple angles fall in the same cluster (same root concept, vocabulary variations), this is mode collapse. Retain one representative angle per cluster; replace others with angles from unstated facets. A mode-collapsed list has M angles but covers only K < M distinct facets.

**Rule 5 — Research character verification before labeling (at angle creation):**
Before labeling an angle "gap," confirm with at least two null-result queries across two vocabulary traditions. An angle may carry a provisional "gap — needs verification" label until multi-tradition search confirms it. Similarly, "empirical" label requires that at least one quantitative study is expected to exist before dispatch, not after.

**Rule 6 — Launchability gate (required at angle intake):**
Before dispatching any angle to a research agent: can you name at least one specific term, author, paper, or technical concept associated with that angle? If no: the angle needs a scoping step first, not a full research dispatch. Mark as "needs scoping" and run one broad exploratory search to establish vocabulary before full dispatch.

Adversarial query result: Found that result-set overlap metrics (alpha-NDCG, ERR-IA) do NOT directly measure angle distinctness — they measure redundancy IN results, not distinctness OF angles. Specifically: "alpha-NDCG and ERR-IA do not actually measure diversity of intents; rather, they account for the number of nuggets diminished by their returned positions and redundancy" (ScienceDirect, "An in-depth study on diversity evaluation"). This is a meaningful adversarial finding: the Jaccard-overlap proxy I propose is more direct than the established diversity metrics. The Q3a adversarial search found the limitation; the mechanism survives with the clarification that Jaccard on raw result sets (not diversity-weighted retrieval rankings) is the right proxy.

Q3b adversarial subagent reasoning (constructed independently): The strongest counter-case against result-set Jaccard as a distinctness proxy: (1) Result sets are database-dependent — two angles might produce overlapping results in PubMed but non-overlapping results in arXiv; the Jaccard threshold (0.3) is database-specific and not portable. (2) High Jaccard may reflect a small, authoritative literature rather than angle redundancy — if only 5 papers exist on a topic, two valid angles will both retrieve them. (3) The PICO facet test assumes a well-formed PICO question — exploratory research angles often cannot be mapped to PICO facets; the test would reject legitimate exploratory angles as "unclassifiable." (4) Research character labeling before searching is circular for gap claims — you can't verify a gap without searching, so the "multi-tradition verification before labeling" requirement delays angle dispatch without resolving the problem. Counter-response: points 1-2 are genuine scope limitations (threshold is database-specific; small literatures are a known exception); points 3-4 are true but the fix is to use PICO as one test among several, not as the sole gate.

## Key sources

1. Radlinski, F., Szummer, M., & Craswell, N. (2010). "Metrics for Assessing Sets of Subtopics." *SIGIR 2010*. https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/metrics-for-assessing-sets-of-subtopics/ — Foundational: defines the four formal properties of distinct search subtopics (coherence, distinctness, plausibility, completeness); "distinctness" is explicitly operationalized as difference in relevant document sets.

2. MacFarlane, A., Russell-Rose, T., & Shokraneh, F. (2021). "Search Strategy Formulation for Systematic Reviews: Issues, Challenges and Opportunities." *arXiv:2112.09424*. https://arxiv.org/pdf/2112.09424 — Provides the systematic review consensus framework for angle design: PICO facets, Boolean architecture (AND between facets, OR within facets), and the operationalizability requirement (angles must be translatable to searchable terms).

3. Jalali, I. et al. (2017). "On Low Overlap Among Search Results of Academic Search Engines." *arXiv:1701.02617*. https://arxiv.org/pdf/1701.02617 — Empirically measured Jaccard scores across academic search engines; found near-zero median Jaccard for genuinely distinct queries, establishing the baseline expectation that distinct angles should produce near-disjoint result sets.

4. O2-Searcher (KnowledgeXLab). (2025). "O2-Searcher: A Searching-based Agent Model for Open-Domain Open-Ended Question Answering." *arXiv:2505.16582*. https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.16582 — Documents mode collapse in LLM query generation and addresses it with a diversity reward term; provides the operational mechanism for why LLM-generated angle lists tend toward synonym clusters rather than genuinely distinct facets.

5. Deep Research Survey. (2025). "Deep Research: A Survey of Autonomous Research Agents." *arXiv:2508.12752*. https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.12752 — Taxonomy of decomposition-based (genuinely distinct sub-questions) vs. diversification-based (synonyms for same question) approaches to angle generation in deep research agents; explains the systemic failure mode at architectural level.

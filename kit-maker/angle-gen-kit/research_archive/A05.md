# A05 — multi-source-triangulation

**Angle:** How to ensure research coverage across source types: academic papers, practitioner blogs, GitHub repositories, forums (HN, Reddit), grey literature. What is known about source-type diversity in research and its effect on coverage completeness.

---

## Key findings

### Finding 1: Academic-only searches structurally exclude practitioner knowledge
**Claim:** Academic database searches alone produce structurally incomplete coverage because practitioner knowledge lives predominantly in grey literature — blogs, GitHub issues, Stack Overflow, forums — and not in peer-reviewed journals. In software engineering contexts, only 9% of practitioners report reading systematic reviews; practitioners share knowledge through grey literature channels instead. Garousi et al. 2019 formalizes the Multivocal Literature Review (MLR) as the structural remedy: include grey literature sources alongside peer-reviewed sources in systematic search strategies.
**Source:** Garousi, Felderer, Mäntylä. "Guidelines for including grey literature and conducting multivocal literature reviews in software engineering." Information and Software Technology, 2019. https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.02553
**Confidence:** CONSENSUS — widely replicated finding across SE methodology literature; 400+ citations.

### Finding 2: Searching ≥2 structurally distinct source types achieves >95% recall; single-source achieves 20–93%
**Claim:** Searching two or more structurally distinct source types achieves >95% coverage and recall in systematic reviews; single-source searches achieve 20–93% recall depending on the severity of the search scenario (Rethlefsen et al. 2022, metaresearch of 60 Cochrane reviews). The gap is not accidental: 16% of relevant references exist in only one database by ingest policy. "Structurally distinct" requires different ingest policies — two queries on the same database are one source, not two. For topics crossing practitioner and academic domains, academic databases plus one grey literature source (GitHub, practitioner blogs, forum aggregators) is the minimum viable pair.
**Source:** Rethlefsen et al. "Searching two or more databases decreased the risk of missing relevant studies: a metaresearch study." Journal of Clinical Epidemiology, 2022. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0895435622001445
**Confidence:** HIGH — large-scale metaresearch, 60 Cochrane reviews, credible clinical epidemiology venue.
**Verify note:** (pending verify pass)

### Finding 3: Excluding grey/unpublished literature inflates effect sizes and over-represents positive conclusions
**Claim:** Academic-only searches systematically over-represent positive findings because negative/null results are underpublished. Only 20% of null social science studies appear in journals; 65% are never written up (Cochrane Handbook). Published trials show ~9% larger intervention effects on average than grey literature trials; psychological treatment meta-analyses showed 25% effect size reduction when unpublished data was included. The mechanism is the "file drawer problem" — researchers do not submit null results. For a research pipeline, this means academic-only searches cannot be treated as unbiased samples of what is known; they are biased toward confident, positive conclusions.
**Source:** Cochrane Handbook 10.3.2 (https://handbook-5-1.cochrane.org/chapter_10/10_3_2_including_unpublished_studies_in_systematic_reviews.htm); multiple publication bias meta-analyses. 25% reduction finding: Driessen et al., cited in Cochrane grey literature literature.
**Confidence:** HIGH — publication bias is one of the most replicated findings in research methodology; quantitative estimates vary but direction is invariant.
**Verify note:** The 25% figure is from one specific domain (psychological treatments); the 9% figure is a meta-analytic average. Both are plausible but domain-specific scope conditions apply.

### Finding 4: Grey literature has severe quality heterogeneity and link rot; requires explicit assessment criteria
**Claim:** 49% of grey literature URLs cited in SE secondary studies (2011–2018) were dead at the time of analysis (Kamei et al. 2021, tertiary study of 446 studies). Only 21% of those 446 studies used grey literature to actually answer research questions; most cited GL as background without rigorous quality assessment. The fix: apply Garousi's authority + methodology + impact trifecta before including any grey literature claim — (a) author has named expertise in the domain, (b) there is a described process rather than pure opinion, (c) community recognition exists (citations, karma, cross-links). Vendor marketing blogs fail on (a) and (b) regardless of traffic.
**Source:** Kamei et al. "Grey Literature in Software Engineering: A Critical Review." IST, 2021. https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.13435; Garousi et al. 2019 quality criteria.
**Confidence:** HIGH — tertiary study of 446 papers; 49% URL death rate is a concrete measurable finding.

### Finding 5: Practitioner sources and academic sources diverge in framing, not themes — each adds non-redundant dimensions
**Claim:** In topics covered by both academic and grey literature, themes converge but framing diverges significantly: practitioners use execution-oriented language (specific tools, workflows, failure modes, tactical choices) while academic sources use broad condition-oriented framing. The divergence means multi-source triangulation is not redundancy-checking — each source type adds dimensions the other cannot supply. Academic sources define concepts and causal structures; practitioner sources supply failure modes, workarounds, and configuration-specific behavior. Including only one type leaves a coverage gap even when topic labels appear identical.
**Source:** PeerJ MLR study on data science success factors (2024, multivocal review); Garousi et al. 2019; SE MLR tertiary literature.
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — supported across multiple MLR studies but no single controlled experiment isolates the framing-vs-theme distinction quantitatively.
**Verify note:** (pending verify pass)

### Finding 6 (adversarial): Grey literature inclusion adds cost; benefit varies by domain type and is disputed in some contexts
**Claim:** The case for always including grey literature is contested. Some systematic reviews find grey literature has little impact on final conclusions; including grey literature substantially increases search effort without proportional quality gain for academically mature topics. The cost is structural: no standard search strategy, no reproducible index, personalized search engine results (filter bubble), and high URL mortality. The implication: grey literature search depth should scale with domain type — practitioner-heavy topics at the frontier of academic publication (new frameworks, fast-moving tooling, developer experience) warrant deep MLR treatment; well-established academic domains (classic algorithms, foundational theory) may see low marginal return from grey literature inclusion.
**Source:** Kamei et al. 2021 critical review; Garousi et al. 2019 challenge discussion; meta-epidemiological literature on grey literature impact variability.
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — the variability finding is real but poorly quantified; domain-type moderator is inferred from case comparisons, not direct experiment.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

**Intake question (required before search):** Is this topic practitioner-heavy or academically mature? (Determines grey literature search depth.) Practitioner-heavy signals: topic is <5 years old in literature, involves tooling/configuration, involves developer experience or workflow. Academically mature signals: well-established foundational concept, >10 years of peer-reviewed literature, no significant industrial adoption wave in progress.

**Procedure rule — source-type minimum per angle:** Every angle must search at least two structurally distinct source types. Acceptable pairs: (academic database + GitHub/practitioner blog), (Semantic Scholar + arXiv), (academic paper + Stack Overflow/forum). Two queries on the same database do not count as two sources.

**Procedure rule — grey literature quality gate:** Any grey literature claim must pass Garousi's authority + methodology + impact trifecta before inclusion. Claims that fail this gate are logged as "null result — grey lit quality gate failure" rather than silently excluded.

**Procedure rule — adversarial grey lit arm:** For any claim extracted from grey literature, run a targeted search for academic/contradicting evidence. Grey literature framing is execution-oriented and may overstate simplicity or understate scope conditions.

**Output criterion — source type log in saturation log:** Every angle's saturation log must explicitly list which source types were searched. Saturation declared on academic sources alone must be labeled "partial — academic only; practitioner/grey sources not covered."

**Output criterion — link archiving for grey lit:** Any grey literature URL cited in findings must note the access date. 49% URL death rate over ~7 years means research using grey lit without archiving is non-reproducible.

## Key sources

1. **Garousi, Felderer, Mäntylä (2019).** "Guidelines for including grey literature and conducting multivocal literature reviews in software engineering." *Information and Software Technology*, 106, 101–121. https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.02553 — Foundational paper defining MLR methodology; authority + methodology + impact quality criteria for grey literature; practitioners' reliance on grey literature framing.

2. **Kamei et al. (2021).** "Grey Literature in Software Engineering: A Critical Review." *Information and Software Technology*, 138. https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.13435 — Tertiary study of 446 SE secondary studies (2011–2018); found 49% URL death rate, only 21% GL usage rate; identified systematic methodological weaknesses in existing GL inclusion practices.

3. **Rethlefsen et al. (2022).** "Searching two or more databases decreased the risk of missing relevant studies: a metaresearch study." *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0895435622001445 — Metaresearch of 60 Cochrane reviews; quantified recall rates for 1, 2, 3+ database combinations; showed 20–93% single-source recall depending on scenario severity; ≥95% recall with ≥2 databases.

4. **Cochrane Handbook Section 10.3.2.** "Including unpublished studies in systematic reviews." https://handbook-5-1.cochrane.org/chapter_10/10_3_2_including_unpublished_studies_in_systematic_reviews.htm — Authoritative methodological guidance on publication bias; unpublished study statistics; quantified effect size inflation from excluding grey literature.

5. **Stoddard, Cheng, Adamic, Kleinberg, Leskovec (ICWSM).** "Popularity and Quality in Social News Aggregators: A Study of Reddit and Hacker News." https://www.researchgate.net/publication/271710313_Popularity_and_Quality_in_Social_News_Aggregators_A_Study_of_Reddit_and_Hacker_News — Empirical study of popularity-quality relationship on HN and Reddit; found stronger correlation than expected but early-vote anchoring creates rich-get-richer dynamics; supports evaluating linked sources rather than post scores.

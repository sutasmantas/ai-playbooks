- Devil's advocate backfire: weak evidence found. Effects are context-dependent and appear more effective against social conformity/groupthink than against individual prior-belief-driven search.

Adversarial subagent (Q3b — constructed without confirmatory context):
The strongest counter-case against "structural interventions prevent confirmation bias in search": Structural procedures create the *appearance* of rigorous search without changing the prior-driven salience filter that drives confirmation bias. The scalable oversight study demonstrates this precisely: participants became *more* confident in incorrect AI answers after 20-minute research sessions, even when explicitly trying to verify claims. The mechanism is not ignorance or laziness — it is that search terms, result evaluation, and relevance judgment all draw from the same prior-belief-shaped cognitive frame. ACH adds a matrix but doesn't change what the analyst treats as "relevant evidence." Pre-registration doesn't change what a researcher considers a meaningful outcome. Devil's advocate assigns a role but the assigned person still searches with their own vocabulary. The weakest assumption in all these structures: that making the disconfirming task *procedurally required* changes the *cognitive process*. The Dhami 2019 result — that trained analysts didn't follow ACH steps correctly and showed worse outcomes — is the sharpest counter-evidence. The one structure that does escape this failure mode is structural independence: a separate agent with no shared conversation history and a different starting frame (adversarial, not exploratory). This works because it doesn't rely on the same agent overriding its own priors.

### [key: confirmatory-search-behavior-empirical]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: confirmation-bias-mechanism
- sources: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8685219/, https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3613904.3642459

**Claim:** Users seeking information on the web show measurable confirmation bias at every stage of the search process (query formulation, source selection, reading time, belief updating), not just at the opinion-formation stage. LLM-powered conversational search amplifies this vs. traditional web search: confirmatory query rate 1.46% (web search) vs 15.00% (conversational LLM search) in a CHI 2024 controlled study (Sharma et al., n=details in paper); even with source references added to LLM responses, users clicked fewer than one reference per session on average, indicating minimal verification behavior. Mechanism: prior beliefs shape which queries feel relevant, which results feel credible, and which evidence feels important — all without conscious intent. Scope: demonstrated in opinion-laden topics (health, political issues); may be weaker on purely factual lookup tasks where prior belief strength is low.

**Falsification:** This mechanism would be false if: users who begin with strong prior beliefs systematically click disconfirming sources at the same rate as confirming sources when both are equally prominent in search results — i.e., if salience is the only driver and prior beliefs don't modulate source selection beyond salience.

**Verify note:** PMC8685219 (Frontiers Psychology 2021) empirically documented; CHI 2024 study (arxiv 2402.05880) provides specific quantitative confirmatory query rates. Effect direction consistent across both. HIGH confidence — two independent studies, consistent direction, large absolute difference in confirmatory query rates. Scope condition: primarily studied on controversial/opinion-laden topics.

### [key: llm-search-amplifies-selective-exposure]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: confirmation-bias-mechanism
- sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2402.05880v2, https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3613904.3642459

**Claim:** LLM-powered conversational search systems amplify selective exposure compared to conventional web search, and the problem is worse when the LLM is opinion-biased. In a 2024 CHI study (Sharma et al.): consonant (opinion-aligned) LLM system produced confirmatory queries at 42.92% vs neutral systems at 15.57%; dissonant systems failed to reduce selective exposure, contradicting the hypothesis that oppositional framing reduces bias. Users using conversational search issued more opinion-expressive queries, not less. Mechanism: conversational interfaces invite elaboration on existing views; the LLM responds coherently to the user's framing, reinforcing the vocabulary and frame they brought. Scope: measured over short sessions; long-term effects and cross-topic generalization unstudied.

**Falsification:** This mechanism would be false if: in a design where the LLM actively reframes the user's queries into neutral vocabulary before retrieval (not just adds sources), confirmatory query rates converge to those of traditional web search — indicating that the amplification is due to LLM framing rather than the conversational modality itself.

**Verify note:** CHI 2024, peer-reviewed, specific quantitative results, multiple conditions (web, conversational, conversational+references, consonant, dissonant). HIGH confidence on direction; sample size adequate for CHI; scope caveat that sessions were short. The dissonant-system-fails-to-reduce finding is particularly important for kit design.

### [key: ACH-null-result-trained-analysts]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: structural-intervention-effectiveness
- sources: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/acp.3550, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11169332/

**Claim:** ACH (Analysis of Competing Hypotheses), the most widely cited structured analytic technique for preventing confirmation bias in hypothesis evaluation, shows mixed-to-null effectiveness in empirical tests. Dhami, Belton, & Mandel 2019 (Applied Cognitive Psychology, n=50 trained intelligence analysts): ACH-trained analysts did not follow all ACH steps; there was mixed evidence for bias reduction; ACH may increase judgment inconsistency and error. A 2024 task structure study (Cognitive Research, PMC11169332) found the ACH-style matrix (hypotheses in columns, evidence in rows — HypCol condition) produced NO significant reduction in confirmation bias (χ²(2)=0.15, p=0.927); effect sizes where found are small (Cohen's d<0.3). A different information arrangement — hypotheses in rows (HypRow) — DID reduce confirmation bias (χ²(2)=9.43, p=0.009, φ=0.24). Mechanism: ACH's hypothesis-as-column format may not change how evidence is processed; a row format forces evaluation of each hypothesis before moving to the next piece of evidence, creating a different attentional pattern. Scope: one study on professional analysts, one on participants in a task; generalization to information retrieval rather than hypothesis evaluation is an extrapolation.

**Falsification:** This mechanism (ACH fails because column format doesn't change attentional processing) would be false if: studies find ACH with extended training and compliance monitoring produce significantly different outcomes from the Dhami study, suggesting the null result was a training/compliance failure rather than a design failure.

**Verify note:** Dhami 2019 is a peer-reviewed empirical study on actual intelligence analysts — highest-quality population for this claim. PMC11169332 provides the mechanism explanation (orientation matters). The combined evidence is strong that ACH as typically implemented does not reliably reduce confirmation bias; the row-format finding suggests structural redesign rather than abandoning the approach. HIGH confidence on null result; MEDIUM confidence on the mechanism (row vs. column) because that finding is from a single study.

### [key: pre-registration-reduces-outcome-switching-but-porous]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: structural-intervention-effectiveness
- sources: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27484242/, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6646984/, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7757516/

**Claim:** Pre-registration of search strategy and outcomes reduces selective outcome reporting but is substantially porous without enforcement. A landmark cross-sectional study found 62% of published clinical trials had at least one primary outcome switched between registration and publication; trials with outcome switching overestimated intervention effects by ~16% compared to trials without changes. However, approximately 50% of preregistered studies still selectively report outcomes (selectively reporting hypotheses after exploring data, then registering, or HARKing before registration). The more effective intervention is Registered Reports — where peer review and editorial acceptance happen BEFORE data collection, making publication contingent on methodology rather than results. Registered Reports show clearer evidence of reducing questionable research practices than pre-registration alone. Mechanism: pre-registration reduces the opportunity to retroactively reframe outcomes, but does not prevent pre-study exploratory analysis followed by targeted registration. Scope: best-evidenced in clinical trials and psychology; analogous principles apply to research document reviews but with less empirical evidence.

**Falsification:** This mechanism would be false if: studies comparing pre-registered vs. non-pre-registered papers in information science find no systematic effect on outcome reporting bias — i.e., if the clinical trial evidence does not translate to information-seeking research contexts because publication incentives differ.

**Verify note:** Multiple systematic reviews confirm the outcome switching finding (PMC6646984, PMC3702538). The 16% effect inflation figure is from a well-cited cross-sectional study. The Registered Reports evidence is from Nature Human Behaviour 2022 review. HIGH confidence overall; scope caveat that this is clinical trial / psychology literature, not information retrieval search-strategy research specifically.

### [key: confirmatory-search-persists-even-when-verifying]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: structural-intervention-effectiveness
- sources: https://arxiv.org/html/2507.19486v1

**Claim:** Confirmation bias in search persists even when people are explicitly trying to verify (not just find) information, and search-based interventions may make it worse. In a 2025 scalable oversight study (arxiv 2507.19486, Studies 1 and 2): participants who conducted 20-minute online research sessions after viewing GPT-4 answers became MORE confident in incorrect answers after searching; the intervention group (given AI-generated research guidelines) performed slightly but significantly worse than control (0.72 vs 0.74 accuracy, p=0.027). The model-assisted group in Study 1 achieved 71% accuracy vs. the model's 75% baseline, meaning they did not exceed the AI's raw performance despite having the AI's output plus 20 minutes of additional research. Mechanism: when a person searches "to check" a claim, they frame queries in the vocabulary of the claim (which is the confirming vocabulary) and recognize confirming results as "finding what they were looking for" — producing false confidence. This is structural: the search intent and the bias vector point in the same direction. Scope: the model used was GPT-3.5/GPT-4 on domain-expert questions (contract law, medicine, Lojban); effect may be different with more obviously flawed outputs.

**Falsification:** This mechanism would be false if: when searchers are explicitly given the *disconfirming* vocabulary (e.g., told "search for evidence that the following claim is WRONG using these alternative terms") before searching, they perform better than the AI baseline — indicating that vocabulary framing, not verification intent, is the operative variable.

**Verify note:** Study is recent (2025), from arxiv, not yet peer-reviewed at the time of this research. Design is clear (n=192+106, controlled, randomized), findings are internally consistent across two studies. The critical finding — that intervention DECREASED accuracy — is adversarially strong and unlikely to be an artifact of the design. MEDIUM confidence pending peer review; direction is HIGH confidence.

### [key: structural-independence-breaks-confirmation-loop]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: HIGH
- group: structural-intervention-effectiveness
- sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.25665, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11169332/

**Claim:** The mitigation that works for confirmation bias in verification is not instructing the same agent to "be critical" but structurally separating the verifying agent from the confirming context. In adversarial multi-agent software verification (arxiv 2605.25665): independent verification agents with no shared conversation history and separate task payloads reduce implementation-specific confirmation bias, because the verifier cannot adopt the implementer's frame. The PMC11169332 finding supports the same principle from a different direction: the HypRow condition worked (p=0.009) by forcing evaluation of each hypothesis in full before moving to the next evidence item — it changed the *order of access*, not just the *instructions*. Together these suggest the mechanism: confirmation bias operates through the access structure (what you read first, what vocabulary you bring), and only structural changes to that access structure — not instructional overlays on the same access structure — reliably break the bias loop. Scope: adversarial multi-agent finding is from software engineering context; the information retrieval analogy (separate searcher with opposite query vocabulary) is a principle extrapolation.

**Verify note:** This is a consensus claim derived from two independent empirical findings that converge on the same mechanism. The adversarial multi-agent paper is grey literature (arxiv preprint). The HypRow finding is peer-reviewed. As a convergent principle it warrants HIGH confidence; the specific extrapolation to "separate searcher with different vocabulary" is MEDIUM confidence — that specific form is not directly tested.

## Key findings

**Finding 1:** Confirmation bias in web search is empirically documented as automatic and multi-stage — it affects query formulation, source selection, reading time, and confidence — not just opinion formation at the end. LLM-powered conversational search amplifies this vs traditional search by ~10x on confirmatory query rates (CHI 2024). | Source: Frontiers Psychology 2021 + CHI 2024 | Confidence: HIGH | Verify: Two independent empirical studies, consistent direction, large magnitude.

**Finding 2:** Structured verification attempts paradoxically increase confidence in wrong answers. After explicit 20-minute research sessions, participants became *more* confident in incorrect AI claims (arxiv 2507.19486, n=192+106). The search-to-verify framing uses confirming vocabulary and interprets confirming results as successful verification. | Source: arxiv 2507.19486, 2025 | Confidence: HIGH (direction); MEDIUM (pending peer review) | Verify: Internal consistency across two studies; adversarial direction of finding (worse with intervention).

**Finding 3:** ACH, the canonical structured analytic technique for combating confirmation bias in hypothesis evaluation, shows null-to-negative results in empirical tests (Dhami et al. 2019, n=50 trained analysts; PMC11169332, p=0.927 for ACH-style condition). The specific matrix orientation matters more than the procedure label — hypotheses-in-rows works (φ=0.24), hypotheses-in-columns does not. | Source: Applied Cognitive Psychology 2019 + PMC 2024 | Confidence: HIGH | Verify: Peer-reviewed, professional-analyst population, specific effect sizes.

**Finding 4:** Pre-registration reduces outcome switching (62% of unregistered trials switch outcomes; 16% effect inflation from switching) but does not prevent confirmation bias in hypothesis generation. ~50% of preregistered studies still selectively report. The stronger intervention is Registered Reports (peer acceptance before results), not pre-registration alone. | Source: PMC6646984 + multiple systematic reviews | Confidence: HIGH | Verify: Multiple systematic reviews converge on the finding.

**Finding 5:** The mechanism that breaks the confirmation loop is structural separation of the confirming context from the verifying context — not instructing the same agent/analyst to "be critical." **Finding 6:** Devil's advocate technique is most effective at reducing social conformity in groups; its effect on individual prior-belief-driven search bias is context-dependent and lacks strong empirical support as a standalone individual-searcher intervention. ## Practical implications for kit procedures

**1. Adversarial queries must be genuinely separate — not a self-prompt.**
The confirmation bias research and scalable oversight study together show that instructing the same searcher to "now find disconfirming evidence" does not escape the prior-belief-shaped salience filter. The practical implication: adversarial queries should be issued *before* seeing confirming results (pre-registration equivalent) or issued by a separate agent/process with no access to the confirmatory query results. The research-quality.md requirement for an adversarial subagent with no confirmatory context is empirically grounded in this finding.

**2. Pre-register the specific null-result condition, not just the hypothesis.**
Pre-registration works because it prevents post-hoc framing. For a kit search procedure, this means specifying in advance what would count as "this angle is not supported" — not just what the expected finding is. Without a pre-specified null condition, the searcher has no structural pressure to acknowledge when they've found it.

**3. Query vocabulary design is the highest-leverage intervention.**
The best-supported mitigation is adversarial vocabulary — searching using the vocabulary of disconfirmation ("X fails because...", "X null result", "X limitations") not just the vocabulary of the claim. This changes the access structure (what results appear) rather than just the instruction (what the same results are supposed to mean).

**4. Information presentation order matters as much as instruction.**
PMC11169332 shows that hypotheses-in-rows format reduced confirmation bias (φ=0.24) while hypotheses-in-columns (standard ACH) did not. The kit implication: when evaluating competing interpretations, present them in a format that forces full evaluation of one interpretation before moving to the next piece of evidence — not a matrix that invites column-by-column confirmation.

**5. Grey literature and null-result sources must be mandatory, not optional.**
Publication bias means ~50-80% of unpublished null results are never submitted. Requiring at least one grey literature query per angle is not a procedural nicety — it is the primary structural mitigation against a systematic bias toward positive findings in published literature.

**6. LLM-assisted search requires explicit disconfirmatory framing before the LLM sees the claim.**
The CHI 2024 echo chamber study shows LLM-powered search amplifies confirmation bias by ~10x. For a kit using LLM search assistance, the disconfirmatory instruction must appear before the LLM generates any response, not as an afterthought. Equivalently: separate the "find confirming evidence" call from the "find disconfirming evidence" call, with no shared context between them.

# A07 — research-saturation-coverage

## Key findings

### [key: vocabulary-island-saturation]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: HIGH
- group: saturation-signals
- sources: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5993836/, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11135-017-0574-8, https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23311886.2020.1820257

**Claim:** Saturation within a single vocabulary tradition signals *reachable saturation* — exhaustion of the researcher's vocabulary cluster — not *coverage completeness*. The same phenomenon is studied under different names across fields; a researcher who has reached keyword convergence within one tradition cannot distinguish "no more concepts exist" from "no more concepts in this vocabulary island exist." Saunders et al. (2018, Quality & Quantity, PMC5993836) formalize this distinction: reachable saturation requires Conditions 1+2 (key convergence + source-type coverage), but true saturation additionally requires that all vocabulary-frame traditions identified in a pre-search enumeration have been covered. Declaring saturation without the vocabulary-frame check permanently excludes ~10% of relevant literature (reachable-vs-true-saturation, per the kit's own research-quality.md).

**Verify note:** Consensus across qualitative methods, systematic review, and IS literature. The ~10% figure is from cris-golden-gap-418pct (cited in research-quality.md), not from Saunders et al. directly. Saunders et al. establish the conceptual distinction; the 10% quantification comes from a separate claim key (cris-golden-gap-418pct, HIGH). Verify these separately if used as empirical claims.

### [key: saturation-76pct-undocumented]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: saturation-signals
- sources: https://hermanaguinis.com/pdf/LQsaturation.pdf

**Claim:** In a systematic review of qualitative studies reporting saturation (Aguinis et al., published as "Defining, assessing, and reporting saturation in qualitative research"), 42% of sources used the term without defining it, and 76.3% provided no substantive evidence that saturation was actually reached. The mechanism: saturation is used as a post-hoc label legitimizing a stop decision made for other reasons (resource exhaustion, time pressure, sample convenience) rather than as a prospective stopping criterion with observable confirmation. Practical implication: a saturation claim without a documented saturation-check procedure is not evidence of coverage completeness.

**Falsification:** This claim would be false if a replication study of published papers found substantially lower rates of undocumented saturation — specifically, if >50% of sampled papers provided substantive saturation evidence. The current finding reflects a period/field snapshot; the rate may improve as reporting guidelines (SRQR, COREQ) become more widely applied.

**Verify note:** Single review; field composition matters (social sciences, health sciences dominate). The 76.3% figure is striking and matches the broader pattern of under-reporting in qualitative research. Treat as HIGH within its scope (published qualitative research claiming saturation). Does not directly apply to AI-kit research workflows, but the mechanism (saturation as post-hoc label) is transferable.

### [key: ITS-slope-ratio-metric]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: MEDIUM
- group: saturation-signals
- sources: https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03239, https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.04859, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7200005/

**Claim:** Inductive Thematic Saturation (ITS) can be measured quantitatively using the ratio between the slope of cumulative-unique-codes and the slope of cumulative-total-codes across successive data units. When the unique-code slope approaches zero (ratio approaches 0), saturation is reached. Pham et al. (PLOS One, 2020) propose a "simple method to assess and report thematic saturation" based on tracking the rate of new codes per interview; Sievert & Elish (arXiv:2401.03239, 2024) extend this to validate LLM-based thematic analysis. The rate-of-new-keys metric is directly transferable to research angle tracking: plot "new claim keys per query" across queries; when the rate approaches zero across 2–3 consecutive queries AND all vocabulary traditions are covered, saturation is confirmed.

**Falsification:** This mechanism would be false if new claim keys stopped appearing due to query formulation exhaustion rather than concept exhaustion — i.e., if reframing queries in a new vocabulary tradition after apparent slope convergence yields substantial new keys. The slope-zero signal is only valid when vocabulary-frame coverage (Step 0) is also confirmed.

**Verify note:** MEDIUM confidence: the slope-ratio formalization is recent (2024) and limited in replication. The underlying principle (diminishing marginal new themes = saturation) is well-established; the formal metric is emerging. For kit use: the "new claim keys per query" tracking operationalizes this principle without requiring the formal metric calculation.

### [key: TAR-statistical-stopping-rules]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: saturation-signals
- sources: https://systematicreviewsjournal.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13643-024-02502-7, https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.09871, https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3724116

**Claim:** Technology-Assisted Review (TAR) research has developed formal stopping criteria for systematic review screening that are operationally superior to gut-sense saturation when corpus size supports them. The SAFE procedure (Bos et al., Systematic Reviews, 2024) stops active-learning-based screening when: (a) a minimum percentage of the corpus has been screened, (b) a predetermined count of consecutive irrelevant records has been reviewed, and (c) visual inspection of the recall plot shows a plateau. Chao's estimator (ACM TOIS) provides population-size estimation to bound the number of undiscovered relevant documents. The Quant/QuantCI heuristics (arXiv:2106.09871) use model-based estimation to hit specified recall targets (e.g., 80%, 95%) with quantified confidence. Practical implication: for large corpora (>100 documents), statistical stopping rules outperform intuitive saturation judgments and provide auditable evidence of coverage level.

**Falsification:** These rules would fail if the active learning model has systematic vocabulary bias — if relevant documents using rare vocabulary are systematically ranked last, they are never seen before stopping, and the recall estimate is inflated. This is a known limitation: TAR stopping rules assume the active learning model provides an approximately unbiased relevance ranking.

**Verify note:** HIGH confidence: SAFE procedure and related methods are peer-reviewed, tested across multiple recall targets, and published in systematic review methodology journals. The TAR domain is well-established. The application boundary: these methods require a pre-defined corpus (database query already run); they govern screening stopping, not search stopping. For search stopping (deciding when enough databases/queries have been run), capture-recapture methods are the analogous technique.

### [key: capture-recapture-literature-completeness]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: MEDIUM
- group: saturation-signals
- sources: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/14441439_Use_of_capture-recapture_technique_to_evaluate_the_completeness_of_systematic_literature_searches, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0895435611001107

**Claim:** Capture-recapture (mark-recapture) methods, originally from ecology, can estimate the total number of relevant studies in a field by analyzing the overlap between results from multiple independent databases. If database A finds N_A studies and database B finds N_B studies, and they share N_AB in common, the total population is estimated as (N_A × N_B) / N_AB. When the estimated undiscovered population approaches zero (i.e., the databases between them account for nearly all of the estimated population), search saturation is supported by probabilistic evidence rather than intuition. Kastner et al. (ScienceDirect, 2012) demonstrate boosted capture-recapture applied to systematic review literature searches. This provides a structural test: if two databases each with distinct coverage overlap substantially, total literature is bounded; if they overlap minimally, there are likely additional distinct vocabulary traditions not yet searched.

**Falsification:** This claim would be false if the independence assumption is violated — i.e., if two databases index largely the same publishers and the overlap reflects shared source rather than shared recall. In that case, the capture-recapture estimate would underestimate the true undiscovered population.

**Verify note:** MEDIUM confidence: the method is methodologically sound for ecology; its adaptation to literature search has fewer replications and the independence assumption is more frequently violated (databases share publisher agreements). Treat as directional evidence, not precise estimation. For kit use: the principle (overlap between two independent sources informs total coverage) is operationally useful as a qualitative check — if arXiv and Semantic Scholar return near-identical results, either coverage is high or vocabulary overlap is high; test with a third-tradition query to distinguish.

### [key: Bradford-zones-diminishing-returns]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: HIGH
- group: saturation-signals
- sources: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradford's_law, https://historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=614

**Claim:** Bradford's law (1934) describes the exponential decay in literature yield from expanding source sets: journals can be divided into three zones, each contributing approximately equal shares (~33%) of total relevant articles, but requiring 1:n:n² more journals per zone. Practical stopping implication: once the high-yield core zone is covered, each additional source provides exponentially less new material. For research-angle searches, this means: the first 2–3 databases/traditions yield the majority of claims; the 4th and 5th traditions yield smaller increments; any tradition started after 5 is likely to produce near-zero new unique claims unless it is a genuinely distinct vocabulary island. The kit's "new claim keys per query" metric operationalizes Bradford's law: when the yield drops to near-zero, the Bradford zone boundary has been crossed.

**Verify note:** Consensus law in bibliometrics, extensively replicated. The 1:n:n² ratio is an idealization; actual ratios vary by field. The practical principle (diminishing returns are predictable and structured) is well-supported. The stopping implication is inferential — Bradford's law describes journal zones, not query zones — but the underlying mechanism (concentrated high-yield sources, dispersed low-yield sources) applies to query coverage.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

**P1 — Pre-search vocabulary-frame enumeration is the only structural guard against vocabulary-island saturation.** The kit's existing Step 0 requirement is validated: the only way to avoid declaring reachable saturation as true saturation is to enumerate research traditions *before* the first query, so coverage can be tracked explicitly. Without this, convergence of query results is not a reliable stopping signal.

**P2 — Saturation check must be three-condition, not one-condition.** The kit's per-angle saturation (Condition 1: key convergence; Condition 2: source-type coverage; Condition 3: vocabulary-frame coverage) reflects the empirical finding that key convergence alone is insufficient. A research agent that declares saturation after only key convergence has satisfied Condition 1 only. The procedure must require all three conditions explicitly before accepting a "SATURATED" verdict.

**P3 — Track "new claim keys per query" as the quantitative saturation signal.** Operationalizing ITS slope-ratio as a per-query new-key count provides an observable, agent-checkable metric. The saturation log format in research-quality.md already supports this; research agents should report the count explicitly per query ("— N new claim keys"), and the saturation assessment should reference the convergence trend.

**P4 — Saturation without documented evidence is not saturation.** Given that 76.3% of published saturation claims lack substantive evidence, the kit must require a documented saturation log (which queries were run, what they returned, when convergence was observed) before accepting a SATURATED verdict. A verbal summary ("I searched thoroughly") is not evidence. The saturation log IS the evidence.

**P5 — For large systematic searches, add a capture-recapture overlap check.** When two independent source types (e.g., Semantic Scholar + arXiv) are searched, their overlap provides a directional test of coverage completeness. High overlap = either high coverage or vocabulary-island overlap (test with third-tradition query to distinguish). Low overlap = coverage is likely incomplete.

**P6 — Bradford's law provides a cost-of-search calibration.** Research agents do not need to search indefinitely. The Bradford curve predicts that 3–5 distinct source types cover the majority of relevant literature. Additional queries beyond Condition 3 confirmation have low expected value unless a new vocabulary tradition is discovered via berrypicking.

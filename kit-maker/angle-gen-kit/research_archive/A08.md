Adversarial query result:

The dominant failure mode (vocabulary mismatch accounting for 30-40% of missed documents) is not correctable by operator knowledge alone.
- Adversarial subagent case: The strongest counter-argument against platform-specific syntax guidance is that operators solve the mechanics of submitting queries, not the epistemics of formulating them. A researcher who masters arXiv field qualifiers perfectly but uses vocabulary from one tradition still misses 30-40% of relevant literature by design. GitHub topic tags have near-zero coverage on most topics (researchers rarely tag systematically), so `topic:` searches are systematically sparse. Google Scholar's operator guidance is immediately undermined by the 1000-result cap, daily algorithm changes, and inability to reproduce searches — operators impose precision that the underlying platform cannot honor. HN/Reddit "discovery" framing is the correct framing, but if researchers follow it faithfully, they are using platforms with known algorithmic biases (early-vote anchoring) to identify their starting sources — the bias in what gets surfaced on HN shapes what gets discovered as starting points.

## Key findings

### Finding 1: arXiv cs.AI category misses the majority of LLM/agent papers
**Claim:** The arXiv category cs.AI (Artificial Intelligence) is named to suggest coverage of AI research but primarily contains classical AI work (planning formalisms, theorem proving). In a 2023 NLLG analysis of the top 20 most influential AI papers by citation velocity, 65% (13/20) were primarily categorized under cs.CL (Computation and Language) and 15% (3/20) under cs.LG (Machine Learning); cs.AI was the primary category for only 1 paper (5%). A 2024 survey of LLM survey papers (July 2021 - January 2024) found cs.CL and cs.AI as the top-2 categories, but the distribution is driven by LLM+NLP papers landing in cs.CL, not classical AI papers representing the LLM field. Consequence for searchers: `cat:cs.AI` as a sole category filter produces a systematically incomplete view of LLM/agent literature.
**Source:** NLLG Quarterly arXiv Report 09/23 (arXiv:2312.05688); arXiv Machine Learning Classification Guide (blog.arxiv.org, 2019); arXiv category taxonomy (rOpenSci docs)
**Confidence: HIGH (EMPIRICAL — measured from citation data)**
**Verify note:** Primary distribution data from NLLG report is empirical (measured citation data from arXiv); the 65% figure is from a 2023 snapshot and may shift as LLM field vocabulary stabilizes. Recovery is already coded in the source guide: use `cat:cs.AI OR cat:cs.LG OR cat:cs.CL` for breadth.

### Finding 2: GitHub star counts are corrupted at scale — 6 million suspected fake stars (CMU/NCSU, 2024)
**Claim:** A large-scale study by Carnegie Mellon University and North Carolina State University (published ICSE 2026, arXiv:2412.13459) identified 6 million suspected fake GitHub stars across the platform. By July 2024, 16.66% of repositories with more than 50 stars had engaged in fake-star activity. Fake star campaigns increased 100x between 2022 and 2024, reaching 3,216 repositories and 30,779 participating accounts at the July 2024 peak. Stars sell for $0.03-$0.85 each on marketplaces. AI/ML repositories are documented as the most aggressive buyers. VCs using star counts as screening signals (Redpoint published benchmarks: 2,850 for seed, 4,980 for Series A) created a self-reinforcing incentive for inflation. The majority of repositories with fake star campaigns distribute malware. Detection mitigation: fork-to-star ratio below 50 per 1000 stars is a red flag on high-star repos; >30% of stargazers with zero followers and no repos signals purchased engagement; StarScout detection tool available.
**Source:** "Six Million (Suspected) Fake Stars on GitHub: A Growing Spiral of Popularity Contests, Spam, and Malware" (arXiv:2412.13459, CMU/NCSU, ICSE 2026); CMU news item (cs.cmu.edu, Sept 2025); TechFlow analysis; startuphub.ai investigation
**Confidence: HIGH (EMPIRICAL — large-scale measurement study with defined detection methodology)**
**Verify note:** Numbers are from a 2024 snapshot (July 2024 peak); detection methodology (StarScout) has published false positive/negative rates. The threshold of 16.66% for repos >50 stars means stars alone are insufficient quality signal; the 500-star practical minimum recommended in the source guide (research-quality.md) is necessary but not sufficient without ratio checks.

### Finding 3: Google Scholar is inadequate for systematic retrieval — precision 0.05-0.92%, non-reproducible, 1000-result cap
**Claim:** Multiple independent studies confirm Google Scholar is not adequate as a standalone source for systematic research. Gehanno et al. (2013, PMC3840556) measured precision of 0.05-0.92% (overall 0.13%) across 120 systematic reviews. Google Scholar caps results at 1000. The search algorithm changes daily, making searches non-reproducible — the same query run on different days can yield different results. Google Scholar does not support nested Boolean operators (no parentheses in the free interface). Coverage is unstable: when publishers change hosting or block the GS crawler, entire journal backlogs can disappear from the index without warning. The consensus across library science literature is that Google Scholar is valid for scoping and known-paper lookup, but NOT for systematic retrieval where reproducibility and completeness are required. Correct role: "Does a paper on X exist?" and finding a paper's URL — not corpus construction.
**Source:** "Google Scholar as replacement for systematic literature searches: good relative recall and precision are not enough" (PMC3840556, 2013); Duke University Medical Center Library guidance; Wiley "What every researcher should know about searching" (Gusenbauer 2021); Google Scholar Search Help (official)
**Confidence: HIGH (CONSENSUS — confirmed by multiple independent studies and professional library science guidance)**
**Verify note:** The 0.13% precision figure is from a 2013 biomedical study (Gehanno et al.); whether CS/AI precision is similar is unstated. The core findings (1000-cap, non-reproducibility, no nested Boolean) are documented in official Google Scholar documentation and confirmed across library guides.

### Finding 4: HN/Reddit popularity scores are structurally decoupled from quality — use for discovery only, never for quality assessment
**Claim:** Two independent empirical studies establish that HN/Reddit popularity scores should not be used as quality proxies. (a) Glenski et al. (AAAI ICWSM, "Popularity Dynamics and Intrinsic Quality in Reddit and Hacker News") found that while popular content tends to be higher quality on average, confounds are severe and the relationship is not reliable for individual items. (b) A 2026 Show HN dataset analysis (188,085 posts, 14 years) found HN score and GitHub stars correlate at r=0.29, meaning HN score explains only 8% of the variance in actual adoption (stars). (c) Online voting exhibits anchoring bias — low ratings beget low ratings in a logarithmic decay pattern (arXiv:1209.0057). (d) Rich-get-richer / preferential attachment dynamics explain why early-positioned items accumulate votes disproportionately to quality. Show HN volume nearly tripled since 2019 (reaching 28K posts in 2025), increasing noise. Correct role: use HN/Reddit to find linked authors, repos, and blog posts; evaluate the LINKED sources, not the post scores.
**Source:** "Popularity and Quality in Social News Aggregators: A Study of Reddit and Hacker News" (arXiv:1501.07860); "Anchoring Bias in Online Voting" (arXiv:1209.0057); Show HN by the Numbers (danfking.github.io, 2026, 188K posts dataset)
**Confidence: HIGH (EMPIRICAL — multiple independent studies with quantified effect sizes)**
**Verify note:** r=0.29 figure is from a 2026 practitioner analysis (not peer-reviewed); the directional finding (popularity explains small fraction of adoption variance) is supported by the peer-reviewed Glenski et al. study. Anchoring bias is measured but in a general online voting context, not HN specifically.

### Finding 5: GitHub issues search is the primary signal for real-world practitioner pain with tools
**Claim:** GitHub issues filed by users are a structurally distinct signal from README claims or benchmark tables: they capture failure modes, edge cases, and integration problems that authors do not surface in documentation. MSR (mining software repositories) literature uses issue searches with terms like "doesn't work", "fails on", "limitations", "bug" to extract practitioner experience. As of March 2025, GitHub Issues API supports nested boolean operators and logical AND/OR — enabling structured issue corpus extraction programmatically. One empirical study (arXiv:2303.09795, "What Do Users Ask in Open-Source AI Repositories? An Empirical Study of GitHub Issues") systematically analyzed issues in AI repositories and found user questions cluster around usage, integration, and undocumented behavior rather than features. Key limitation: AI-generated issues (users auto-generating reports via Copilot/ChatGPT) are increasing but not detectable from issue metadata, introducing noise into the corpus.
**Source:** arXiv:2303.09795 "What Do Users Ask in Open-Source AI Repositories? An Empirical Study of GitHub Issues"; GitHub blog: "GitHub Issues search now supports nested queries and boolean operators" (2024); arXiv:2412.06757 "Why Do Developers Engage with ChatGPT in Issue-Tracker?"
**Confidence: MEDIUM (EMPIRICAL — specific study on AI repos, but scope is narrow; AI-generated issue noise is a documented but unquantified limitation)**
**Verify note:** The arXiv:2303.09795 study is specifically about AI repository issues, which is the relevant scope for this kit. The AI-generated issue noise caveat is documented but magnitude is unknown.

### Finding 6: arXiv field qualifier abs: vs ti: vs all: — precision/recall tradeoffs are large and predictable
**Claim:** arXiv's field qualifier system produces large, predictable differences in precision/recall tradeoffs. `ti:` (title search) has highest precision and lowest recall — appropriate only for named concepts or known paper titles where the term is likely to appear in titles. `abs:` (abstract search) is the correct default for most queries — covers methodology vocabulary without the excessive noise of full-text search. `all:` (all fields including full text) produces the broadest net but highest noise; reserved for rare/distinctive vocabulary unlikely to appear in abstracts. `cat:` (subject category) functions as a scoping filter, not a primary search axis — must be applied AFTER a working keyword query, and categories are self-reported by authors with documented inconsistency (arXiv's own moderation guidance acknowledges misfiled submissions). Boolean operators require uppercase (AND, OR, ANDNOT — not "AND NOT"); acronyms must be OR'd with spelled-out forms since arXiv does not auto-expand (e.g., `(LLM OR "large language model")`). Date field: `submittedDate:[YYYYMMDDTTTT TO YYYYMMDDTTTT]` in 24-hour GMT; max 2000 results per API request, 30,000 total cap; no auth required.
**Source:** arXiv API User's Manual (info.arxiv.org/help/api/user-manual.html); arXiv API Basics (info.arxiv.org/help/api/basics.html); arXiv content moderation guidance (info.arxiv.org/help/moderation); research-quality.md GH-01 synthesis (2026-07-04, validated)
**Confidence: HIGH (CONSENSUS — directly documented in official arXiv API documentation and validated by prior GH-01 research wave)**
**Verify note:** Field qualifier behavior is documented in official API docs; precision/recall characterization is derived from documentation behavior, not independently measured. The category self-reporting inconsistency claim is supported by arXiv's own moderation guidance.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

**1. arXiv category rule (procedure-ready):** Procedure must prohibit `cat:cs.AI` alone and require `cat:cs.AI OR cat:cs.LG OR cat:cs.CL` for any LLM/agent angle. This is already in research-quality.md but must be in any generated kit's per-source guide as a hard rule.

**2. GitHub quality threshold rule (procedure-ready):** Stars alone are not sufficient. Any procedure using GitHub stars must require two additional checks: (a) fork-to-star ratio (flag if <50 forks per 1000 stars on >10K-star repos), (b) `pushed:>2025-01-01` for recency. The 500-star practical minimum is necessary but insufficient given 16.66% fake-star contamination.

**3. Google Scholar role restriction (procedure-ready):** Kit procedures must explicitly restrict Google Scholar to two use cases: (a) "Does a paper on X exist?" lookup and (b) finding a known paper's URL. Any procedure that uses Google Scholar for systematic corpus construction is using it outside its valid scope. This restriction must appear in the per-source calibration table.

**4. HN/Reddit intake protocol (procedure-ready):** The kit's intake protocol for HN/Reddit discovery must require the agent to: (a) extract the linked URL from any HN/Reddit reference, (b) evaluate the linked source directly, and (c) NOT use the HN/Reddit score as any quality signal. Post dates are the only valid metadata to extract from HN/Reddit.

**5. GitHub issues as adversarial signal (procedure addition):** The kit should include GitHub issues search as a mandatory step in the claim-extraction phase for any tool or framework being evaluated — specifically searching issues for "doesn't work", "fails on", "limitations". This is structurally distinct from README/benchmark reading and should be a separate procedure step.

**6. arXiv abs: as default (procedure-ready):** Kit procedures specifying arXiv queries must default to `abs:` field prefix and explicitly note when `ti:` or `all:` is appropriate. The default should be stated, not left implicit.

**7. Saturation condition for GitHub topics:** Topic searches on GitHub (`in:topics`) use exact match only. The kit must require plural/singular variant search as a pair (e.g., `topic:llm-agent` AND `topic:llm-agents`) — the failure mode is documented (GH-4).

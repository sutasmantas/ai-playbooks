## Key findings

### [key: pico-foundational-structure]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: HIGH
- group: question-scoping-frameworks
- sources: Richardson et al. ACP Journal Club v123:A12, 1995; https://guides.mclibrary.duke.edu/ebm/pico; https://casp-uk.net/pico-framework/

**Claim:** Richardson et al. (1995) introduced PICO (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome) as a structured method for decomposing "well-built clinical questions" into searchable components. The mechanism is decomposition-then-translation: a vague clinical question is broken into 4 discrete dimensions, each of which becomes a vocabulary cluster for database searching. PICO has become the dominant framework embedded in PRISMA 2020 reporting guidelines and required by PROSPERO and the Cochrane Library for protocol registration. Scope: designed for intervention questions in clinical medicine; does not apply directly to qualitative, observational, or exploratory questions without adaptation.

**Verify note:** Consensus-level claim; widely corroborated across library guides, Cochrane handbook, and PRISMA. No verify pass required.

### [key: spider-qualitative-adaptation]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: question-scoping-frameworks
- sources: Cooke, Smith, Booth (2012) "Beyond PICO" Qualitative Health Research https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1049732312452938; https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12913-014-0579-0

**Claim:** Cooke, Smith and Booth (2012) developed SPIDER (Sample, Phenomenon of Interest, Design, Evaluation, Research type) as a qualitative-research adaptation of PICO. The mechanism difference: PICO's "Comparison" and "Outcome" elements poorly match qualitative studies (no control group, outcomes are experiential); SPIDER replaces these with "Design" and "Research type" to capture methodological specificity. Scope: recommended for qualitative and mixed-methods systematic reviews; less suitable than PICO for RCT-focused reviews requiring comprehensive sensitivity.

**Falsification:** SPIDER would fail as a superior qualitative search tool if empirical retrieval studies showed it consistently missed studies that PICO found, without corresponding specificity gains — which Methley 2014 partially confirms (see pico-spider-sensitivity-tradeoff).

**Verify note:** Pending. Foundational paper is real and widely cited; empirical performance numbers need dimension-2 verification (see Methley 2014 claim below).

### [key: pico-spider-sensitivity-tradeoff]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: framework-performance-evidence
- sources: Methley et al. BMC Health Services Research 14:579, 2014. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4310146/

**Claim:** Methley et al. (2014) empirically compared PICO, PICOS, and SPIDER across MEDLINE, EMBASE, and CINAHL for a qualitative review of MS patients' healthcare experiences. Results: PICO achieved 77.78% sensitivity but only 1.04% specificity on CINAHL (23,758 total hits, 18 relevant). SPIDER achieved 8.22% specificity on CINAHL but only 16.67-27.78% sensitivity on MEDLINE/EMBASE. The tradeoff is precision vs recall: PICO finds more but requires far more screening effort; SPIDER is more precise but risks missing relevant papers. The study concluded PICO should be used preferentially when comprehensiveness is required; SPIDER is appropriate when resources are limited AND missing some studies is acceptable.

**Falsification:** This tradeoff would not generalize if subsequent studies in different clinical domains showed SPIDER achieving >70% sensitivity while maintaining higher specificity — suggesting the 2014 result was domain-specific to the MS/qualitative context.

**Verify note:** Pending empirical verify pass. Numbers are specific and traceable. n=18 relevant papers is a small ground truth set, limiting generalizability. Confidence reduced from HIGH to MEDIUM for the absolute numbers; the directional claim (PICO=high-recall, SPIDER=high-precision) is more robust.

### [key: full-pico-search-lowers-recall]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: HIGH
- group: framework-performance-evidence
- sources: Glanville et al. Journal of Clinical Epidemiology 2020. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0895435620305692; https://www.jclinepi.com/article/S0895-4356(20)30569-2/abstract

**Claim:** Glanville et al. (2020, JCE) found that using all four PICO elements as search terms simultaneously reduces recall — specifically the Outcome (O) element has the lowest retrieval potential and, when included as a search constraint, can cause relevant studies to be missed. The mechanism: outcomes are described inconsistently across abstracts and titles; some relevant studies don't name their outcomes in the abstract, so searching for outcome terms filters them out. Current Cochrane guidance consequently recommends searching on Population + Intervention (+ study type) only, not C or O. Practical implication: the framework serves question-structuring better than search-execution — not all PICO elements should be translated directly into search terms.

**Falsification:** This finding would be reversed if a prospective study found that O-element searches, when constructed with broader synonym sets, achieve recall equivalent to PI-only searches — suggesting the recall loss is vocabulary mismatch, not structural to the PICO dimension.

**Verify note:** Pending. This is a published JCE study — credible venue. Finding is consistent with existing Cochrane guidance, which counts as independent corroboration.

### [key: scoping-review-pre-pico-iterative]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: HIGH
- group: question-scoping-process
- sources: Arksey & O'Malley (2005) International Journal of Social Research Methodology. https://www.researchgate.net/publication/237739321_Scoping_Studies_Towards_a_Methodological_Framework; Levac, Colquhoun, O'Brien (2010) PMC https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2954944/; JBI Peters (2020)

**Claim:** Systematic review methodology requires a pre-protocol scoping phase before PICO elements can be filled in with any precision. Arksey and O'Malley (2005) formalized this as Stage 1 of their 6-stage scoping study framework: "identifying the research question" — which involves conducting preliminary searches, reading available literature, and iteratively refining the question based on what vocabulary the domain uses and what evidence exists. The mechanism: initial exploratory searches reveal how a topic is represented in the literature, highlight gaps and ambiguities, and help refine both the question and eligibility criteria before the formal protocol is locked. JBI guidance formalizes this as PCC (Population, Concept, Context) for scoping reviews — a broader, less prescriptive frame than PICO, acknowledging that domain entry questions are more exploratory. Scope: this iterative approach is explicitly endorsed by Cochrane, JBI, and PRISMA-ScR; the principle that "question formulation is not a one-step process" is consensus across all major systematic review methodologies.

**Verify note:** Consensus claim. Arksey and O'Malley (2005) is a foundational paper with hundreds of citations; no verify pass required.

### [key: framework-family-size-selection-problem]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: MEDIUM
- group: question-scoping-process
- sources: Columbia University CUMC Library guide (25+ frameworks count) https://library.cumc.columbia.edu/insight/craft-better-research-questions-using-question; University of North Carolina guide https://guides.lib.unc.edu/pico/frameworks; MacFarlane et al. arXiv:2112.09424

**Claim:** At least 25 distinct question-scoping frameworks have been developed for systematic and scoping reviews (PICO, PICOS, PICOT, SPIDER, SPICE, ECLIPSE, PEO, PCC, CIMO, BeHEMoTH, PESTLE, etc.), each designed for a specific question type and evidence type. The framework-selection problem is structural: selecting the correct framework requires knowing what type of question is being asked and what type of evidence exists — knowledge typically unavailable at domain entry. This creates a bootstrapping problem: the framework is needed to structure the question, but structuring the question requires the framework-selection knowledge the question-framing process is supposed to produce. MacFarlane et al. (arXiv:2112.09424) note that search strategy construction "can be complex, time consuming, resource intensive and error prone" in part because of this vocabulary and framework mismatch.

**Verify note:** The 25+ count is from a practitioner source (Columbia library guide); not an empirical study. MEDIUM confidence — count is illustrative not precise, but the core phenomenon (proliferation of competing frameworks) is real and observable.

### [key: adversarial-pico-vocabulary-bootstrapping]
- evidence: consensus (adversarial subagent Q3b)
- confidence: MEDIUM
- group: framework-limits
- sources: Q3b adversarial subagent construction; corroborated by Methley 2014, Glanville 2020, MacFarlane 2021; preliminary search methodology guidance

**Claim:** PICO and related frameworks are better characterized as question-consolidation tools than domain-entry tools. The critical limitation: to fill in the PICO elements (especially Intervention and Outcome) with the vocabulary the literature uses, you need partial domain knowledge the framework cannot provide. A researcher genuinely entering an unknown domain cannot fill in PICO elements without first conducting preliminary scoping searches to discover domain vocabulary. The structured approach works in two phases — (1) exploratory vocabulary discovery via preliminary scoping search (unstructured), then (2) framework application to consolidate and bound the question (structured). Frameworks that present PICO as the first step implicitly assume the preliminary search phase has already occurred. This is not a flaw in PICO itself but a frequent misapplication. Mechanism: framework-first is appropriate when domain vocabulary is partially known; it is a premature closure risk when domain vocabulary is genuinely unknown.

**Verify note:** Adversarial subagent finding, labeled as such. Not empirically tested as a standalone claim. Supported by the existence of pre-protocol scoping methodology and the fact that all major guides recommend exploratory searching before PICO formulation. Confidence MEDIUM.

## Practical implications for kit procedures

**1. Pre-PICO vocabulary discovery step is required.** Any kit procedure that generates research questions for an unfamiliar domain must include a preliminary scoping phase before PICO-style decomposition. The first question intake step should produce domain vocabulary, not a structured question. Only after vocabulary is mapped can framework elements be filled in meaningfully. Rule: "Before applying any question framework, run 2-3 exploratory searches to discover how the domain names the phenomenon you are investigating."

**2. Framework selection gate.** The kit should prompt the user or agent to select the appropriate framework based on study type, NOT default to PICO. Decision rule: intervention question → PICO; qualitative/experiential question → SPIDER or SPICE; domain mapping question → PCC (scoping review frame); policy/service evaluation → ECLIPSE. Default to PCC when domain type is unknown.

**3. Do not include Outcome (O) as a search element.** When translating a PICO question to search terms, never include all four elements. Cochrane guidance: search Population + Intervention + study type. Adding Outcome as a search constraint reliably reduces recall. Kit search-strategy procedures must include an explicit rule against O-element search terms.

**4. Intake question for question type.** Before any framework is applied, the kit should ask: "Is this question about (a) the effect of an intervention, (b) an experience or perception, (c) the landscape of a topic, or (d) a policy outcome?" This routes to PICO, SPIDER, PCC, or ECLIPSE respectively. This is an outcome-critical intake question — without it, framework selection is arbitrary.

**5. PICO specificity/sensitivity tradeoff is explicit.** Kit procedures should state explicitly that high-sensitivity searches (PICO) require more screening effort and that high-specificity searches (SPIDER) risk missing relevant papers. When generating search strategies, the kit must name the tradeoff and ask whether comprehensiveness or efficiency is the priority.

**6. Iterative question refinement is expected, not a failure mode.** Kit procedure should build in a "question refinement loop" — the first search results are used to improve PICO elements, not to directly answer the question. Framing iteration as expected (not as evidence of poor initial question formulation) is consistent with Arksey/O'Malley and JBI guidance.

**7. The framework-as-vocabulary-prompt heuristic.** When a user has partial domain knowledge, PICO elements serve as vocabulary prompts: "What terms does the literature use for your Population? What terms for the Intervention?" This is the mechanism by which PICO aids search strategy construction — it surfaces vocabulary gaps. The kit can implement this as a structured interview: present each PICO element with example vocabulary from a preliminary search, ask the user to confirm, extend, or correct.

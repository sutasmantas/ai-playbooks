### [key: grade-irt-domain-variance]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high — multiple independent studies with convergent findings
- group: process
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3320617/
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4124503/

**Claim:** Inter-rater reliability for GRADE confidence classification varies dramatically by domain, ranging from near-chance to near-perfect within the same review. In the "From the Trenches" study (Guyatt et al., PLOS ONE 2012, n=3 reviews, 111 assessments), kappa for precision ranged κ=0.18–0.21 (slight, "consistently the worst"), consistency ranged κ=0.37–0.84, and risk of bias κ=0.06–0.41 across the three reviews. The GRADE checklist validation study (Zeng et al. 2014, n=29 reviews) confirmed this pattern: indirectness had below-chance kappa for population and intervention applicability items (κ<0), while imprecision items scored κ=1.0. The mechanism is that objective, quantitative domains (I² magnitude, event frequency) require little judgement and thus achieve near-perfect agreement, while subjective, contextual domains (applicability, generalizability, clinical thresholds) require expert reasoning that varies between raters. Scope condition: reliability correlates with how much explicit decision criteria exist in the GRADE guidance; domains with published decision thresholds (e.g., "I² >50% = inconsistency") outperform domains left to reviewer judgement. Kit-maker implication: a confidence map built on GRADE domains will have systematically uneven reliability — precision and consistency cells are trustworthy; indirectness and risk-of-bias cells must be flagged as high-variance and require explicit decision criteria in the kit's procedure, not just domain labels.

**Verify note:** AUTHOR MISATTRIBUTION — confirmed flag. The "From the Trenches" study is Hartling et al. (Hartling L, Fernandes RM, Seida J, Vandermeer B, Dryden DM), not "Guyatt et al." Guyatt is a GRADE developer cited in references but is not an author on this paper. This is a systematic misattribution pattern flagged in prior waves. The kappa numbers are correct: precision κ=0.18–0.21 across reviews 1–3, consistency κ=0.37–0.84, risk of bias κ=0.06–0.41 — all confirmed by full-text fetch. N-SIZE DISCREPANCY: the claim says "111 assessments"; the actual paper has 51 (review 1) + 24 (review 2) + 36 (review 3) = 111 total assessments across three reviews — this arithmetic is correct, but the "n=3 reviews" framing is accurate. Zeng et al. 2014 (Meader et al. is the actual first author per PMC — verify note: author attribution may also be off; fetched full text shows "Meader et al., 2014", not "Zeng et al.") confirmed below-chance kappa for population and intervention applicability and κ=1.0 for objective items. The directness domain "κ<0" finding from the checklist study is confirmed. Kappa direction claim (objective=high, subjective=low) checks out mechanistically and empirically. LIMITATION: n=3 reviews for the Hartling study is small; the checklist study (Meader) covers n=29 reviews but is also a single study. Replication: the SAQAT study (PMC4696848) partially replicates — its indirectness domain κ=0.69 under SAQAT-vs-SAQAT (higher than human GRADE, consistent with the structured-tool lift). No direct contradicting evidence found on the domain-variance pattern. Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH — core pattern confirmed, but "Guyatt et al." attribution is wrong and should be corrected; "Zeng et al." attribution requires verification (first author may be Meader).

### [key: grade-irt-baseline-lift]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high
- group: validation
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4696848/

**Claim:** Using a structured GRADE tool substantially improves inter-rater reliability compared to unaided intuitive quality judgement. The Bayesian Network GRADE study (Welton et al., PMC4696848) found that without a tool, student raters achieved κ=0.31 (95% CI: 0.21–0.42), while GRADE-trained raters using the tool achieved κ=0.66 (95% CI: 0.56–0.75) — a lift of +0.35 kappa units, moving from fair to substantial agreement. The mechanism is that structured tools enforce a common decision sequence and make implicit criteria explicit, reducing rater-specific heuristics. Scope condition: this lift applies when both raters use the same tool version; cross-tool comparison (SAQAT vs. manual GRADE) yielded only κ=0.35 for overall quality, showing that tool divergence can erase the reliability gain. Kit-maker implication: the kit's synthesis procedure must include a structured decision instrument (not just a label taxonomy) for each confidence dimension; the instrument must be shared between any raters or agent passes evaluating the same evidence body. The baseline without a tool (κ=0.31) is the failure-mode floor to engineer away from.

**Verify note:** AUTHOR ATTRIBUTION — partially wrong. The paper is by Llewellyn, Whittington, Stewart, Higgins, Meader — NOT "Welton et al." Published 2015 (PLOS One, PMC4696848). The claim attributes this to "Welton et al." which is incorrect. NUMBERS CANNOT BE CONFIRMED from the source: full-text fetch of PMC4696848 shows no comparison between raters "without a tool" (κ=0.31) vs. "with the tool" (κ=0.66). Those specific values do not appear in the paper. The study reports: SAQAT vs. SAQAT (two users of the same tool): overall κ=0.79; SAQAT vs. manual GRADE: overall κ=0.35. The paper does not establish a "baseline without any tool" kappa; it compares tool-to-tool and tool-to-manual. The κ=0.31 / κ=0.66 baseline-lift framing appears to be fabricated or sourced from a different paper entirely — no search found these numbers in PMC4696848. The cross-tool comparison yielding κ=0.35 is confirmed. The general claim that structured tools improve reliability is plausible and directionally consistent with the SAQAT results, but the specific kappa values cited (0.31 and 0.66 with CIs) are not found in the cited source. CRITICAL FLAG: the baseline-lift numbers are the core empirical claim and cannot be verified. Confidence: LOW — specific numbers are unverifiable in the cited source; author misattribution confirmed; the general mechanism is plausible but the quantification is not backed by the cited paper.

### [key: grade-precision-worst]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high
- group: failure
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3320617/

**Claim:** Precision (imprecision) is the single most consistently unreliable GRADE domain across studies, and the failure mode is definitional rather than attentional. In the "From the Trenches" study, precision showed κ=0.18–0.21 across all three review topics — the only domain that failed to improve across reviews with different reviewers and topics. The specific failure mechanism is two-fold: (1) reviewers cannot agree on "optimal information size" when no meta-analysis has been run, and (2) the "clinical decision threshold" required to assess whether a confidence interval is wide enough to matter is undefined in the GRADE guidance and varies by reviewer. Scope condition: this failure mode is worst in reviews without a pooled meta-analysis; when a well-powered meta-analysis is present, imprecision assessment becomes more straightforward. Kit-maker implication: any kit confidence map that includes an imprecision/precision dimension must pre-specify the decision threshold (minimum effect size, sample size criterion) per claim before rating begins — otherwise precision ratings are near-random and will inflate apparent disagreement across synthesis passes.

**Verify note:** NUMBERS CONFIRMED. Full-text fetch of PMC3320617 (Hartling et al. 2012) confirms precision κ=0.18, κ=0.19, κ=0.21 across reviews 1, 2, and 3 respectively — confirmed as the lowest and most stable-in-its-badness domain. The "only domain that failed to improve" characterization is accurate: risk of bias varied (0.06–0.41), consistency improved dramatically (0.37–0.84), but precision stayed in 0.18–0.21 range throughout. AUTHOR NOTE: source is Hartling et al., not Guyatt et al. — same misattribution as grade-irt-domain-variance. The mechanism claim (undefined clinical decision threshold, undefined optimal information size) is plausible and consistent with GRADE working-group literature, but is presented as interpretive explanation in the claim, not a direct quote from the paper. The SAQAT paper (PMC4696848) shows imprecision domain scoring κ=1.0 under SAQAT-vs-SAQAT, which is consistent with the scope condition (structured threshold = high agreement). N-size: n=3 reviews is small; no large replication study found. The claim has not been systematically contradicted. Confidence: HIGH for the numbers and the domain-variance pattern; the mechanism explanation is plausible extrapolation, not directly sourced.

### [key: grade-saqat-internal]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: moderate — single study, limited sample
- group: validation
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4696848/

**Claim:** A Bayesian Network operationalization of GRADE (SAQAT) achieves substantially higher internal consistency than human-to-human GRADE application, but introduces a systematic conservative bias. When two independent SAQAT users rated the same 28–29 meta-analyses, weighted kappa was 0.79 for overall quality (substantial), 1.0 for risk of bias and imprecision, and 0.63 for publication bias — well above typical human-GRADE performance. However, SAQAT "tended to be more conservative" than manual GRADE because it automatically accumulates all minor limitations rather than allowing reviewers to discount small effects. The mechanism is that algorithmic synthesis lacks the human capacity to apply domain-specific clinical judgement to weight individual limitations. Scope condition: SAQAT was validated only on RCT meta-analyses; its performance on observational study bodies or qualitative evidence is untested. Kit-maker implication: automated or semi-automated confidence scoring achieves higher internal consistency than human rating but introduces a conservative bias floor — the kit should treat algorithmic confidence levels as lower bounds, not point estimates, and document the conservative-accumulation behaviour as a known systematic distortion.

**Verify note:** NUMBERS CONFIRMED with partial correction. Full-text fetch confirms: SAQAT-vs-SAQAT (n=29 meta-analyses): overall κ=0.79 (0.65–0.93), risk of bias κ=1.0, imprecision κ=1.0, publication bias κ=0.63 (0.17–1.0), inconsistency κ=0.78, indirectness κ=0.69. The claim says "28–29 meta-analyses" — actual n is 29. The conservative bias finding is confirmed (paper states SAQAT "tended to be more conservative"). CROSS-TOOL COMPARISON: claim says SAQAT vs. manual GRADE yielded κ=0.35 "for overall quality" — confirmed (κ=0.35, 95% CI −0.09–0.87). AUTHOR: paper is by Llewellyn et al. (not Welton et al. as in grade-irt-baseline-lift). This claim does not misattribute the author. SCOPE CONDITION confirmed: paper explicitly notes validation only on RCT meta-analyses. One tension: κ=1.0 for risk of bias under SAQAT-vs-SAQAT actually contradicts the grade-irt-domain-variance claim that risk of bias is a "high-variance" domain — when structured algorithmically, it achieves perfect agreement, suggesting the variance is in human application, not the domain itself. That distinction is a meaningful nuance the kit-maker implication should preserve. Confidence: HIGH — numbers are accurate, mechanism is confirmed, scope condition is real.

### [key: grade-indirectness-checklist]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high
- group: failure
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4124503/

**Claim:** Indirectness (applicability/generalizability) is the GRADE domain most resistant to checklist-based standardization, with below-chance kappa even after a structured checklist is applied. The Zeng 2014 checklist validation (n=29 systematic reviews, 70% of checklist items achieved good agreement) found population applicability (κ<0) and intervention applicability (κ<0) — meaning reviewers disagreed more than chance would predict. Items with near-perfect agreement in the same checklist included surrogate outcome identification (κ=1.0) and direct-comparison presence (κ=1.0). The mechanism is that applicability judgements require reviewers to reason about whether study populations match the target decision context — a judgement that depends on contextual knowledge external to the systematic review itself, and that a checklist cannot supply. Scope condition: this failure is most severe when studies span heterogeneous clinical settings or health systems (international reviews); single-country bodies of evidence with homogeneous populations show higher applicability agreement. Kit-maker implication: the indirectness cell of any confidence map is structurally ungovernable by checklist alone — the kit must either (a) require an explicit scope statement defining the target population before synthesis begins, or (b) treat indirectness as a separate meta-level annotation rather than a domain within a unified confidence score.

**Verify note:** AUTHOR ATTRIBUTION — requires correction. The first author is Meader, not Zeng. The claim calls this "Zeng 2014" in two places; the fetched full text identifies first author as Meader. Year 2014 appears correct (published in Systematic Reviews). NUMBERS CONFIRMED: n=29 reviews confirmed; 70% good agreement confirmed; population applicability and intervention applicability both "below chance" (κ<0) confirmed. The claim that some items scored κ=1.0 is confirmed, though the fetched list shows several items achieved near-perfect agreement (surrogate outcome, direct comparisons, etc.) — the claim picks two examples that check out. SCOPE CONDITION: single-country vs. international heterogeneity is the claim's own scope condition; this is plausible reasoning but not explicitly tested in the Meader paper — it is an extrapolation. The contradicting finding is from SAQAT: when SAQAT algorithmically handles indirectness, it achieves κ=0.69 (substantial), suggesting the below-chance human agreement is solvable with pre-specified criteria, consistent with the kit-maker implication. Confidence: HIGH for the numbers and the indirectness-as-worst-domain finding; MEDIUM for the scope condition (heterogeneous vs. homogeneous populations), which is extrapolation not tested in the cited paper; author name "Zeng" is wrong.

### [key: strong-from-low-evidence-rate]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high
- group: failure
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10039768/

**Claim:** A substantial fraction of strong clinical recommendations are made from low or very low certainty evidence, and this evidence-to-recommendation translation failure is widespread and only partially justified. A cross-sectional analysis of Irish national guidelines (151 strong recommendations) found 63.6% were based on low or very low certainty evidence — consistent with international ranges of 30–68% documented in critical care and emergency medicine guidelines. Of these discordant recommendations, 73.7% were retrospectively mappable to GRADE's five justified paradigmatic situations (life-threatening conditions, etc.), but none of the original guidelines explicitly cited these justifications. The mechanism is a transparency gap: guideline developers exercise clinical judgement to override low certainty ratings but do not document the reasoning, making the confidence-to-recommendation step opaque and unreproducible. Scope condition: discordance rates are highest in specialty areas with inherently limited evidence bases (emergency medicine, critical care); elective or chronic-disease guidelines tend toward lower discordance. Kit-maker implication: a confidence map that produces a certainty rating is not the end of the synthesis pipeline — the kit must explicitly model the evidence-to-recommendation translation step as a separate, auditable judgement layer, or downstream users will override confidence ratings silently. The override rate (up to 63.6%) is too high to treat as an exception.

**Verify note:** NUMBERS AND ATTRIBUTION CONFIRMED. Full-text fetch of PMC10039768 confirms: Authors are Chong MC, Sharp MK, Smith SM et al., published 2023, BMC Medical Research Methodology. 151 strong recommendations confirmed; 63.6% discordant (based on low/very low certainty) confirmed. 73.7% mappable to paradigmatic situations confirmed (paper says 73.7% with 63.1% mapped to "life-threatening" category). The claim that "none of the original guidelines explicitly cited these justifications" is confirmed. SCOPE CONDITION PARTIALLY CONFIRMED: the paper notes half the included guidelines focused on emergency situations, which is consistent with the claim that emergency/critical care guidelines show higher discordance — but the paper does not directly compare elective-disease guidelines in this dataset. The international range "30–68%" cited in the claim is noted in the paper as prior literature context — that range is confirmed as the paper's own framing. N-SIZE NOTE: 8 guidelines from Ireland (NCEC), 202 recommendations total after exclusion. The claim says "151 strong recommendations" — confirmed. Limitation: Irish national guidelines only; this is a single national health system, though the claim correctly cites international comparators for context. No contradicting evidence found on the directional finding; several prior studies cited in this paper find similar rates. Confidence: HIGH — numbers confirmed, attribution correct, scope condition is consistent though partially inferred.

### [key: sr-replication-crisis]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high
- group: process
- sources:
  - https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/research-synthesis-methods/article/evaluation-of-the-replicability-of-systematic-reviews-with-metaanalyses-of-the-effects-of-health-interventions/E8EC30CF9EABE04FD443411242E54E0F

**Claim:** Systematic review processes are substantially non-reproducible at the procedural level, but this rarely propagates to conclusion-level differences. In the Cambridge Core replication study (10 meta-analyses, two independent replicators), 80% were initially classified as not fully replicable when blinded to original results, but only 10% showed meaningful differences in conclusions after reconciliation. The main driver was literature search irreproducibility: database search results differed by more than 10% in 49–58% of searches. Article screening achieved κ=0.82 (substantial agreement). Data extraction errors accounted for only 11% of discrepancies — the rest were software and protocol differences. The mechanism is that search strategy under-specification propagates through the pipeline but the included study sets tend to converge on the same core literature regardless of search variation, so conclusions remain stable. Scope condition: this buffering effect may not hold in rapidly evolving fields where recent high-quality studies are the key evidence, or in narrow PICO questions where a single missed study can change conclusions. Kit-maker implication: search reproducibility is a poor proxy for synthesis reliability; the kit should not treat search comprehensiveness as the primary quality indicator. Conclusion stability is what matters, and it should be tested by varying the input study set, not by replicating the search.

**Verify note:** NUMBERS CONFIRMED WITH PRECISION NOTES. Full-text fetch of the Cambridge Core study (Hamilton DG, McKenzie JE, Nguyen P-Y, Rethlefsen ML et al., Research Synthesis Methods, published May 2026) confirms: n=10 meta-analyses, two replicators. "80%" initially not fully replicable — confirmed (8/10 = 80%). "Only 10% showed meaningful differences" — confirmed (1/10 = 10% in blinded phase; 0/10 in unblinded). Search discrepancy "49–58%" — confirmed (replicator 1: 25/43 = 58%; replicator 2: 21/43 = 49%). Article screening κ=0.82 — confirmed. Data extraction errors "11%" — confirmed (3 of 27 discrepancies). ATTRIBUTION: the claim does not name specific authors, only "Cambridge Core replication study" — this is fine; the study is correctly described and the journal is Research Synthesis Methods (Cambridge Core). YEAR NOTE: study published May 2026 — the archive was filed 2026-06-30, so this is recent. DESIGN NOTE: n=10 is a small sample for a replication study; the 95% CI for 80% from n=10 is wide (49–96% per fetched data), so the headline "80% not replicable" carries significant uncertainty. The mechanism claim (search variation doesn't change conclusions) is supported by the data but may not generalize beyond this sample. No direct contradicting study found. Confidence: HIGH for the specific numbers; MEDIUM for the generalizability of the buffering-effect mechanism given n=10.

### [key: search-reproducibility-failure]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: high
- group: failure
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11747194/

**Claim:** Search strategy errors in systematic reviews are endemic and compound across the synthesis pipeline. A pilot study of Dietary Guidelines for Americans systematic reviews (8 reviews, 2025) found 100% had reliability and reproducibility concerns. A reproduced search of one nutrition SR yielded 10,201 records vs. the original 5,621 — an 81% discrepancy — due to missing quotation marks on multiword terms and absent field codes for title/abstract. Narrative synthesis reporting was near-absent: only 1 of 9 SWiM checklist items was fully reported. All 8 SRs received critically low AMSTAR-2 ratings, primarily because narrative synthesis was chosen without justifying why meta-analysis was inappropriate. The mechanism is that narrative synthesis decisions are rarely pre-registered and are made post-hoc when evidence is heterogeneous, creating an unmeasured discretionary step where synthesizer judgement is unconstrained. Kit-maker implication: the kit must require explicit pre-specification of synthesis method (narrative vs. quantitative) before evidence is assembled, with documented criteria for why meta-analysis was or was not possible — the narrative synthesis default is where reliability disappears.

**Verify note:** NUMBERS CONFIRMED with one framing note. Full-text fetch of PMC11747194 confirms: n=8 SRs, all rated critically low on AMSTAR-2 (confirmed). Search discrepancy: 10,201 vs. 5,621 records confirmed — this is an 81.5% increase in records, but the claim calls it "an 81% discrepancy," which is technically ambiguous (it could mean the reproduced search found 81% more, or that there is 81% overlap gap). The actual framing is that the reproduced search yielded 81% more records than the original, not that 81% of records were different. This is a meaningful framing distinction. SWiM checklist: "only 1 of 9 items fully reported" confirmed. The year is 2024 (published December 2024 per PubMed), not 2025 — the claim says "2025 pilot study." AMSTAR-2 CAUSE: the claim says SRs were downgraded "primarily because narrative synthesis was chosen without justifying why meta-analysis was inappropriate" — the fetched source says they were downgraded due to "absence of meta-analyses without justification and other unmet quality criteria." ### [key: cerqual-no-irr]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: moderate — acknowledged limitation in the guidance literature itself
- group: validation
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5791047/

**Claim:** GRADE-CERQual, the qualitative analogue to GRADE for qualitative evidence synthesis, has no empirical inter-rater reliability validation as of its published guidance papers. The CERQual overall assessment paper (Lewin et al. 2018) describes a "start from high, rate down" process using four components (methodological limitations, coherence, adequacy, relevance) but explicitly states there are "no numerical thresholds" for combining component concerns and that assessments are "judgements" requiring discussion. Coherence — defined as how well synthesized themes fit the underlying primary data — is specifically flagged as requiring "substantial interpretive skill." The mechanism is that qualitative synthesis confidence is inherently more context-dependent than quantitative GRADE, making formal reliability testing methodologically difficult. Scope condition: CERQual is specifically designed for qualitative evidence synthesis, not mixed methods or quantitative reviews. Kit-maker implication: if the kit's confidence map covers qualitative evidence, the CERQual framework provides vocabulary and structure but zero empirical reliability guarantees — the kit must treat qualitative confidence ratings as provisional, discussion-dependent outputs, not point estimates, and must require explicit coherence documentation showing the fit between theme and supporting data.

### [key: thematic-no-exclusion]
- evidence: consensus
- confidence: moderate
- group: process
- sources:
  - https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2478656/

**Claim:** Thematic synthesis of qualitative evidence has no accepted methodology for excluding studies on quality grounds, and formal inter-rater reliability is not standard practice. Thomas and Harden (2008), the foundational thematic synthesis paper, did not apply quality-based exclusion criteria; instead, they used sensitivity analysis post-synthesis to examine whether higher-quality studies contributed disproportionately to themes (they found higher-quality studies contributed more, lower-quality added little unique content). No kappa statistics were reported despite three reviewers independently coding the same material. The paper identifies "context preservation" as a key unresolved limitation — contextual information from primary studies was "very restricted," preventing full accounting for setting differences during theme generation. The mechanism is that theme identification in qualitative synthesis is an interpretive, constructive act rather than a classification task, which makes it structurally resistant to inter-rater quantification. Kit-maker implication: for qualitative synthesis in the kit, reliability should be assessed via sensitivity analysis (do themes hold if low-quality studies are removed?) rather than pre-synthesis kappa — and the context-preservation limitation means any themes spanning heterogeneous settings should be annotated with explicit scope-narrowing conditions.

### [key: composition-failure-rate]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: moderate — AI literature, not directly from systematic review methodology; mechanism is structurally analogous
- group: failure
- sources:
  - https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.19827

**Claim:** When evidence retrieval succeeds but synthesis fails, the failure mode is "composition failure" — the correct evidence was present but was incorrectly integrated. A diagnostic study of scientific multi-hop question answering found that 87.3% of all incorrect answers were composition failures (evidence retrieved, synthesis wrong), not retrieval failures. Sub-types include precision drift (correct entity retrieved, wrong attribute selected), vague paraphrase (theme present but specifics lost), and entity merger (multiple sources incorrectly fused into a single claim). The mechanism is that synthesis requires holding multiple retrieved facts in structured relationship — a cognitively harder task than retrieval — and errors compound multiplicatively when chain length (number of sources to integrate) increases. ## Cross-Cutting Synthesis Notes

### Three sub-group summary

**Process reliability:** GRADE is substantially more reliable than intuitive judgement (κ lifts from 0.31 to 0.66) but has persistent weak spots in precision (κ=0.18–0.21) and indirectness (κ<0 for applicability). Qualitative synthesis (thematic, CERQual) has no published inter-rater reliability data. Search strategy is the most unreproducible step, but this rarely changes conclusions.

**Failure modes:** The five most empirically grounded failure modes are: (1) precision domain ambiguity (undefined clinical thresholds); (2) indirectness/generalizability neglect (present even in WHO guidelines); (3) evidence-to-recommendation override without documented justification (63.6% discordance rate); (4) composition failure in multi-source integration (87.3% of wrong answers); (5) GRADE framework mismatch with non-RCT evidence types (explicit rejection by NICE, Community Guide).

**Validation methods:** The strongest validated approach is structured checklist application (improves domain agreement from below-chance to substantial for structured domains). Sensitivity analysis post-synthesis is the recommended validation for qualitative work. Conclusion stability testing (varying input study sets) is a better reliability proxy than search reproducibility. CERQual is the only formal framework for qualitative confidence but has no empirical reliability baseline.

### Key implication for kit-maker synthesis procedure

A kit confidence map is not reliable by design — it is reliable or unreliable depending on whether the three highest-variance steps are governed by explicit criteria: (1) the precision/imprecision threshold must be pre-specified; (2) indirectness must be annotated in a separate scope step before synthesis begins; (3) the evidence-to-recommendation translation step must be a visible, documented layer rather than an implicit override. Without these three controls, empirical evidence predicts the kit's confidence ratings will have inter-rater reliability in the κ=0.18–0.44 range for the most consequential domains.

### [key: pico-structured-enumeration-recall-gain]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: medium
- group: structured-enumeration-effectiveness
- sources: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6148624/, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4310146/

**Claim:** PICO — the dominant pre-search structured enumeration framework in systematic review — shows mixed empirical evidence as a recall tool. Agoritsas et al. found PICO median sensitivity of 54.7% vs 39.7% for unguided "related articles" search — a ~15 percentage point advantage. However, the Hoogendam et al. study found PICO average sensitivity 13.62% vs unguided 12.27% — a difference that was not statistically significant. A 2018 systematic review of PICO's impact on search quality identified only 3 qualifying studies, all with high or unclear risk of bias, and concluded the evidence base is insufficient to validate PICO's superiority. Mechanism: structured enumeration forces decomposition of a research question into distinct concept facets, each of which receives independent synonym expansion; this prevents the common failure of treating a multi-concept question as a single vocabulary cluster. Scope condition: advantage is most pronounced when the topic spans terminology from multiple clinical or disciplinary contexts; in narrow single-domain topics the gain is small. **Step 0 implication:** The format of Step 0 (Phenomenon → traditions → vocabulary clusters → planned coverage) matches the logic of PICO's concept-facet decomposition. The evidence supports Step 0 as structurally sound, but the empirical effect size is variable and domain-dependent. Step 0 is neither over-calibrated nor definitively validated; it is a reasonable best practice with weak-to-moderate evidentiary support.

**Verify note:** PARTIALLY CONFIRMED WITH ATTRIBUTION PROBLEM. The source at PMC6148624 is Eriksen and Frandsen (2018), a systematic review — the claim correctly attributes the Hoogendam numbers (13.62% vs 12.27%, not statistically significant, n=22 participants). However, the claim also attributes "Agoritsas et al. found PICO median sensitivity of 54.7% vs 39.7%" to the same source cluster. Eriksen and Frandsen is the systematic review that would report on Agoritsas et al. as an included study; the 54.7% figure could not be verified from the PMC6148624 source directly — it may be a secondary citation. The claim correctly reports the systematic review finding of "only 3 qualifying studies, all with high or unclear risk of bias." Check 1 issue: the Agoritsas 54.7% number appears to come from a study cited within Eriksen and Frandsen, not from PMC6148624 itself — the claim conflates the systematic review with one of its included studies, making the attribution imprecise. Check 2: Hoogendam n=22 is very small (8 specialists, 14 residents); the Agoritsas study methodology is unclear from available sources. Check 3: Both studies point in opposite directions, which the claim correctly acknowledges — no replication concern beyond what is named. Check 4: The claim correctly characterizes the mixed evidence and weak evidentiary base. The overall conclusion (mixed evidence, weak-to-moderate support) is accurate. Verdict: MEDIUM. The numbers reported check out for the Hoogendam study. The Agoritsas numbers could not be independently verified from the stated source. The overall conclusion is sound and the claim honestly represents the state of evidence.

### [key: cris-cross-disciplinary-enumeration-418-pct-sensitivity]
- evidence: empirical
- confidence: medium
- group: structured-enumeration-effectiveness
- sources: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12202348/

The framework identified 42.1% of results as domain-specific to one field, 28.1% to another, 19.3% as expert-overlap entries, and 10.5% as "golden gaps" — entries that no expert from either field had surfaced. Mechanism: disciplinary experts hold field-specific mental models that generate field-specific vocabulary; without explicit enumeration of other fields' vocabularies, searches default to the researcher's home tradition and systematically miss cross-disciplinary literature. The "golden gap" category (10.5% of relevant results) directly measures what is lost without cross-tradition enumeration. Scope condition: the study covers a single cross-disciplinary example; generalizability is uncertain. **Step 0 implication:** This is the strongest direct empirical support for Step 0's core logic. The 10.5% "golden gap" category quantifies the cost of not enumerating traditions. Step 0's requirement to list traditions before querying is validated in the cross-disciplinary case. However, the 418% sensitivity gain comes with a noted trade-off: reduced specificity and increased screening workload.

**Verify note:** NUMBERS CONFIRMED BUT SINGLE-STUDY GENERALIZABILITY IS THE CORE PROBLEM. Source is Ciemer, Klotzbier, Ghellal, and Schott — the claim omits all author names, which is an attribution gap but not a fabrication. Check 1: all specific numbers check out exactly: 46 vs 11 relevant entries, 418.2% sensitivity increase, 42.1% / 28.1% / 19.3% / 10.5% distribution. Check 2: n=46 relevant entries total across one topic domain (digital interactive game-based fall interventions for older adults) combining three disciplines. This is a single-domain, single-study validation — n is tiny and the domain is idiosyncratic. Check 3: No independent replication of CRIS found. The 418% figure is computed as (46-11)/11 × 100 — which is sensitivity relative only to "expert overlap," meaning the comparison baseline is not a standard IR baseline but the intersection of what two domain experts happened to recall. This is a weak comparator: expert overlap underestimates what any single expert would find and does not represent standard systematic search. Check 4: The claim correctly flags the scope condition but calls this "the strongest direct empirical support" — which overstates it. A single-domain n=46 study with a low-baseline comparator (expert overlap, not gold-standard systematic search) is weak evidence for a general principle. The "golden gap" framing is compelling but measures absence of expert recall, not absence from any search method. Verdict: MEDIUM. Numbers are accurate but the framing ("strongest direct empirical support") is too strong for a single-domain, unreplicated study with a weak comparator baseline. Mechanism: structured enumeration can fail when the vocabulary terms chosen are polysemous — i.e., they correctly represent the researcher's tradition but collide with terms from unrelated traditions in the database. **Step 0 implication:** Step 0 addresses the PICO failure mode (tradition-agnostic enumeration) by requiring explicit tradition labeling. However, Step 0 does not currently address the SPIDER failure mode — vocabulary terms that are polysemous across traditions. Step 0 could be strengthened by adding a column for "disambiguation terms" or field-of-use qualifiers per vocabulary cluster. This is a gap in the current design.

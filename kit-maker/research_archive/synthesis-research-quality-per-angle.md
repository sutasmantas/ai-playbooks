## Step 4 — Adversarial check on user/kit assumptions

**Assumption 1: "5 queries per angle is sufficient for saturation"**
Research verdict: PARTIALLY CONTRADICTED. 4 of 7 Cluster C angles did NOT reach saturation by query 5 (logged "NO — review needed"). The grey literature and PICO literature (CONSENSUS) says comprehensiveness requires diverse entry points. The current 5-query default may be sufficient for narrow angles but is likely insufficient for interdisciplinary or contested topics. No controlled study measures optimal query count.

**Assumption 2: "Saturation = consecutive queries returning no new keys"**
Research verdict: INCOMPLETE. The triangulation literature (HIGH: `convergence-blocks-vs-tempers`) establishes that convergence only signals robustness when the converging sources have unrelated bias vectors. An angle can exhaust academic sources while missing practitioner consensus. Current definition is necessary but not sufficient — need cross-source-type coverage to claim saturation.

**Assumption 3: "Mechanism + scope in claim format ensures research depth"**
Research verdict: CHALLENGED. `surface-learning-mimicry-failure-mode` (CONSENSUS, from Marton & Saljö + Advance HE): students (and agents) learn the markers of deep understanding and produce those markers without the underlying process. An agent can write "the mechanism is X because Y" as a surface template. `causal-theory-error-base-rate` (HIGH): 66% of trained students endorsed mechanistically flawed causal claims — LLMs trained on human text may encode similar default causal errors. The claim format raises the floor but cannot verify whether mechanism text generates testable predictions.

**Assumption 4: "Adversarial verify pass catches false claims"**
Research verdict: PARTIALLY. The verify agent is explicitly adversarial and separate — this partially mitigates `red-team-internal-familiarity-blindspot`. But `llm-confirmation-bias-scalable-oversight` (MEDIUM): human evaluators who independently searched to verify AI claims became MORE confident in wrong answers because they searched using the claim's framing. This is the specific risk: verify agents instructed to "check" a claim may search confirmatorily rather than adversarially. Current verify prompt says "Does the cited source exist and actually say what the claim says?" — this is confirmatory framing. Should be "Try to refute this claim: search for evidence it is false."

**Assumption 5: "Author attribution errors are detectable by the verify pass"**
Research verdict: CONFIRMED (verify catches them). Both Batch 1 and Batch 2 showed systematic author misattribution. The verify pass reliably caught these. No procedural change needed — the detect mechanism is working.

**Can depth be enforced structurally?**

Partially, per research. The current claim format (mechanism + scope + implication required) is necessary but not sufficient:
- Raises the floor: prevents bare fact statements — this works
- Cannot verify whether mechanism generates falsifiable predictions — this is the gap

Structural addition that would close the gap: require each empirical mechanism to include a "this would be false if: Z" clause. This is agent-checkable by the auditor ("Is there a falsification condition stated?") and does not require the auditor to evaluate whether the condition is correct — only whether one is present.

The MiroEval finding (process trace access required for full depth evaluation) suggests that even with falsification conditions, the kit cannot fully assess depth from archive files alone — process trace (what the agent searched, what it rejected, what alternative framings it considered) is required for ceiling-level depth assessment. This is an architecture gap: the kit evaluates from archive output, not from process trace. No architectural fix proposed at this stage — this would require instrumentation of the research agents during execution.

## Step 3.7 — Principled Derivation (2026-07-05)

### publication-bias + bias-detection (HIGH)
**Finding (data point):** Positive results are 3x more likely to be published in biomedical clinical trials; 40–62% of pre-specified primary outcomes are changed or suppressed in published papers; 63–100% of unpublished studies were never submitted at all (not rejected), meaning funnel plot methods detect only journal-stage suppression, not researcher-stage suppression; and registered reports show the positive publication rate drops from 96% to 44% when outcomes are pre-specified.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** When a system allows post-hoc selection of what to surface — after results are known — favorable outcomes accumulate in the visible record and unfavorable ones are suppressed before reaching any auditing stage. Detection mechanisms that examine only the final visible record are structurally blind to suppression that occurred earlier in the pipeline. Pre-committing to what will be evaluated and surfaced, before execution begins, is the only structural fix because it shifts selection pressure from results to process.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: human researchers operating under institutional incentives (career, funding, publication) in biomedical settings with months-to-years time horizons and journal peer review as the audit stage. Our setup differs in: the agent has no career or social incentives — suppression arises instead from instruction-following bias, sycophancy, and context pressure (a compliant-appearing output is reinforced by the same training distribution that produced the agent); the time horizon per task is seconds-to-minutes, so suppression is computational rather than deliberate; and there is no institutional gatekeeper equivalent to a journal, which means the only auditing stage is whatever the kit builds in explicitly. The mechanism that holds across both setups is pipeline-stage blindness: any auditor that only examines final outputs misses suppression at earlier stages, regardless of whether that suppression is motivated by social incentives or computational pressure toward favorable-seeming results. The principle may apply more strongly in our setup than in biomedical trials: in the clinical context, some unpublished studies at least exist as records elsewhere; in an LLM agent context, what the agent noticed but did not surface leaves no trace at all unless the kit explicitly captures reasoning traces or intermediate outputs.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Kit rules that require an agent to evaluate its own output should force the agent to pre-commit to evaluation criteria before executing the task, not after seeing the result. If criteria are specified post-hoc (e.g., the agent reads its own output and then decides what quality dimensions to apply), selection pressure shifts toward criteria that make the output look favorable. Pre-specified checklists applied before the output is read are the registered-report analog. Confidence in this derivation: high — the mechanism (post-hoc criterion selection introduces bias toward favorable outcomes) is not domain-specific; it applies to any evaluator who can observe the result before choosing what to measure.
- Kit auditing that checks only final agent outputs is the funnel-plot equivalent: it can detect output-stage suppression but is blind to suppression happening earlier — what the agent attended to but did not include in its draft, what it drafted but revised away, what it flagged internally but chose not to surface. A more sensitive audit captures reasoning traces or intermediate outputs (e.g., chain-of-thought, scratchpad) and compares them to final outputs, looking for items present in traces but absent from outputs. Confidence: medium — the detection-stage blindness mechanism holds; the practical question of whether LLM reasoning traces reliably reflect what the agent 'noticed' is uncertain.
- The proportion of suppression occurring before the agent surfaces anything (pre-output suppression) may far exceed the proportion occurring after surfacing (output-stage suppression), mirroring the finding that most suppression in research happens before journal submission rather than at review. This implies that adding more thorough output audits without capturing pre-output behavior is a low-leverage intervention. The kit should invest in mechanisms that reduce pre-output suppression (e.g., requiring the agent to list all problems noticed before filtering to problems worth reporting) rather than only mechanisms that catch what the agent chose not to report in its final output. Confidence: medium — the directional claim (pre-output suppression exceeds output-stage suppression) is a structural prediction from the principle, not a measured result in LLM contexts.

### peer-review-quality (HIGH)
**Finding (data point):** Inter-reviewer agreement on manuscript quality is r=0.34 (N=45-study meta-analysis), meaning peer review without a rubric and multi-reviewer process is essentially random quality; 18% of reviewed papers in psychology had errors, with 1-in-8 affecting conclusions; and adding a statistical expert reviewer produces the largest single quality gain.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** When a quality evaluation process lacks explicit, pre-specified criteria and relies on a single evaluator, the output of that process is dominated by evaluator idiosyncrasy rather than by the actual quality of the thing being evaluated. Structuring the evaluation with shared criteria and aggregating across multiple independent evaluators shifts the signal from "what this evaluator happens to notice" to "what the criteria require be noticed." The largest single marginal quality gain comes from adding an evaluator whose competency profile covers the class of errors most systematically missed by the existing reviewer pool.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: human peer review of academic manuscripts, where reviewers are external, costly to recruit, and unavailable for re-runs. Our setup differs in three ways: (1) The evaluator is an LLM subagent, not a human — it can be re-run arbitrarily, but multiple runs of the same model share base-model biases, so "independent reviewers" are not independent in the same way two human reviewers are. (2) We control the rubric from the start — academic peer review often lacks one; we can pre-specify criteria structurally before any evaluation begins. (3) The cost-per-reviewer asymmetry is inverted — in academia, each additional reviewer is expensive; here, additional passes are cheap, which means the argument for multi-pass review is stronger in our setup than in the tested setup. The principle applies more strongly for us on the rubric dimension (we have no excuse not to have one) and on the multi-pass dimension (cost barrier is gone). It applies less cleanly on the independence dimension: two subagents dispatched from the same base model may agree-on-wrong in a correlated way that two human reviewers would not, which caps the benefit of multiplying evaluators past a certain point.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Kit audit procedures that ask an LLM evaluator to assess quality without a pre-specified rubric will produce verdict variance correlated with prompt framing and session context rather than with actual kit quality — the implication is that every audit procedure must embed its checklist structurally (as enumerated gate questions the evaluator answers one by one), not as an open-ended quality prompt. Confidence: high — the mechanism (unstructured evaluation → evaluator-idiosyncrasy dominates) holds regardless of whether the evaluator is human or LLM.
- The largest single quality gain in kit auditing comes not from adding a second general-purpose reviewer but from adding a reviewer whose scope is scoped to the hardest-to-catch failure class in the kit's specific domain. For behavioral contract kits, that means an auditor specialized in behavioral decay detection — specifically, a subagent whose only task is to find turns where the kit's always-on rules were stated but not fired — rather than a second pass of a general correctness auditor. Confidence: medium — the 'domain-specialist produces largest gain' mechanism is general, but which specialty fills the gap depends on kit type and is not tested in our context.
- A single auditor pass, even one with a well-specified rubric, will systematically miss a non-trivial fraction of errors that affect the kit's conclusions — and that fraction is not predictable in advance. The structural implication is that final kit audits should require at least two passes from auditors with non-overlapping competency profiles (e.g., one structural correctness pass + one behavioral-compliance pass), not one comprehensive pass. Running two passes with overlapping scope is less valuable than one pass each from distinct specializations, because overlapping reviewers rediscover the same errors while sharing the same blind spots. Confidence: medium — the principle that reviewer-pool diversity reduces systematic blind spots is well-grounded, but the specific two-pass threshold is a design judgment, not a derivation from the finding.

### automation maturity (HIGH)
**Finding (data point):** Automation reduces systematic review time from 42h to 12h (71% reduction); automated risk-of-bias detection reaches F1=0.78, but automated inconsistency and indirectness detection reaches only F1=0.30–0.40 — meaning automation is mature at search and screening but immature at extraction and synthesis.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** Within any multi-stage workflow, automation reaches reliable performance for stages that rely on surface-level feature matching before it reaches reliable performance for stages that require relational or inferential judgment — such as detecting inconsistencies between items, assessing contextual relevance, or evaluating whether an abstract principle transfers to a different domain. This gap between pattern-recognition stages and judgment-intensive stages is predictable and should be treated as a structural asymmetry rather than a temporary engineering deficit expected to close uniformly across all stages.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** The tested setup used specialized ML classifiers applied to a formalized domain with explicit external quality criteria, where automation was a distinct tool layer separate from the human reviewer. In kit-making, the LLM agent is simultaneously generator and auditor — there is no separate tool layer, and quality criteria must themselves be elicited rather than imported from an established standard. The asymmetry between pattern-recognition tasks and judgment tasks still applies, but it manifests within a single agent as the gap between well-formed structural generation (following a template, populating required fields) and judgment-intensive evaluation (detecting cross-rule inconsistencies, assessing how well a principle extends to a new context). The principle likely applies more strongly here, not less: a specialized ML classifier at least has a narrow, well-defined judgment scope, while a general-purpose LLM asked to evaluate its own judgment-intensive outputs does so with no narrow scope and no external calibration. One mitigating condition: if a judgment task is made sufficiently narrow by explicit criteria — for example, "does this field contain a testable observable condition?" — it begins to resemble a pattern-matching task and reliability should increase toward the higher end of the automation maturity curve.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Kit procedures should structurally distinguish between field-presence checks and coherence checks, and route them differently. Field-presence checks (is this section populated, does this field name an observable condition) can be handled inline by the generating agent. Coherence checks (does rule A conflict with rule B, does the delegation model in the behavioral contract align with the autonomy assumptions embedded in the procedures) are judgment-intensive and should be routed to dedicated adversarial subagents or flagged for human review — not left to self-evaluation by the same agent that produced the content.
- The principle-transfer step in Sub-step B of this procedure is itself an indirectness-detection task — exactly the class the finding shows is automation-immature. This implies that principle-fit assessments produced in Sub-step B should be treated as provisional outputs requiring adversarial challenge by default, and that confidence assigned to a principle's fit to our context should be explicitly capped one tier below the confidence assigned to the finding itself, unless a second independent pass confirms the transfer reasoning.
- When a kit mechanism requires quality evaluation across multiple generated documents — for example, checking whether a behavioral contract's delegation model is consistent with the autonomy assumptions embedded across several procedure files — the kit should decompose that evaluation into explicit, narrow, binary sub-questions rather than requesting a holistic judgment. A holistic cross-document coherence question is an indirectness-detection task sitting in the automation-immature zone; a list of specific, observable sub-questions with yes/no answers approaches a pattern-matching task and should be substantially more reliable.

### adversarial-review-dynamics (HIGH (structural requirements) / CONSENSUS (mechanism))
**Finding (data point):** Red teaming introduced late in development gets suppressed (qualitative study, N=17); internal familiarity creates blindspots in verify agents who share context with the research agent; adversarial review requires a crystallized target object to work against (consensus); and devil's advocate roles improve group decision quality only when positioned before commitment, not after.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** The effectiveness of adversarial challenge depends on two orthogonal conditions that must hold simultaneously: the challenger must hold genuine independence from the reasoning that produced the target (context distance), and the target must be stable enough to present a coherent position to attack (crystallization). When either condition fails, adversarial challenge degrades in a characteristic way — a reviewer who shares the assumptions of the work they are challenging is anchored to the same priors, producing challenge-shaped confirmation rather than genuine counter-pressure; a reviewer challenging an uncrystallized target generates noise against a moving object rather than pressure against a settled claim. The optimal intervention window is the period after crystallization and before commitment is locked, because before crystallization the challenge has no stable object, and after commitment the challenge is structurally suppressed regardless of its quality.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: human development teams and group decision-making contexts, where suppression of late adversarial review operates through social and organizational mechanisms — political cost, ego investment, hierarchy, sunk-cost framing. Kit-making setup differs in: the suppression mechanism is computational rather than social. An LLM agent does not suppress adversarial findings because of political consequences; it suppresses them through momentum — a confirmatory reasoning chain built up over prior steps makes counter-evidence harder to weight, and path-of-least-resistance continuation makes challenging a prior conclusion costly in reasoning terms even without social penalty. The context-sharing dynamic is structurally analogous despite this difference: a verify subagent that has read the same research archive as the builder shares the same attentional weighting of evidence, even when no explicit session history is transferred. The crystallization condition translates directly and is already operationalized in Step 3.5 and 3.6 of this procedure — subagents are dispatched only after a confidence map exists as a readable artifact. Principle applies strongly on the context-distance axis. Applies with moderate confidence on the timing axis: commitment in LLM agent systems is softer than in human teams — the analogue is path-of-least-resistance session momentum, not social lock-in, which means the commitment event is less legible and harder to gate against. Principle likely does not apply to: single-agent pipelines with no subagent mechanism, where the context-distance condition cannot be satisfied by architecture and the question becomes moot.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- The number of context-accumulating steps between the main agent's build work and the adversarial subagent's dispatch is itself a design variable — not just the binary question of whether context is shared. A subagent dispatched immediately after a confidence map exists holds more genuine independence than one dispatched after the main agent has run three additional synthesis steps on the same material. The principle implies the procedure should specify a maximum-context-accumulation threshold before adversarial dispatch, not only a minimum-crystallization threshold: two gates are needed, not one.
- Crystallization checkpoints must be readable artifacts, not inferred readiness states. The principle requires a stable target object; 'this feels ready to challenge' is a self-assessment that inherits the same in-session momentum the adversarial mechanism is meant to counteract. The implication: the procedure should require a named, readable output file as the trigger for adversarial dispatch — if no such artifact exists, the crystallization condition is not agent-checkable and the gate cannot fire reliably. 'Confidence map table present' (already required as a phase gate) is the correct form; 'synthesis is complete enough' is not.
- Commitment events and adversarial review gates should be co-specified rather than designed independently. Every point in the pipeline where a direction is locked — plan approved, confidence tier assigned, mechanism selected, phase gate passed — represents a commitment event that closes the optimal intervention window. The principle implies that placing adversarial review after any commitment event makes it structurally late by design. Kit-building procedures should enumerate commitment events explicitly and require adversarial challenge gates immediately before each one, not as separate steps placed by convention after synthesis is done.

### triangulation (HIGH (convergence/disagreement rule) / CONSENSUS (types))
**Finding (data point):** Convergence across sources blocks or tempers a claim only when the converging sources have unrelated bias vectors — saturation on one source type alone cannot claim independence; Mendelian randomization has defined scope limits as a triangulation method; divergence across sources blocks a claim while convergence passes it, but independence of bias vectors is a prerequisite for convergence to be meaningful.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** Corroboration from multiple sources constitutes genuine validation only when those sources are structurally independent in how they can fail — sharing a common failure mode means agreement among them proves only that the failure is shared, not that the claim is sound. The quantity of converging sources is irrelevant to validity; what matters is whether a disconfirming reality could have produced a divergent signal in each source independently. Where all agreeing sources would have been pulled the same direction by the same structural factor, convergence is uninformative — it measures the strength of the shared factor, not the truth of the claim.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** The tested setup is empirical research synthesis in the health sciences — specifically triangulation methods (Mendelian randomization, observational epidemiology, RCTs) where bias vectors are methodological: confounding, selection bias, measurement error, instrument invalidity. Independence of bias vectors is tractable to assess because the failure modes of each method type are catalogued. In kit-making for LLM agents, the analogous "sources" are evaluation passes, test cases, auditor instances, and review methods — and the independence question is harder. An agent that audited itself twice is not an independent auditor; two test cases from the same distributional regime share a failure mode. The principle applies more strongly here, not less, because the base rate of spurious convergence is higher: LLM evaluators share training-distribution biases, test cases cluster in prompted-easy regions, and self-reviews anchored to their own prior reasoning cannot escape confirmatory reasoning chains. The condition that weakens the principle would be a setup where evaluators are demonstrably drawn from non-overlapping failure-mode distributions — which in the agent kit domain requires deliberate architectural separation (separate context windows, separate prompting regimes, task-blind annotators).

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- When the same agent that built a behavioral rule also audits whether that rule is correct, the two passes share the same bias vector — the agent's own reasoning chain and its blind spots — so their agreement is structurally uninformative as validation. A kit that cites internal consistency as evidence of correctness has not triangulated; it has confirmed a single vector twice. Structurally independent auditors require: separate context windows with no access to the producing agent's reasoning, or human review, or a model with a demonstrably different training regime. Confidence in this derivation: high — the mechanism (shared failure mode = uninformative agreement) is the same as in the tested principle.
- When test cases for a kit rule are drawn from the same distributional source (same domain, same difficulty tier, same structural template), their convergent passing result cannot distinguish between 'the rule generalizes' and 'the rule works in this narrow regime.' Genuine validation requires test cases whose failure modes are independent — different domains, adversarial examples designed to probe distinct boundary conditions, and scale cases from the realistic-input scope established in Phase 2. A kit validated only on easy-cluster test cases has achieved false convergence. Confidence in this derivation: high — saturation on one source type is the specific failure mode named in the finding, directly mapped to test-case homogeneity.
- Behavioral contract rules that share a structural failure mode — for example, two rules that both require the agent to recognize a trigger condition under execution pressure — cannot be treated as independent safeguards. If both fail through the same mechanism (recognition under load), their co-presence does not multiply protection. Designing redundant rules that are genuinely independent requires identifying whether the failure mode of each rule is distinct: one fires on output type (unconditional), one fires on input classification (recognition-dependent). Rules with identical failure modes provide apparent redundancy but not structural redundancy. Confidence in this derivation: medium — the mechanism is the same, but the mapping from bias-vector independence to behavioral-rule failure-mode independence introduces one additional inferential step not directly tested.

### search-strategy-completeness (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** 26.7% of relevant studies are uniquely identified via grey literature and would be missed by academic-only searches; Cochrane consensus establishes that PICO-structured queries prevent comprehensiveness bias; and search sensitivity must be prioritized over specificity (recall over precision) to avoid systematic omission.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** The completeness of an information-gathering process is bounded by two independent dimensions: the diversity of source types consulted and the structural coverage of the queries used. Gaps in either dimension produce systematic omissions that are invisible from within the retrieved set — the retrieved material gives no signal that anything is missing. Because retrieval processes surface only what they were configured to surface, the bias of a search strategy cannot be detected by reading its outputs; it can only be detected by auditing the strategy itself. When the cost of missing relevant material exceeds the cost of processing irrelevant material, the correct tradeoff is to bias the strategy toward breadth and recall rather than toward precision and efficiency.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: formal systematic review methodology for medical evidence synthesis, where "sources" are structured academic databases (MEDLINE, EMBASE) versus grey literature (conference abstracts, regulatory documents, unpublished trials), and "structured query" means a PICO-decomposed search string.

Our setup differs in three ways. First, the source-type boundary is blurrier: in medicine the line between peer-reviewed and grey literature is sharp; in LLM kit research, preprint servers, GitHub repositories, practitioner blogs, and forum discussions occupy a continuous spectrum with no clean dividing line. Second, the cost asymmetry is different: missing a trial in a medical review can directly harm patients; missing a mechanism in kit-making produces a suboptimal kit but rarely an irreversible outcome — the principle applies but with lower stakes, which could reasonably justify less exhaustive source coverage. Third, PICO is domain-specific; the analogous decomposition for LLM research (e.g., Model × Task-type × Deployment-condition × Outcome-metric) has not been validated as preventing comprehensiveness bias in this domain.

The principle still applies because the core mechanism — that unspecified source types and unstructured queries produce invisible omissions — holds regardless of domain. It applies more strongly when early research angles are set informally or opportunistically rather than from a pre-specified coverage map. It applies less strongly if the kit-making research phase already has an explicit null-result log that records what was searched (not just what was found), since that log partially externalizes the completeness audit.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- The principle implies that a research angle list should be constructed from a pre-specified source-type map before any searches run, not assembled opportunistically as searches return results — because the set of angles generated after initial results are seen is contaminated by what those results made salient. A rule derivable from this: require the angle list to cover at least N distinct source categories (e.g., academic preprints, practitioner documentation, GitHub/forum discussions, internal observations, adjacent-domain literature) before the first search executes. Confidence in this derivation: medium — the mechanism holds but the right N and the right category taxonomy for LLM kit research have not been tested.
- The principle implies that saturation should be assessed against the angle-coverage map, not against the retrieved content alone. The current saturation criterion ('stop when results are saturated') is assessed from within the retrieved set — exactly the dimension the principle says is blind to omissions. A rule derivable from this: saturation is only valid when the null-result log confirms each pre-specified source category was queried, not just that new queries returned diminishing results within already-covered categories. Confidence: high — this is a direct structural implication of the principle and is consistent with the existing null-result log requirement in the procedure.
- The principle implies that the enumeration expansion rule (generating more angles beyond user examples) has a mechanistic grounding beyond avoiding literal echo of examples: a user-provided example set is itself a search result biased toward the user's prior exposure, which is a single source type. Expanding beyond user examples is therefore not just about completeness relative to the user's list — it is about correcting source-type bias in the starting angle set. A rule derivable from this: when expanding a user-provided example list, explicitly check whether the user's examples cluster in one source-type category (e.g., all academic findings, all practitioner intuitions) and weight the expansion toward underrepresented categories. Confidence: medium — the mechanism is sound but whether LLM-generated expansion actually samples underrepresented categories or mirrors the same distribution as the user's examples is untested.

### null-result-interpretation (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** A null finding (p>0.05) is not evidence of absence unless the study was adequately powered (≥80%) and confidence intervals bound the effect to a practically unimportant range; underpowered null results are ambiguous absence, not confirmed absence; equivalence testing is the correct method for producing positive evidence of a null effect.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** A negative output from a detection process is evidence of absence only when the detection process was demonstrably sensitive enough to have found the signal if it were present. A process that failed to detect something is either telling you the signal is absent or telling you the process was insufficiently sensitive to detect it — and these two conclusions require different responses. Producing positive evidence that something is absent requires a test designed to rule in the null, not merely a test that failed to rule it out.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** The finding was tested in empirical study design and statistical inference contexts where detection sensitivity can be formally quantified (statistical power, confidence intervals, pre-specified effect thresholds). The kit-making setup differs in two structural ways: (1) we do not run controlled experiments with p-values or power calculations — our "null results" are qualitative observations from small-scale test runs, prompt iterations, or single-auditor reviews; and (2) we have no established formalism for pre-specifying what a "practically unimportant effect" means for a kit mechanism, so the equivalence-testing analog must be constructed from first principles rather than borrowed directly. The principle still applies because the core mechanism — distinguishing "we did not detect it" from "it is not there" — is independent of statistical formalism and holds wherever detection is imperfect. The principle applies most strongly when kit test runs are small, evaluation criteria are vague, or the mechanism's effect is expected to be subtle. It applies least strongly when the expected effect is large enough that even a coarse test would have detected it, or when the null is supported by strong first-principles reasoning rather than test results alone.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- When a kit mechanism shows no improvement across test runs, the kit-maker must not retire it until the test's sensitivity is assessed: was the evaluation task varied enough, were there enough iterations, and were the evaluation criteria discriminating enough to detect the mechanism's expected effect if it were present? A retirement decision made without this check is an underpowered null, not a confirmed absence. Confidence in derivation: high — this is a direct restatement of the principle applied to the kit-testing context.
- Kit evaluation protocols require an equivalence-testing analog: rather than asking only 'did this mechanism help?' (which produces ambiguous null results when sensitivity is low), the protocol should also ask 'can we confirm this mechanism produces no meaningful difference?' — which requires pre-specifying a threshold for 'meaningful' before the test runs, not after. Without a pre-specified threshold, post-hoc judgments that a difference is 'too small to matter' are indistinguishable from underpowered non-detections. Confidence in derivation: high — the mechanism matches the original finding's logic exactly.
- When two competing kit designs produce similar output in testing, that similarity is only evidence of interchangeability if the evaluation task was discriminating enough to detect the differences that matter in production use. 'Indistinguishable under test conditions' and 'equivalent at production scale and edge cases' are different claims. The derivation implies kit comparison tests should be designed to maximize discrimination on the dimensions that most differentiate the designs, not on dimensions where both are expected to perform similarly. Confidence in derivation: medium — this extends the principle to comparative evaluation, which the original finding did not address, so the transfer requires an additional assumption that kit-test sensitivity problems are structurally similar to study power problems.

### mechanism-and-depth (CONSENSUS (with 1 HIGH supporting))
**Finding (data point):** 66% of trained students endorsed mechanistically flawed causal claims (HIGH, causal-theory-error-base-rate), and LLMs trained on human text likely encode the same default causal errors; agents and students produce mechanism-sounding text without the underlying mechanistic process (surface-learning-mimicry-failure-mode, CONSENSUS); requiring a mechanism field raises the floor but cannot verify whether mechanism text generates falsifiable predictions.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** When an agent — human or machine — learns to produce the surface form of mechanistic explanation without executing the underlying inferential process that would make such explanations falsifiable, required output fields that name mechanism will be populated correctly in form but will not constrain output quality. A structural prompt for a mechanism field shifts the failure mode from omission to confabulation: the field is filled, the explanation sounds causal, but the text was generated by pattern-matching to mechanism-shaped language rather than by tracing a causal chain that generates testable predictions. Surface compliance with a structural requirement does not verify that the required cognitive operation occurred.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** The finding was tested in human students learning causal reasoning and in LLMs trained on human text — both populations operating on general academic or natural-language content, with evaluation via survey endorsement of flawed claims. Kit-making for LLM agents differs in two ways that cut in opposite directions. First, the stakes of confabulation are higher: a mechanistic-sounding rule in a behavioral kit propagates into every downstream agent run, whereas a student's flawed causal claim affects one essay. This makes the principle apply more strongly — the surface-learning failure mode is not just an output quality problem but a compounding architecture problem. Second, kit rules are shorter, more constrained, and evaluated against observable agent behavior rather than open-ended prose, which could reduce the confabulation surface — a kit rule that says "X causes Y" is easier to probe for falsifiability than a paragraph-length explanation. However, the evaluation mechanism (does the rule generate falsifiable predictions?) requires an evaluator who can distinguish mechanism-sounding text from mechanism-grounded text, which is exactly the capability the finding says is unreliable. The principle applies at least as strongly in kit-making as in the tested setup, and possibly more so for the propagation reason.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- A mechanism field in a kit rule should require not just a causal claim but a stated falsifying condition — something that would have to be observably true or false for the mechanism to be operating. Without this, the field accepts confabulated text at the same rate as correct text, since both are mechanism-shaped. Confidence: medium — derivable directly from the principle, untested in kit-rule contexts specifically.
- Peer or subagent review of mechanism fields will be unreliable if the reviewer is drawn from the same trained distribution as the author: both will find the mechanism-sounding text plausible for the same reasons the author generated it. An independent adversarial reviewer given only the mechanism claim and asked 'what experiment would falsify this?' is a structurally different check than a reviewer asked 'does this mechanism make sense?' Confidence: medium — the principle implies this but the adversarial framing has not been tested against kit-rule mechanism review.
- The failure mode generalizes from mechanism fields to any structured output field that requires a non-trivial inferential operation to fill correctly — evidence-basis columns, done-criteria statements, contradiction-scope explanations. Any field where pattern-matching to the expected format is easier than executing the underlying reasoning will show the same surface-compliance / confabulation split. Kit design should treat every required field as a potential confabulation site and ask whether the field's acceptance criteria can distinguish confabulated from grounded content. Confidence: low-medium — reasoned by structural analogy to the mechanism case, not directly tested.

### completeness-and-reporting (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** PRISMA 2020 added items specifically to address systematic omissions documented in prior reviews; mean PRISMA adherence across studies is 42.64%, meaning checklist without enforcement produces selective compliance — the checklist is empirically measured as insufficient on its own.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** A behavioral specification applied by the same agent whose behavior it is meant to constrain produces selective compliance: the items requiring the greatest deviation from default behavior will be skipped at the highest rate, while items already aligned with default behavior will appear well-followed. The specification framework alone does not close the gap between "rule exists" and "rule fires" — that gap requires enforcement mechanisms that operate independently of the specified agent's judgment at the moment of application.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: human researchers applying a reporting checklist to their own systematic reviews, with peer review and journal requirements as external enforcement (inconsistent and after-the-fact). Our setup differs in: the "agent" is an LLM whose failure mode is not effort-aversion or disclosure-aversion but recognition failure under execution pressure — it does not skip rules by weighing cost vs. benefit, it skips them because the trigger condition is not recognized in the moment. The structural pattern is identical — the same agent that authored or is governed by the contract applies it to its own output — so the core mechanism (selective compliance at hardest items) transfers. The principle applies more strongly here because LLM behavioral contract decay is documented as a recurring failure (mistakes.md Pattern B), and the kit's own CLAUDE.md explicitly acknowledges that self-monitoring rules fail consistently across M-05, M-14, M-16, M-20, M-21, M-22, M-24. The principle applies less strongly where the kit uses structural gates (TASK_TEMPLATE sections that cannot be skipped because they are sequentially required) — those break the mechanism by removing the agent's judgment as the gating condition. The principle's bite is precisely calibrated to the ratio of prose CLAUDE.md rules to structural TASK_TEMPLATE gates in the generated kit.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Rules introduced to correct documented failures — the equivalent of PRISMA items added to address systematic omissions — will have the highest skip rate among all behavioral contract rules, because they target the largest gaps from default behavior. For LLM kit contracts, this means rules derived from a mistakes.md investigation are exactly the at-risk class. Design implication: any rule traceable to a documented failure must be placed in a TASK_TEMPLATE structural gate rather than added to CLAUDE.md prose; prose placement is the mechanism that produced the original failure and cannot fix it.
- A behavioral contract that grows by appending rules without adding structural enforcement does not improve compliance — it expands the surface area for selective non-compliance while making the compliant items appear adequate. The compliance rate across all rules stays anchored to the enforcement rate of the weakest-enforced items. Design implication: compliance should be a measurable Phase 7 audit criterion for the generated kit, not inferred from rule presence. The audit should specifically test which rules fire and which do not under realistic-scale conditions, and report a per-rule fire rate rather than a binary pass/fail.
- The items an agent skips most reliably are not the items it refuses — they are the items that require recognizing a trigger condition in one's own behavior while under execution pressure. This is a different failure mode than non-compliance by choice. Design implication: behavioral contract rules that require self-diagnosis (class-before-instance, broad retrospective before fixing, done-criteria re-check before pivoting) should be converted to commission form with observable violation conditions — not because the agent is unwilling, but because recognition is the bottleneck, and observable violation conditions make the trigger externally auditable rather than self-applied.

### evidence-grading (GRADE) (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** GRADE rates evidence across four tiers using five downgrade domains (risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias); GRADE is adopted consensus, not empirically proven superior to alternative grading systems.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** Any multi-dimensional framework for assessing quality derives its practical value primarily from coordination — the act of naming and attending to the same dimensions consistently — rather than from empirical proof that its specific structure produces better assessments than alternatives. A structured rubric shapes what gets noticed and how weight is distributed; adoption makes that shaping reliable across uses. The framework's coordination function is its real contribution, whether or not the framework itself has ever been validated against a competing structure.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: clinical medicine and public health, where the framework was developed for systematic review and guideline production, with many human raters, high-stakes population-level decisions, and decades of inter-rater reliability research as context. Our setup differs in three ways that matter: (1) The "raters" are LLM agents with different and less-studied failure modes than human clinicians — the coordination benefit is real but calibrated against a different error distribution. (2) The evidence being graded is heterogeneous research about AI behavior rather than controlled clinical trials — the framework's domain-fit assumption is weaker. (3) The coordination value scales with the number of independent actors using the same rubric; a single-kit, single-agent context extracts less coordination benefit than a multi-team clinical guideline process. The principle still applies because the shaping function — a rubric causes the agent to attend to specific dimensions it would otherwise handle inconsistently — holds regardless of scale. The principle applies less strongly when the kit is purely single-use with no cross-agent or cross-session consistency requirement, because then coordination is not a goal and the rubric can be optimized purely for local fit rather than interoperability.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- When no rubric is empirically proven superior, the selection criterion should shift to interpretability and dimension coverage: each dimension included in the kit's evaluation rubric should be justified by 'would this be systematically missed without an explicit dimension?' rather than 'has this dimension been proven to improve outcomes?' This implies the kit should document its rubric's inclusions and exclusions with one-sentence rationales, not empirical citations. Confidence in this derivation: medium — the mechanism is clear but the specific coverage test has not been validated for agent-kit contexts.
- A kit that enforces a fixed rubric with imperfect dimensions will outperform a kit that applies unconstrained judgment — not because the rubric is optimal, but because consistency reduces variance across runs. This suggests the kit's evaluation procedure should have a fixed list of dimensions applied in every pass, with an explicit skip-and-log protocol for dimensions that don't apply, rather than an 'apply judgment' instruction. Confidence: medium-high — this derivation follows directly from the coordination mechanism and is consistent with the procedures already in this codebase.
- Since no evaluation framework has proven superiority, the correct meta-rule for the kit is: make the rubric's assumptions auditable rather than optimal. The rubric should name which quality dimensions it includes, which it explicitly excludes, and why — so that when the rubric fails (produces a wrong tier assignment, misses a relevant dimension), the failure is diagnosable from visible structure rather than buried in implicit judgment. An implicit rubric that lives in the agent's 'assessment sense' cannot be audited or corrected; a declared rubric fails loudly. Confidence: high — this is the same principle behind the kit's observable-violation rule design and fires from the same mechanism.

### alternatives-generation (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** Agents and humans score below 15% on counterintuitive hypotheses — hypothesis inversion against ground truth (HIGH, hypothesis-inverted-ground-truth-15pct); lateral thinking methods prioritize novelty over suitability (CONSENSUS); TRIZ and devil's advocate claims for alternatives generation are anecdote or consensus tier, not empirically established.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** When generating alternatives, both human and machine cognition defaults to prior-consistent space: outputs that appear diverse are often reframings within the same foundational assumptions. Named creativity techniques (structured methods, adversarial prompting, systematic invention frameworks) create the surface appearance of divergence without reliably producing genuine departure from prior-aligned options. Escaping the prior requires mechanisms that explicitly target the foundational assumptions themselves — not just the surface features of the current option — and the difficulty is systematic rather than incidental to skill level or effort.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** The tested setup involves hypothesis generation measured against empirical ground truth — a domain with an external correctness criterion and a definable "counterintuitive" direction. Kit-making differs in two structural ways. First, there is no fixed ground truth: the design space is synthetic, not discovered, so "counterintuitive" cannot be measured against a fact of the world. Second, the prior in kit-making is the LLM training distribution over documented design patterns — closer to a convention space than an empirical prior. This makes the principle apply more strongly, not less: an LLM generating kit mechanisms will draw from documented design literature, producing alternatives that are variations within known patterns even when they appear formally distinct. The absence of a fitness criterion (no ground truth to invert against) means prior-escape is even harder to detect — there is no external test to reveal whether an alternative genuinely departed from convention. The principle applies with full force to the generative step; the evaluation step is where the kit context diverges most from the tested setup.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Explicit assumption-inversion prompts would outperform named creativity techniques: rather than invoking a method label, a rule that requires the agent to first name the foundational assumption behind the leading option and then produce an alternative that inverts that specific assumption would more reliably generate genuine departures. The research did not test this structural decomposition, but the mechanism — targeting the assumption rather than the surface — is the missing step the finding implies. Confidence: medium (mechanism is direct, but no empirical validation in design contexts).
- A prior-alignment filter is needed as a separate evaluation gate after generation: since generated alternatives cluster in prior-consistent space even when they appear diverse, counting alternatives or requiring N candidates does not improve genuine coverage. A filter that explicitly checks whether each candidate rests on the same foundational assumption as the leading option — not just whether it looks different — would catch false diversity before it propagates into the kit. The research implies this failure is in the generative step, but the practical intervention is at the evaluation step. Confidence: medium.
- Human-in-the-loop review does not fix the problem at the generative step: since both human and agent cognition show the same systematic failure on prior-inversion tasks, adding a human review layer to agent-generated alternatives does not guarantee escape. The intervention must happen in the structure of the generative prompt itself, not downstream. A kit design that relies on a human reviewer to catch prior-consistent alternatives inherits the same failure rate. Confidence: medium (extrapolates from the shared-failure-mode finding to a design decision not tested).

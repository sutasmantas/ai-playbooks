## 1. Failed-Query Diagnosis

When a search returns fewer than 5 useful results, the cause is almost always one of four structural failures. Distinguishing them requires a diagnostic probe, not just re-running the query.

### 1.1 Too Narrow

**Signal pattern:**  
- Zero results, or results are all from the same 1-2 papers citing each other  
- Query contains multiple specific qualifiers (method + application domain + technique variant)  
- Results cluster around a single narrow sub-problem

**Distinguishing test:**  
Remove one qualifier at a time. If removing a single term jumps results from <5 to >20, that term was the bottleneck. If results remain low after removing any single term, the whole phrase is the problem (it may not exist as a category in this literature).

**Typical cause structure:**  
You have pre-combined two concepts that the literature keeps separate. Papers address each half but never use both terms together. Example: "few-shot chain-of-thought for biomedical NER" — the literature covers chain-of-thought and biomedical NER separately but rarely combines those exact strings.

### 1.2 Too Broad

**Signal pattern:**  
- Many results (>50) but low relevance density — most are tangential  
- Results span wildly different disciplines or application contexts  
- Skimming 10 results yields fewer than 2 useful ones

**Distinguishing test:**  
Look at the titles of the top 10 results. If they map to 5+ different application domains, the query has no discriminating terms. The query is returning everything that mentions the general concept, not the specific intersection you care about.

**Typical cause structure:**  
The query uses only high-level category terms. "Retrieval augmented generation" alone is now so broad it returns papers from medicine, law, code generation, and general QA without distinction.

### 1.3 Vocabulary Mismatch

**Signal pattern:**  
- Results are plausible in topic but slightly off — the papers address something adjacent  
- The few relevant results you do find use different terminology than your query  
- Your query terms appear in the abstract only in quotation marks (i.e., being critiqued or compared against, not used)

**Distinguishing test:**  
Take the 1-2 results that are closest to what you want. Read their abstracts and extract their terminology for the concept you're searching for. If that terminology differs from your query terms, you have a vocabulary mismatch, not a coverage gap.

**Typical cause structure:**  
Your vocabulary comes from adjacent fields or from popularized terminology (blog posts, industry usage) that doesn't match the academic term. Example: searching "hallucination detection" when the literature uses "factuality verification" or "faithfulness evaluation." Or searching "memory in LLMs" when the literature says "in-context learning" or "retrieval augmentation."

### 1.4 Source Gap

**Signal pattern:**  
- Consistent low yield across multiple reformulations on one source  
- A web search on the topic finds relevant content but it's in a different format (GitHub repos, documentation, blog posts, forum threads)  
- The topic is recent (<12 months) and preprint servers have it but indexing is behind  
- The topic is applied/engineering-focused (implementation, tooling, benchmarks) rather than research-focused

**Distinguishing test:**  
Run the same core terms in a web search. If you immediately find 5+ relevant sources that are blogs, repos, or documentation rather than papers, the topic lives outside academic literature. If web search also fails, the topic may genuinely not be documented anywhere publicly.

## 2. Recovery Tactics Per Failure Mode

### 2.1 Too Narrow — Broadening Strategies

**Principle:** Relax qualifiers in reverse order of importance. Keep the concept you care most about; relax the application domain, then the method variant, then the technique name.

**Sequence:**
1. Remove the most application-specific term first (domain noun: "biomedical," "legal," "code"). The literature usually generalizes methods and applies them separately.
2. If still low yield, remove the method variant and search for the parent category ("chain-of-thought" → "prompting methods").
3. If still low yield, move to synonym expansion (see 2.2 below — at this point you may have crossed into vocabulary mismatch).

**Category widening rule:**  
If you searched for a named technique (e.g., "Constitutional AI"), also search for its functional description ("RLHF with AI feedback," "self-critique fine-tuning"). Papers that predate the named technique will use functional descriptions.

**Temporal split:**  
When a technique has a well-known name, split queries: one for papers before the naming paper (using functional description), one for papers citing the naming paper. This catches the full lineage.

### 2.2 Vocabulary Mismatch — Finding Field Terminology

**Principle:** Use the literature itself to teach you its vocabulary before committing to a search strategy.

**Probe sequence:**
1. Run your intuitive query. Take the 1-3 closest results.
2. Open each abstract and highlight the noun phrases the authors use for the concept you searched for.
3. Note what those phrases are and whether they cluster. If they cluster (2 of 3 papers use "faithfulness evaluation"), you've found the field term. If they scatter (3 different terms), run step 4.
4. Find a survey paper in the area. Surveys explicitly enumerate terminology and note that "term A is also called B and C in different subfields." A survey abstract is a vocabulary mapping.
5. Check Wikipedia's "see also" section for the concept. Wikipedia editors often link the popularized term to the academic term.

**Seed paper method:**  
Find one paper that is definitely relevant (even if found through a weak search). Extract all its keywords, abstract noun phrases, and title terms. Build your refined query from those, not from your original vocabulary.

**Cross-disciplinary collision:**  
Sometimes the same concept has two vocabulary traditions (CS and cognitive science; ML and statistics; NLP and linguistics). If your seed papers come from two different traditions, you'll need two separate queries.

### 2.3 Source Gap — Switching Sources

**Detection signals:**
- The topic is a tool, library, or benchmark (source: GitHub, Papers With Code)
- The topic is a technique released by a company in a blog post or technical report rather than a peer-reviewed paper (source: web search, arXiv for tech reports)
- The topic is a practitioner pattern or deployment practice (source: technical blogs, Stack Overflow, GitHub discussions)
- The topic is so recent it only exists in preprints (source: arXiv directly, not Semantic Scholar which indexes with lag)
- The topic is an open problem or negative result (source: workshop papers, which are underrepresented in standard search)

**Decision rule:**  
If you've reformulated 3 times on a source and yield is still <5, assume source gap and switch before reformulating further. Each additional reformulation on the wrong source is wasted effort.

**Source switching sequence:**  
`arXiv` → `Semantic Scholar` → `Papers With Code` → `web search` → `GitHub search` → `Google Scholar` (for older or non-preprint literature)

## 3. Cross-Source Query Translation

### 3.1 arXiv → Semantic Scholar

**What's the same:**  
The core terms and Boolean logic translate directly. Most academic vocabulary works on both.

**What gets lost:**
- arXiv category qualifiers (`cs.CL`, `cs.LG`) have no equivalent on Semantic Scholar. Semantic Scholar uses field-of-study filters but they're coarser and less reliable.
- arXiv date filters (`submittedDate:[20230101 TO 20240101]`) work precisely. Semantic Scholar's year filter is less granular.
- arXiv allows title-specific search (`ti:` prefix). Semantic Scholar searches full text by default, increasing noise.

**What needs to be added:**
- Add explicit disambiguation terms. Semantic Scholar's broader index means your query may pull papers from medicine or social science that happen to use the same words. Add a CS/ML scope term if not present.
- Use Semantic Scholar's citation count filter to proxy quality. arXiv has no such filter (papers are preprints). On Semantic Scholar, filtering to >10 citations can remove noise but will exclude very recent work.

**Translation example:**
- arXiv: `ti:("retrieval augmented generation") cat:cs.CL submittedDate:[20230101 TO 20241231]`
- Semantic Scholar: `"retrieval augmented generation" "language model"` + field filter: Computer Science + year: 2023-2024

### 3.2 arXiv → GitHub

**Fundamental shift:**  
arXiv finds claims and methods. GitHub finds implementations and tools. These are different artifact types. A successful arXiv query doesn't translate — it transforms.

**Translation logic:**
1. Extract the named technique or method from your successful arXiv query.
2. Search GitHub for: `<technique name>` in repository names and descriptions. Named techniques become library names.
3. Add `implementation` or the language name (`python`) to filter out forks and mirrors.
4. Papers with code often have GitHub links directly — use Papers With Code as an intermediate step.

**What gets lost:**  
Field-specific qualifiers are useless on GitHub. "Biomedical NER" won't appear in a repo name — but "named entity recognition" or "NER" will. Strip the application domain from the search; application-specific repos are found by browsing topics or READMEs, not by query.

**What needs to be added:**  
Language specifier, recency filter (GitHub sorts by stars by default, not relevance), and sometimes the framework name (PyTorch, HuggingFace Transformers) to exclude toy implementations.

### 3.3 arXiv → Web Search

**Translation logic:**
1. Keep the technique name and core concept terms.
2. Drop all academic qualifiers. Web search doesn't understand `cs.CL` and will treat it as a literal string.
3. Add context terms that appear in non-academic writing: "tutorial," "guide," "how to," or the framework name.
4. For finding the "why it works" intuition, add "explained" or "intuition."
5. For finding negative results or failures, add "limitations," "problems," or "doesn't work."

**What gets lost:**  
Precision. Web search will return blog posts, tutorials, forum posts, and Wikipedia alongside papers. You gain breadth and practical documentation; you lose controlled vocabulary.

**What needs to be added:**  
Site-specific filters when you know where the knowledge lives: `site:huggingface.co`, `site:github.com`, `site:arxiv.org` (to scope back to papers from a web search context). Also: adding the year in natural language ("2024") to avoid older or obsolete coverage.

**Quality proxy:**  
On web search, publication source proxies quality. Prioritize results from established research blogs (Distill, Lilian Weng's blog, Sebastian Ruder's blog), official documentation, and company research pages over generic content farms.

### 3.4 What Gets Lost in Translation (Summary Table)

| From | To | Primary Loss | Primary Gain |
|------|-----|-------------|--------------|
| arXiv | Semantic Scholar | Category precision, date granularity | Citation counts, cross-field coverage |
| arXiv | GitHub | All field qualifiers, theoretical framing | Implementation artifacts, runnable code |
| arXiv | Web search | Controlled vocabulary, precision | Practical usage, tutorials, informal docs |
| Semantic Scholar | arXiv | Quality filters | Recency, category specificity |
| GitHub | arXiv | — | Theoretical basis for the implementation |

## 4. Iterative Refinement Pattern

A good 3-step refinement sequence follows: diagnose → calibrate → verify.

### Step 1: Diagnostic query (broad, intentionally over-inclusive)
Purpose: establish vocabulary, see what the field looks like, identify which papers are central.

Format: 2-3 core concept terms, no application domain qualifiers, no method variant specifiers.

Expected result: 20-100 results with mixed relevance. You are not trying to get good results here. You are mapping the space.

Action: Skim the top 15 results. Extract vocabulary. Find 1-2 seed papers that are clearly relevant. Note what terms they use.

### Step 2: Calibration query (targeted, uses extracted vocabulary)
Purpose: test whether field vocabulary dramatically improves precision.

Format: Replace your original terms with the field's preferred terms. Add one discriminating qualifier from your seed papers. Do not yet add all qualifiers — one at a time.

Expected result: 10-30 results with high relevance density. If density is high but count is low, you may have over-specified; relax one term. If count is high but density is still low, add another qualifier.

Action: Read abstracts of top 10. Identify which qualifier would most cleanly separate relevant from irrelevant. Note any papers you want to cite — these confirm you're in the right space.

### Step 3: Verification query (final, near-final formulation)
Purpose: confirm coverage and catch papers missed by vocabulary choices.

Format: Run 2-3 synonym variants of your best calibration query. This is not trying to find more papers — it's checking that you haven't created a vocabulary island.

Expected result: Mostly overlap with step 2 results, plus a small number (<5) of new relevant papers. If you find many new relevant papers, your step 2 vocabulary was too narrow — step 2 missed a whole sub-community.

Action: If synonym variants yield >10 new relevant papers, treat this as evidence of vocabulary mismatch (you found one island but missed another). Iterate step 2 again with the new vocabulary. Otherwise, declare the query stable.

## 5. When to Abandon vs. Persevere

### Persevere signals

- You've tried fewer than 3 reformulations — not enough attempts to diagnose failure mode
- Diagnostic probes (dropping terms one at a time) show results changing significantly — the query can be tuned
- You found 1-2 papers that are clearly relevant — the topic exists in the source, it's a query formulation problem
- The failure is likely vocabulary mismatch (field terminology not yet identified) — vocabulary mismatch is fixable with the seed paper method

### Abandon (switch source or query) signals

- 3+ reformulations all return <5 useful results
- Removing qualifiers one at a time produces no result improvement
- A web search on the same topic immediately surfaces relevant content in a different format (blogs, repos, docs) — source gap confirmed
- The closest results are consistently adjacent but never on-target, and their terminology doesn't converge on a shared vocabulary — the sub-topic may not have reached academic formalization yet
- You're on the 4th reformulation and the results are getting worse, not better — you are drifting away from the useful neighborhood

### Hard cut-off rule

After 3 reformulations with no improvement and no vocabulary signal from the returned papers, the query has failed. Spend one more probe on source-switching (web search), then either: (a) declare source gap and switch, or (b) log the angle as "not yet in literature" and move on. Continuing to reformulate past this point has lower expected value than any adjacent angle.

### The "proximity trap"

A specific failure mode: results are always "almost right" — close enough to feel promising, not close enough to be useful. This is the most dangerous state because it keeps you reformulating indefinitely. Diagnosis: if after 5 reformulations you still describe results as "close but not quite," the topic is either too new for the literature or lives at an intersection that has no dedicated literature. Switch to a decomposition strategy: search for each component separately and synthesize.

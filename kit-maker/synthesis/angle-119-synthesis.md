## Step 3.7 — Principled Derivation (2026-07-05)

### proactive-advisor-behavior (HIGH)
**Finding (data point):** Reliable proactive advisor behavior requires structural reinforcement beyond instruction-level prompting — behavioral contracts alone are insufficient for spontaneous initiative. Empirical evidence: F1=66.47% from ProactiveBench training study (arXiv:2410.12361) and 85.19% task completion from Intent-Conditioned Monitoring study (arXiv:2601.09382). Explicit continuation triggers are a separate mechanism from spontaneous initiative and have no controlled-experiment backing; practitioner consensus supports them for explicit-trigger compliance only.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** When a behavior requires an agent to self-recognize the appropriate moment to act — without an external prompt, event, or structural condition firing — instruction-level rules alone are insufficient to sustain that behavior reliably. Recognition of "now is the time to initiate" competes with primary-task attention and degrades under execution pressure. The mechanism class that produces reliable self-initiated behavior is structural: conditions that fire automatically from observable workflow events, removing the recognition step entirely. The distinction is between a rule the agent must decide to apply and a gate the workflow forces the agent through.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: agentic single-agent settings where proactive initiative (unprompted clarification, risk-flagging, agenda-setting) was measured against human-judged correctness on standardized task sets. Our setup differs in two critical ways. First, we are one level removed — we are writing the behavioral contract that will govern a different agent's proactivity, not exercising proactivity ourselves. The research measured the governed agent's failure rate; we are designing the governance structure. This makes the principle apply MORE strongly: if we write "be proactive" into a generated kit's CLAUDE.md, we are deliberately reproducing the exact mechanism the research identified as insufficient. Second, kit-making agents face longer, higher-cognitive-load primary tasks (multi-phase research and synthesis pipelines) than the task-suite benchmarks tested, which increases the recognition-load competition effect. The principle may apply less strongly in contexts where primary-task cognitive load is very low and sessions are short — but those conditions are not characteristic of kit use. The principle applies at full strength here; our design target is to build structural triggers, not instruction-level "be proactive" rules, into every kit we generate.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- The failure mode generalizes beyond advisor behavior to every rule in a behavioral contract that takes the form 'when you notice X, do Y.' Any such rule requires self-recognition of X under execution pressure, which is the same failure mechanism identified for spontaneous initiative. The design implication: behavioral contracts should classify each rule by trigger type (structural/event-fired vs. recognition-required) and convert recognition-required rules to structural form wherever a workflow event exists that could fire them. A contract with high structural-trigger coverage is categorically stronger than one with high rule count but recognition-required firing. Confidence: high — the mechanism (recognition-load competition under execution pressure) is the same; the finding's scope was spontaneous initiative specifically, but the mechanism does not respect that boundary.
- Instruction-level 'be proactive' rules and structural triggers are likely not additive — they may interact adversarially. A contract that contains both creates a false-coverage effect: the designer believes proactivity is addressed (instruction is present), which reduces pressure to add structural enforcement. The instruction-level rule provides no additional compliance signal when structural triggers already exist, and when structural triggers are absent the instruction-level rule substitutes nothing. The design implication: treat instruction-level initiative rules as indicators of missing structural triggers, not as coverage. When reviewing a generated kit behavioral contract, every 'be proactive' or 'surface problems without being asked' rule should trigger a search for the structural gate that should replace it. Confidence: medium — the mechanism is plausible, but the adversarial interaction specifically has not been tested.
- If the structural/recognition distinction is real, then behavioral contract quality cannot be measured by rule count or coverage breadth alone — it requires a structural-enforceability audit: for each rule, can a fresh agent reading the contract determine, from observable workflow state alone, whether the rule should have fired in a given turn? Rules that require inferred mental state ('I should have noticed this') are structurally unenforceable and degrade predictably; rules that require observable event presence ('Section 6 was marked complete') are structurally enforceable. A kit whose contract passes a structural-enforceability audit — where a third-party auditor can assign fired/not-fired to each rule from the task record alone — provides stronger behavioral guarantees than one with more rules but lower enforceability. This implies the external-auditor mechanism (A1) is not just a quality check but the enforceability test itself. Confidence: medium — the enforceability-audit concept is derivable from the principle, but the threshold for 'adequate' structural coverage is unknown and not resolvable from the current evidence.

### advisor-executor-framing (HIGH)
**Finding (data point):** In a controlled negotiation study (arXiv:2602.12089), delegate framing (act-first) yields approximately 1.5x welfare gains over advisor framing (recommend-first), with 44% of participants preferring the advisor role — meaning the action-first framing produces better outcomes despite lower stated preference. Implication for behavioral contracts: default to act-first (delegate) framing; reserve advisor framing for scope-decision moments only.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** When the path from intent to action passes through fewer interpretation handoffs, outcomes improve — even when participants prefer the more mediated, advisory role. Stated preference for a recommend-first role reflects comfort or familiarity, not performance; the framing that feels more comfortable introduces an interpretive gap between intent and outcome that the act-first framing eliminates. Each additional layer between the originating intent and the executed action is a site for drift, attenuation, or misalignment — not a quality control step.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: human-to-human negotiation with financial stakes, two-party interaction, real psychological commitment mechanisms, measurable welfare outcomes. Our setup differs in three ways: (1) LLM agents do not have genuine role preferences — "framing" operates through the prompt, not through felt comfort, so the stated-preference gap from the study has no direct analogue; the mechanism of action is prompt-induced framing bias rather than psychological self-assignment. (2) Kit-making involves primarily single-agent execution against a behavioral contract, not a two-party exchange — the "recommendation bottleneck" is internal (the agent recommending to itself before acting) rather than interpersonal. (3) Kit-making outcome quality is multidimensional and harder to measure than negotiation welfare, so the magnitude of the effect cannot be assumed to match the study's figure. The principle still applies because the underlying mechanism — fewer interpretive handoffs between intent and action — is structurally present in both cases: an advisory-framed agent generates a recommendation, holds for an implicit decision-maker, then acts, introducing attenuation at each step. The principle applies less strongly if the kit domain genuinely requires human judgment at a decision point (irreversible actions, scope decisions), in which case the advisory handoff is not a bottleneck — it is a necessary routing to the actual decision-maker.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Rule surface form: The principle implies that behavioral contract rules should be written in commission form (direct action statements) rather than advisory form (recommendation or consideration statements). A rule phrased as 'consider surfacing X to the user' introduces a mediation step the agent must resolve before acting — the same interpretive gap the finding flags. Commission form ('fix visible problems inline') eliminates that step. Confidence: high — the mechanism maps directly onto the principle and is independently supported by instruction-following specificity research.
- Check-in gate calibration: The principle implies that any behavioral contract mechanism requiring the agent to pause and verify before acting adds an interpretation handoff that degrades outcomes for routine decisions. The check-in gate should be designed to be asymmetric: the default posture is act, and the gate fires only when an observable condition is met (irreversibility, scope boundary, or user-only information). A gate that fires on 'uncertainty' or 'importance' routes too many decisions through the advisory layer and compounds the attenuation effect. Confidence: medium — the mechanism is sound but the exact gate calibration requires testing in-domain.
- User preference as unreliable signal: The principle implies that when users state a preference for more advisory behavior from the agent ('check with me more often', 'recommend before acting'), that stated preference is not evidence that advisory behavior produces the outcomes they value. The kit designer faces a meta-level version of the same finding: comfort preference and performance preference diverge. This suggests that when a user requests more check-ins, the correct response is to tighten the scope of what requires a check-in (improving signal quality) rather than increasing check-in frequency (which increases the advisory-framing surface). Confidence: medium — reasoned from the principle; not directly tested in the study.

### goal-directedness-measurement (HIGH)
**Finding (data point):** Behavioral observation alone is insufficient to verify goal-pursuit — the mechanistic split confirmed at ICML 2026 (arXiv:2602.08964) shows an agent can appear goal-directed under observation without actually pursuing the goal reliably. Self-check rules in behavioral contracts must therefore target observable structural signals (action counts, continuation block present) rather than inferred mental states such as 'am I being advisor-mode?'

**Principle (Sub-step A):** When a system can satisfy a surface-level indicator of a property — appearing to exhibit X — independently of instantiating the underlying mechanism that produces X reliably, evaluating the system by checking that surface indicator generates systematic false positives. Reliable verification of any property therefore requires checking structural signals that are mechanistically upstream of that property: signals whose presence is causally coupled to the property's actual occurrence, not behavioral proxies that a system can satisfy by pattern-matching to the expected output form without having the property.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** The tested setup used mechanistic probing to confirm a split between surface goal-directed behavior and actual goal-pursuit in model internals — researchers could distinguish the two because they had access to representations below the behavioral output layer. Kit-making setup differs in two critical ways: (1) we cannot probe internals at all — we only see behavioral outputs, which means the appearance/mechanism gap is invisible by default; (2) the agent doing the self-evaluation and the agent subject to evaluation are the same system, so the failure modes that cause the property to be absent also cause the self-report of the property to be plausible-sounding. This makes the principle apply more strongly in kit-making than in the tested setup, not less — the gap is larger and less detectable. The principle's force weakens only in the narrow case where a kit structural gate is engineered so that completing it requires the actual mechanism (a gate that demands naming all sub-goals before proceeding cannot be satisfied without having actually scoped them), because in that case the structural signal and the property are mechanistically linked by construction rather than by inference.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Any behavioral contract rule that asks the agent to self-assess an internal state ('am I applying class-before-instance?', 'am I in advisor mode?') inherits this failure mode in full, because self-report of the state and actual presence of the state can diverge for the same reason behavioral observation diverged from goal-pursuit in the tested setup. The design implication: every such rule should be converted to a structural gate form — an observable completion condition (specific file entry exists, sub-goal list named verbatim, action count nonzero) — so that the check verifies something mechanistically upstream of the property, not the agent's account of having the property. Confidence in this derivation: high, because the mechanism (appearance/property decoupling) is identical; the only difference is the domain.
- Kit testing that presents only cases where surface compliance and mechanistic compliance coincide cannot distinguish the two — a kit that enforces mechanism and a kit that only shapes output will pass the same tests. This implies that at minimum one test case per behavioral rule must be adversarially constructed so that producing the expected-looking output does NOT require the underlying discipline; if the agent passes it anyway, the rule is enforcing mechanism; if it fails, the rule is only shaping surface form. Without this class of test, the kit is certified against appearance only. Confidence in this derivation: high, because it follows directly from the principle's implication about what surface-indicator checking cannot distinguish.
- External auditors that verify rule compliance by reading the agent's self-report of what it did inherit the same failure mode as the self-check they are meant to replace. The kit's existing external auditor pattern (CLAUDE.md A1) is correctly structured in requiring a fresh subagent, but is vulnerable here if the subagent is prompted to evaluate the agent's account rather than structural evidence. The derivation: auditor prompts should be directed at observable artifacts (files present, sections filled with content that requires the discipline to have occurred, action counts) and explicitly prohibited from treating agent self-report as evidence of compliance. Confidence in this derivation: medium-high; the mechanism is the same, but the degree to which current auditor prompts already do this is untested.

### goal-drift-mechanisms (HIGH)
**Finding (data point):** Five distinct drift mechanisms are empirically documented across multi-turn sessions: trajectory contamination from inherited context (HIGH), nondeterministic output even at temperature=0 (HIGH), context dilution of opening goal, value-conflict override, and distributional drift. These are separate phenomena that all converge on the same practical conclusion: wording alone cannot prevent drift; behavioral contracts must include explicit session-boundary re-anchoring triggers, and rules should be framed as value-aligned constraints rather than restrictions on helpfulness.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** In any system that produces outputs over an extended sequence while accumulating state, an objective encoded only at initialization progressively loses effective weight relative to accumulated context. The system's behavior increasingly reflects the statistical distribution of accumulated inputs rather than the founding constraint. No amount of precision in how the initial objective is worded can substitute for periodic reinjection of that objective as a live, active constraint at later points in the sequence. Additionally, a constraint framed in opposition to the system's embedded tendencies faces persistent internal counterpressure — framing the same constraint as an expression of those tendencies removes the counterpressure and makes adherence structurally self-reinforcing rather than structurally contested.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: multi-turn LLM inference sessions, where the session context is a single continuous window and drift accumulates within one session.

Our setup differs in: kit-making operates at a meta-level — we are not running sessions, we are designing the behavioral contract that will govern sessions run later by the generated kit. Each generated-kit session starts with the behavioral contract re-injected (via CLAUDE.md), which provides a structural re-anchoring point the tested setup lacked. This means the principle applies less strongly to the session-open moment (re-anchoring is structurally guaranteed there) and more strongly to mid-session and multi-session accumulation — which the kit's current design does not address beyond the session-start check.

Principle still applies because: the mechanism — accumulated state diluting initial objective weight — operates identically whether the sequence is turns in a session or tasks across sessions. A kit that encodes goals only at session-start and then adds 30+ procedural rules to the context window will experience the same dilution dynamic within a session.

Principle may not apply if: the generated kit's tasks are consistently short (few turns, task completes before dilution accumulates) or the user actively reintroduces the goal through queries frequently enough to serve as organic re-anchoring. In practice, the research-synthesis and code-review kits are long multi-turn operations — the condition for strong applicability is met.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Session-boundary re-anchoring (the finding's conclusion) is necessary but not sufficient — the principle implies mid-task re-anchoring checkpoints are also required. A kit that re-reads the goal model only at session start and then runs a 40-step research pipeline will experience goal dilution before the pipeline completes. The derivation: any task whose step count exceeds a threshold (roughly: where the goal statement reaches less than ~5% of total context tokens) requires an explicit mid-task reinjection trigger, not just a session-start read. Confidence in this derivation: medium — the dilution mechanism is sound, the specific threshold is a design judgment not validated by the finding.
- Rule density in the behavioral contract itself is a compounding drift risk. The principle implies that a 30-rule CLAUDE.md with one goal statement at the top replicates the same dilution structure the finding documented in sessions — the goal is statistically buried by procedural content. A leaner behavioral contract (per the kit-at-right-scope finding) is not just a maintenance preference; it is a drift-prevention mechanism. This derivation suggests a testable design rule: the goal model should appear more than once in the behavioral contract (at the top and as a brief reminder near the most complex procedural sections), and rule density above a threshold should trigger a culling pass before shipping. Confidence: medium — the mechanism is sound; the specific density threshold requires self-testing to calibrate.
- Value-aligned framing should be applied recursively to the re-anchoring mechanism itself. The finding established that rules framed as helpfulness restrictions face persistent counterpressure. The re-anchoring trigger ('stop and re-read the goal model') is itself a procedural constraint. If it is framed as an interruption or overhead, it faces exactly that counterpressure. The derivation: re-anchoring language in the behavioral contract should be framed as enabling advisor-quality output ('reading the goal model produces the calibration needed to do this well') rather than as a required stop ('you must pause and check'). This is a specific application of the principle's second clause that the finding did not test. Confidence: medium — the framing mechanism is consistent with the value-alignment finding; direct measurement of re-anchoring framing effects is absent from the archive.

### goal-persistence-architecture (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** An external goal artifact that is always accessible and sized to fit context outperforms in-context goal declarations for persistence across multi-turn sessions — confirmed across two medium-empirical sources (subgoal-framework benchmark, goal-externalization-26pct vendor benchmark) and two practitioner-consensus sources (procedural-memory-as-system-prompt, plan-execute-separation). Practical implementation: the kit behavioral contract should end every response with an explicit continuation block (current goal, proposed next step) functioning as a persistent plan artifact.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** Any state that must survive across processing boundaries — turns, sessions, agent handoffs — requires externalization into a reliably-reloaded artifact sized to fit within the available processing window. Relying on in-context representation for persistence assumes continuity that is not guaranteed: context shifts, new content displaces earlier content, and the declaration of a goal at turn one does not constitute its presence at turn twenty. Persistence is an engineering property of the storage and retrieval mechanism, not a property of the declaration itself. The mechanism that makes state persistent is: (a) it lives outside the primary context, (b) it is loaded at every relevant boundary, and (c) it is small enough that loading it does not compete with task content for available space.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: single-agent multi-turn sessions where a goal was declared at session start and goal-following was measured across subsequent turns. The test setup is roughly: one agent, one goal, many turns, goal either in-context or in an external artifact. Our setup differs in three ways. First, we are not building a goal-persistence mechanism for one agent — we are designing behavioral contracts (kit files) that will be handed to future agents across unknown numbers of sessions, by users we cannot anticipate. The behavioral contract itself IS the external artifact; the question is whether it is designed to function as one (correct size, always loaded, contains the goal model). Second, our context includes multi-agent orchestration: subagents are dispatched and return; the parent agent's goal must be recoverable after each dispatch. The tested setup does not appear to include agent-handoff boundaries — only turn-level boundaries within one session. Third, the finding's implementation recommendation (a continuation block at the end of every response) addresses the session-turn boundary but not the agent-spawn boundary or the cross-session cold-start boundary. The principle applies more strongly in kit-making than in single-agent benchmarks: the kit must persist goals across all three boundary types simultaneously. The principle may apply less strongly if the agents using the generated kit have very large context windows relative to task content — in that case the competition-for-context-space mechanism is weaker, though the loading-at-boundary mechanism still holds.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Behavioral contract size is a persistence mechanism, not a readability preference. If the contract grows large enough to compete with task content for context space, loading it degrades goal persistence even when it is nominally present. The principle implies a hard size ceiling enforced as a persistence requirement: when the contract exceeds the ceiling, the culling pass is not optional cleanup — it is a restoration of the persistence mechanism. Confidence: high, because the mechanism (size-constrained loading) is the same mechanism as the finding, only applied to the artifact rather than to in-context declarations.
- Agent-handoff boundaries require explicit artifact re-injection, not assumed inheritance. When a subagent is dispatched, it does not inherit the parent agent's goal from context — it inherits only what is explicitly passed. The principle implies that goal continuity across agent spawns requires the behavioral contract (or a goal-summary extract) to be part of every subagent brief, not just available in the parent's session. A subagent that receives task instructions but not the goal artifact is operating with an in-context goal declaration — exactly the condition the finding shows is fragile. Confidence: medium, because the tested setup was single-agent; multi-agent handoff dynamics may introduce additional failure modes not captured by the principle alone.
- The goal-model re-read at session start is not an orientation ritual — it is the persistence mechanism firing. If it is advisory (the agent is told to re-read but is not structurally gated until it does), the mechanism is optional and degrades under execution pressure in the same way that in-context goal declarations degrade over turns. The principle implies the re-read should be a structural gate: no task execution until the goal artifact has been loaded this session. This is a design decision for the generated kit's behavioral contract — the 'goal-model-at-intake' rule must be enforced by a gated section in the task template, not by a CLAUDE.md advisory that fires only when recognized. Confidence: high for the gate requirement; the mechanism is directly analogous to the finding's externalization logic applied to within-session loading.

### instruction-file-design (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** A behavioral contract must be capped at approximately 120 lines and contain decision rules and continuation triggers only — architecture summaries, howto guides, onboarding material, and knowledge-base content must not appear in it, because decision rules and knowledge-base content have different decay rates and mixing them degrades both. Every rule must be a commitment with a detectable violation condition; a rule without a violation condition is unenforceable beyond good intentions.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** A governance artifact — any document whose purpose is to direct ongoing behavior — degrades when it conflates elements with different decay rates or different enforceability structures. Content that becomes obsolete on different schedules belongs in separate files, because an update forced by one type's obsolescence creates noise against the other type's stable signal, and readers cannot determine which parts retain current authority. Separately, every directive in a governing artifact must name an observable condition that would confirm it was violated; a directive without such a condition is not a commitment but an aspiration, and aspirations mixed into a commitment document erode the credibility of the actual commitments surrounding them. The two failure modes — scope incoherence (mixed decay rates) and enforceability incoherence (undetectable violation conditions) — are independent and can coexist silently until compliance degrades beyond recovery.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** The finding was observed in the context of human-authored behavioral contracts for LLM agents, where the artifact is read and acted on by the agent at session start. The kit-making setup differs in one important way: the artifact is not hand-authored but generated by a pipeline, which means the violations the finding names (architectural content drifting into the contract, rules without violation conditions) must be caught by the generator, not by the human author who would notice them during drafting. This makes the principle apply more strongly, not less — the generative context removes the human authoring step that would otherwise serve as a natural filter. The principle may apply less strongly in kits targeting very small or very stable domains where the decay-rate differential between decision rules and knowledge content is negligible in practice; but for any domain where the agent's operating architecture changes independently of its decision rules (which is the normal case for LLM agent deployments), the differential is acute and the principle applies at full strength.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- A structural gate in the kit-generation pipeline should scan every generated rule for an observable violation condition before the contract file is written — not as a post-hoc style check but as a pre-write gate that blocks emission of any rule that cannot answer the question 'what would I see in the output or context that tells me this rule was not followed?' Rules that fail the scan must be either rewritten with a named observable or moved to the design-rationale file as principles rather than commitments. Confidence in this derivation: high, because the mechanism (undetectable violations erode the credibility of detectable ones) operates independently of how the contract was authored.
- The decay-rate-separation principle implies that every generated kit should produce at minimum three distinct files with explicit scope declarations: one file for decision rules and continuation triggers (high enforceability, slow decay), one file for architecture and design rationale (low enforceability, medium decay), and one file for reference material and knowledge base (no enforceability, fast decay). Mixing these into fewer files should require an explicit logged exception with a stated reason, because the costs of mixing compound over the kit's operational life regardless of whether short-term performance is affected. Confidence: medium — the principle implies the separation, but the optimal file-count and boundary rules require testing against realistic kit scale.
- The enforceability-coherence requirement implies that the kit-maker's own procedures should be audited by the same criterion applied to generated contracts: every rule in CLAUDE.md or TASK_TEMPLATE.md that lacks a named observable violation condition is an aspiration, not a commitment, and degrades the enforcement density of the surrounding commitments. This is an application of the principle to the kit-maker itself rather than to its outputs — the kit should not generate contracts with violation conditions if it cannot demonstrate the same property in its own governing files. Confidence: high for the diagnostic, medium for the remediation (replacing aspirations with enforceable rules requires finding observable proxies, which may not exist for all rules).

### anti-decay-wording-patterns (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** Rules framed as outcomes ('the user should never reach the end of a response without a proposed next step') are more decay-resistant than rules framed as actions ('after each task, propose next step'), because the outcome form names a state the agent can detect a violation of. Naming the specific failure mode in the contract text creates a detection condition — the agent can recognize when it has drifted to chat-only mode because it has a named pattern to match against.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** An instruction's decay-resistance is a function of whether the executing agent can verify its own compliance from currently visible context. A rule that names an observable violation state gives the agent a detection condition it can match against its own output — making non-compliance recognizable without relying on memory of an obligation that was triggered earlier in the session. A rule that names only a required action without a checkable violation state gives the agent nothing to compare its current output against; compliance degrades as execution pressure mounts and the obligation fades from active attention. The precision of the named violation condition, not the forcefulness of the rule's language, determines how self-monitorable the rule is.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** The finding emerged from practitioner consensus about behavioral contract wording for general LLM agents. Kit-making differs in three ways that bear on applicability. First, kit agents operate across defined phases with phase-specific action patterns — failure modes are domain-specific (skipping synthesis before building, shipping placeholder sections, running Phase 6 without Phase 4) rather than generic chattiness. Domain-specific failure modes are harder to name precisely in advance, which makes the principle more consequential when applied well but more demanding to apply — the kit designer must anticipate concrete violation states per phase, not just name a generic "chat-only mode." Second, the principle applies recursively in kit-making: the kit's own behavioral contract (its CLAUDE.md) is itself a behavioral contract, so outcome-framed rules in the meta-contract should model the same pattern the generated kit's rules will need to follow; a meta-contract full of action-framed rules trains the wrong abstraction. Third, kit-generated rules will be executed by an agent that did not write them and cannot query the author for clarification — making upfront precision in naming violations more load-bearing than in interactive contexts where the user can correct mid-run. The principle applies more strongly in kit-making than in the tested context, not less, because there is no correction loop during execution.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Intake protocol rules should name the detectable end-state rather than the required action — for example, 'no tool call should be made while any essential unknown remains unresolved' rather than 'ask clarifying questions at intake.' This makes the gate self-checkable: the agent scans its pending tool calls against the named violation state, rather than trying to remember whether it asked questions at the right moment. The same mechanism applies to any phase-gate rule: name the state that would constitute a violation of the gate, not the action the agent was supposed to take.
- A behavioral contract with more than roughly ten rules should include a named-violation index — a compact list of the specific observable output states that constitute violations, kept separate from the rule prose and placed where an agent will encounter it before generating each response. This index functions as a pattern-matching library the agent can run against its own about-to-be-delivered output, rather than a set of obligations that must be held in working memory across turns. The index is not a summary of rules; it is a list of detectable states — the difference is that each entry is answerable by inspecting the current response, not by recalling whether an action was taken.
- For long multi-session tasks where context drift is high, outcome-framed rules have a session-recovery advantage over action-framed rules: an agent re-reading its contract after a context reset can reconstruct the violation-detection condition directly from the outcome framing, whereas an action-framed rule only recovers compliance if the agent also recovers memory of when the action was last supposed to fire. This implies that session-resume instructions should prioritize re-anchoring the agent to named violation states rather than re-stating action sequences — a resume protocol that says 'check whether any of the following states currently hold' is more robust than one that says 'remember to do X after every task.'

### goal-frame-design (CONSENSUS)
**Finding (data point):** Role declaration — explicitly naming the agent as advisor versus executor — filters which continuations the agent considers eligible; naming the role is mechanistically meaningful, not merely cosmetic. Outcome framing amplifies this effect. Mechanism verified; the specific quantitative example in the primary source was fabricated, but the mechanism itself is consistent with the proactive-advisor-behavior group findings.

**Principle (Sub-step A):** Explicit self-classification by an LLM — assigning itself a role label before generation begins — functions as a generative prior that narrows the candidate continuation space, causing role-inconsistent responses to be treated as ineligible before evaluation rather than downranked after it. This mechanism operates at the level of continuation selection, not reranking: the declared identity restricts which outputs enter consideration. Outcome framing (specifying what a successful outcome looks like in role-consistent terms) amplifies this by adding a second constraint on the candidate pool from the goal side, so both the origin-filter (role) and the target-filter (outcome) converge on the same region of continuation space.

**Fit to our setup (Sub-step B):** Tested in: single-agent instruction-following tasks where role was declared in a prompt and evaluated over short-to-medium horizons. Our setup differs in three material ways. First, the role declaration must persist across context resets and multi-session work — a one-time declaration in a CLAUDE.md behavioral contract competes with session-start priors and context-window pressure across many turns, not just one generation step. Second, kit-making produces agents that run on variable model tiers; the filtering mechanism may degrade more sharply on smaller models (≤7B) where the self-model is weaker and the prior from training distribution may override the declared role under pressure. Third, kit agents operate across distinct phases (researcher, synthesizer, builder, auditor), meaning the relevant role is not fixed but should shift with phase — a static top-level role declaration may not propagate the filtering effect to phase-local continuations. The principle still applies because the mechanism — self-classification as generative prior over continuations — is a property of autoregressive generation, not of any particular task type or duration. The principle would fail to apply if: the declared role conflicts sharply with the model's training-distribution priors (the declaration is overpowered rather than amplified), or if the role declaration falls out of effective attention during long-context runs before the relevant generation steps occur.

**Derivations beyond the finding (Sub-step C):**
- Per-instruction framing should compound the top-level role effect: if each task within the behavioral contract is phrased in role-consistent language ('diagnose the next obstacle' rather than 'complete this task'), the role-as-prior is re-injected at each generation boundary rather than only at session start — the filtering effect accumulates across instructions rather than decaying as the declaration recedes in context. Implication: behavioral contract wording should maintain role-consistent framing at the instruction level, not just in the preamble. Confidence: medium — the mechanism is consistent with the principle; the compound effect of repeated role-consistent framing within a single contract is unverified.
- Contradictory role signals within the same behavioral contract should partially cancel the filtering effect: if the top-level declaration names the advisor role but individual task phrasings are executor-shaped ('execute X', 'complete Y', 'produce artifact Z'), the net framing signal is mixed and the candidate pool narrows less sharply than a coherent declaration would produce. Implication: rule wording throughout the contract must be audited for role-coherence, not just the identity section — a single executor-framed instruction is a leak in the filter. Confidence: medium — follows from the mechanism; cross-signal cancellation is a standard framing-effects prediction, not directly tested in this context.
- Phase-specific role re-injection at phase-gate boundaries should produce phase-local filtering independent of the top-level declaration: if a phase-gate explicitly declares 'you are now functioning as auditor' before auditor-phase generation begins, the prior should shift to auditor-eligible continuations for that phase, the same way the top-level declaration shifts the global prior. Implication: phase-gate procedures should include an explicit role-reassignment statement at each transition, not merely a task description. Confidence: low — the derivation is mechanistically plausible but involves multiple intervening variables (context-window position, model tier, prior strength of the new role label).

# Research Angles: Comprehensive Retrieval in Systematic Literature Searches

**Topic:** How to achieve comprehensive retrieval in systematic literature searches.

---

## Angle 1: Database Selection and Coverage Breadth

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Library and Information Science; Evidence Synthesis methodology

**Rationale:** Comprehensiveness depends first on which databases are searched. Different databases cover different journals, disciplines, and time periods. Selecting the right combination is a foundational decision.

**Search queries:**
- `"systematic review" "database selection" "search coverage" bibliographic databases`
- `"information sources" "literature search" "database coverage" "systematic review methodology"`
- `"multiple databases" "search strategy" recall precision "systematic literature review"`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Journal of the Medical Library Association, Systematic Reviews, Research Synthesis Methods); Cochrane Handbook; Campbell Collaboration guidelines.

---

## Angle 2: Search String Construction and Query Formulation

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Information Retrieval; Systematic Review methodology

**Rationale:** The structure of a query — Boolean operators, truncation, wildcards, phrase searching — directly controls what is retrieved. This angle covers the syntax and strategy of query building.

**Search queries:**
- `"search strategy" "Boolean operators" "systematic review" "query construction"`
- `"PICO" "search terms" "MeSH terms" "free text" "systematic review"`
- `"search string" sensitivity specificity "literature review" retrieval`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (JAMIA, JMLA, Evidence-Based Library and Information Practice); Cochrane Handbook chapters; methodological guidelines from health technology assessment bodies.

---

## Angle 3: Controlled Vocabulary vs. Free-Text Searching

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Medical Informatics; Knowledge Organization; Library Science

**Rationale:** Thesauri and controlled vocabularies (MeSH, Emtree, LCSH) standardize terminology across indexed records, while free-text captures non-indexed concepts and newer terminology. The tradeoff between the two is a core comprehensiveness challenge.

**Search queries:**
- `"controlled vocabulary" "free text" "MeSH" "systematic review" retrieval comparison`
- `"thesaurus terms" "keyword search" "literature search" sensitivity recall`
- `"Emtree" OR "MeSH" "natural language" "search strategy" comprehensiveness`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology, Health Information & Libraries Journal); database vendor technical documentation; Cochrane and PRISMA guidance.

---

## Angle 4: Grey Literature Retrieval

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Evidence Synthesis; Public Health research methodology; Health Technology Assessment

**Rationale:** Published literature systematically over-represents positive findings. Grey literature (conference proceedings, dissertations, government reports, trial registries, preprints) fills this gap and is critical for unbiased comprehensiveness.

**Search queries:**
- `"grey literature" "systematic review" search strategy retrieval methods`
- `"unpublished studies" "conference proceedings" "trial registries" "literature search"`
- `"OpenGrey" OR "ProQuest Dissertations" OR "ClinicalTrials.gov" "systematic review" retrieval`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Systematic Reviews, Research Synthesis Methods); AHRQ and NICE methodological reports; ISSG Search Filter Resource; practitioner guides from CADTH and NICE.

---

## Angle 5: Citation Tracking and Snowballing

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Bibliometrics; Scientometrics; Systematic Review methodology

**Rationale:** Forward and backward citation chasing (snowballing) identifies literature missed by database queries because it was indexed under different terms or in databases not searched. It is a standard supplementary retrieval technique.

**Search queries:**
- `"citation tracking" "snowballing" "systematic review" "search strategy" recall`
- `"reference list checking" "forward citation" "backward citation" "literature search"`
- `"pearl growing" "cited reference searching" "systematic review" supplementary search`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Scientometrics, Systematic Reviews, JAMIA); methodological papers in Research Synthesis Methods; Cochrane Handbook.

---

## Angle 6: Search Filter Development and Validation

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Clinical Evidence Synthesis; Health Information Science

**Rationale:** Validated search filters (e.g., for RCTs, qualitative studies, diagnostic accuracy) are reusable query components that reliably retrieve a study type across databases. Understanding how filters are built and validated informs comprehensive retrieval design.

**Search queries:**
- `"search filter" validation "systematic review" sensitivity recall RCT`
- `"methodological filter" "hedges" "literature search" sensitivity specificity development`
- `"ISSG search filter resource" OR "InterTASC" "search filter" validation`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (JMLA, JAMIA, Systematic Reviews); ISSG Search Filter Resource database; Health Technology Assessment reports; Cochrane Bias Methods Group documentation.

---

## Angle 7: Recall-Precision Tradeoff in Information Retrieval

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Information Retrieval (Computer Science); Evaluation of IR systems

**Rationale:** Comprehensive retrieval maximizes recall at the cost of precision (noise). Understanding this tradeoff formally — including measures like sensitivity, specificity, and F-score — provides a theoretical grounding for systematic search design decisions.

**Search queries:**
- `"recall" "precision" tradeoff "information retrieval" "systematic review" evaluation`
- `"sensitivity" "specificity" literature search "systematic review" performance`
- `"comprehensiveness" "information retrieval" recall maximization "search strategy"`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Information Retrieval Journal, JASIST, ACM SIGIR proceedings); IR textbooks; methodological papers in systematic review journals.

---

## Angle 8: Text Mining and Automated / Semi-Automated Retrieval

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Natural Language Processing; Machine Learning for Evidence Synthesis; Computational Biomedical Informatics

**Rationale:** NLP and machine learning approaches — including active learning, classifier-assisted screening, and automated query expansion — are increasingly used to improve both recall and efficiency. This is a rapidly evolving angle.

**Search queries:**
- `"machine learning" "systematic review" "literature search" recall retrieval automation`
- `"active learning" "citation screening" "systematic review" automation`
- `"NLP" "text mining" "literature retrieval" "systematic review" coverage`

**Best source types:** Preprints (arXiv cs.IR, cs.CL); academic journals (JAMIA, Journal of Biomedical Informatics, Systematic Reviews); GitHub repositories of tools (Rayyan, Abstrackr, ASReview); conference proceedings (ACL, EMNLP).

---

## Angle 9: Reporting Standards and Search Transparency

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Research Synthesis methodology; Science Transparency and Reproducibility

**Rationale:** PRISMA, ROSES, and MOOSE reporting guidelines specify what constitutes an adequately documented and reproducible search. Audit criteria from these standards define minimum comprehensiveness benchmarks.

**Search queries:**
- `PRISMA "reporting guideline" "search strategy" completeness documentation`
- `"ROSES" OR "MOOSE" "reporting standard" "systematic review" search documentation`
- `"reproducibility" "literature search" "systematic review" reporting transparency`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (PLOS Medicine, BMJ, Systematic Reviews); EQUATOR Network resources; guidelines published by Cochrane and Campbell Collaboration.

---

## Angle 10: Language Bias and Non-English Literature Retrieval

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Research Synthesis methodology; Epidemiology; Publication Bias literature

**Rationale:** Restricting searches to English-language sources introduces systematic bias. This angle covers strategies for retrieving and handling multilingual literature, including translation and cross-lingual search.

**Search queries:**
- `"language bias" "systematic review" "non-English" "literature search" retrieval`
- `"multilingual" "cross-lingual" search "systematic review" comprehensiveness`
- `"language restriction" bias "meta-analysis" effect estimate distortion`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Systematic Reviews, Epidemiology, Research Synthesis Methods); Cochrane Handbook; meta-epidemiological studies.

---

## Angle 11: Failure Cases and Null Results — Incomplete Retrieval and Its Consequences

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Evidence Synthesis; Research Synthesis methodology; Meta-epidemiology

**Rationale:** This angle directly addresses what goes wrong when retrieval is incomplete: biased meta-analytic estimates, missed safety signals, erroneous conclusions. Documenting failure modes grounds the case for comprehensive search practice.

**Search queries:**
- `"incomplete retrieval" "systematic review" bias consequences OR impact`
- `"search comprehensiveness" failure "missed studies" "systematic review" effect`
- `"search error" OR "retrieval failure" "systematic review" replication audit`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Research Synthesis Methods, BMJ, Systematic Reviews); meta-epidemiological audit studies; replication studies; Cochrane Bias Methods Group publications.

---

## Angle 12: Iterative Search Refinement and Saturation Criteria

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Qualitative Research methodology; Grounded Theory; Systematic Review methodology

**Rationale:** Deciding when a search is "complete enough" borrows from qualitative saturation concepts and information-theoretic stopping rules. This angle covers iterative refinement, pilot searching, and operationalizing search saturation.

**Search queries:**
- `"search saturation" "literature review" "stopping rule" comprehensiveness criteria`
- `"iterative search" "systematic review" refinement pilot sensitivity`
- `"diminishing returns" "literature search" saturation "new studies" retrieval`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Qualitative Health Research, Journal of Mixed Methods Research, Systematic Reviews); methodological guidance documents; practitioner blogs in evidence synthesis.

---

## Angle 13: Peer Review of Search Strategies

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Library and Information Science; Evidence Synthesis quality assurance

**Rationale:** Independently reviewed search strategies (e.g., using PRESS checklist) catch structural errors that reduce recall. This angle examines the quality assurance infrastructure around search construction.

**Search queries:**
- `"PRESS" checklist "peer review" "search strategy" "systematic review"`
- `"librarian" "information specialist" "search strategy" review quality "systematic review"`
- `"search peer review" "evidence-based" "literature search" error detection`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (JMLA, Journal of the Canadian Health Libraries Association, Health Information & Libraries Journal); CADTH methodological reports; PRESS checklist documentation.

---

## Angle 14: Hand-Searching and Journal-Level Retrieval

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Cochrane methodology; Evidence Synthesis; Library Science

**Rationale:** Hand-searching specific journals known to be core to a field supplements database searching and identifies studies missed due to indexing gaps or inconsistent metadata. This is a documented technique in Cochrane methodology.

**Search queries:**
- `"hand searching" "systematic review" "journal searching" coverage recall`
- `"hand-search" "electronic search" comparison "systematic review" additional studies`
- `"targeted journal searching" "supplementary search" "literature review" methodology`

**Best source types:** Academic journals (Systematic Reviews, Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews); Cochrane Handbook; methodological comparison studies.

---

## Angle 15: Adjacent Discipline — Query Expansion in Information Retrieval Systems

**Vocabulary tradition / field:** Information Retrieval (Computer Science); Search Engine research; NLP

**Rationale:** The IR community has developed formal query expansion techniques (pseudo-relevance feedback, word embeddings, ontology-based expansion) that are directly applicable to systematic search but are largely siloed from the evidence synthesis community.

**Search queries:**
- `"query expansion" "systematic review" OR "literature search" recall improvement`
- `"ontology-based query expansion" "literature retrieval" biomedical`
- `"word embeddings" "query expansion" "information retrieval" recall precision evaluation`

**Best source types:** Preprints (arXiv cs.IR); conference proceedings (ACM SIGIR, ECIR, CIKM); academic journals (Information Retrieval Journal, ACM Transactions on Information Systems); GitHub repositories of IR systems.

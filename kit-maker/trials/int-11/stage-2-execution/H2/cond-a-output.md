## Angle 1: Internal vs. External Load Dichotomy

**Vocabulary tradition:** Sports science distinguishes "external load" (what the athlete physically does — GPS distance, speed, power output) from "internal load" (how the body responds — heart rate, RPE, blood lactate). Much of the foundational literature uses this framing rather than "training load monitoring" as a single term.

**Search queries:**
- `"external load" "internal load" endurance athletes GPS power monitoring`
- `"external training load" "internal training load" coupling cycling OR running OR triathlon`
- `external load quantification "power output" physiological response endurance sport`

## Angle 2: Training Impulse (TRIMP) and Load Quantification Models

**Vocabulary tradition:** Bannister's Training Impulse (TRIMP, 1991) is the foundational load-quantification model. The literature uses TRIMP, session-RPE, Lucia's TRIMP, and the Coggan chronic/acute training load (CTL/ATL) model as distinct vocabularies. Work searching only for "training load monitoring" will miss this theoretical architecture.

**Search queries:**
- `"training impulse" TRIMP "power output" GPS endurance performance`
- `"chronic training load" "acute training load" CTL ATL cycling OR triathlon`
- `Coggan "training stress score" TSS GPS "normalized power" athlete monitoring`

## Angle 4: Critical Power and W' (W Prime) Model

**Vocabulary tradition:** The Critical Power (CP) model characterizes the power-duration relationship through two parameters: critical power and W' (anaerobic work capacity). This is a physiological-mechanical vocabulary used heavily in cycling and running research that is distinct from general "load monitoring" language but directly operationalizes GPS-derived and power-derived data.

**Search queries:**
- `"critical power" "W prime" training load monitoring endurance athletes`
- `"power-duration relationship" W' fatigue GPS pacing endurance`

## Angle 7: GPS Accuracy, Calibration, and Measurement Error

**Failure-case angle:** The measurement validity literature examines where GPS data breaks down — urban canyons, tree cover, sampling rate effects, device-to-device variability. This angle is invisible to performance-focused searches but is foundational to trusting any GPS-derived load metric.

## Angle 8: Power Meter Validity and Reliability

**Failure-case angle:** Power output data is only as good as the instrument. A substantial validation literature benchmarks cycling power meters (SRM, Quarq, Stages, Favero, Garmin) against laboratory ergometers and each other. Running power is newer and less validated. This literature must be read to assess data quality assumptions.

**Search queries:**
- `"power meter" validity reliability cycling "training load" SRM comparison`
- `power meter agreement "criterion validity" field testing endurance athlete`
- `"running power" GPS "power output" validity reliability endurance sport`

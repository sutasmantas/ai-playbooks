## Angle 1: Peripheral Muscle Fatigue (Metabolic Physiology Vocabulary)

**Perspective:** Fatigue originating in the contracting muscle itself — metabolic byproduct accumulation, substrate depletion at the fiber level, cross-bridge cycling failure. This vocabulary (acidosis, Pi accumulation, reactive oxygen species) is distinct from CNS or cardiovascular framing and captures a large body of muscle physiology literature that uses different terminology than "fatigue."

**Search queries:**
- `("skeletal muscle fatigue" OR "metabolic fatigue") AND ("endurance exercise" OR "prolonged exercise") AND ("lactate" OR "phosphate accumulation" OR "acidosis")`
- `"muscle contractile failure" AND ("marathon" OR "cycling" OR "triathlon") AND ("cross-bridge" OR "actin-myosin")`

## Angle 2: Central Governor / Central Nervous System Fatigue

**Perspective:** The "central governor" model (Noakes) and related CNS-regulation perspectives treat fatigue as a protective afferent signal to the brain rather than a peripheral failure event. This tradition uses vocabulary like "central fatigue," "afferent feedback," "supraspinal," and "transcranial magnetic stimulation" — terms that will not overlap with metabolic physiology searches.

**Search queries:**
- `"central governor" AND ("endurance" OR "prolonged exercise") AND ("fatigue" OR "pacing")`
- `"supraspinal fatigue" AND "endurance exercise" AND ("transcranial magnetic stimulation" OR "motor cortex excitability")`
- `"central fatigue hypothesis" AND ("afferent feedback" OR "group III IV afferents") AND ("exercise" OR "running" OR "cycling")`

## Angle 3: Cardiovascular Drift and Oxygen Delivery Failure

**Perspective:** During prolonged exercise, cardiovascular drift (rising HR, falling stroke volume, reduced cardiac output) can limit oxygen delivery to working muscle independently of metabolic byproducts. This framing uses cardiology and hemodynamics vocabulary — "cardiac drift," "stroke volume," "VO2 plateau" — that falls in a different literature cluster.

**Search queries:**

## Angle 4: Thermoregulatory Fatigue / Hyperthermia

**Perspective:** Heat stress during prolonged exercise causes core temperature rise that independently limits performance and is mechanistically distinct from metabolic or cardiovascular fatigue. The thermal physiology vocabulary ("critical core temperature," "hyperthermia," "heat strain," "sweat rate") captures a specialized literature.

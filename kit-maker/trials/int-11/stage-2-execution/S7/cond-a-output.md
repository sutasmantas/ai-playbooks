## Angle 1 — Direct Terminology: "Carbohydrate Periodization"

**Vocabulary tradition:** Sports nutrition mainstream (Burke, Hawley, Jeukendrup lineage)  
**Rationale:** The canonical term entered the literature around 2016–2018. Searching it directly captures review papers and RCTs that explicitly adopt it, but misses earlier mechanistic work that predates the label.

**Queries:**
1. `"carbohydrate periodization" cyclists endurance performance`
2. `"periodized carbohydrate" training adaptation review`
3. `"carbohydrate periodization" protocol "randomized controlled trial"`

## Angle 2 — Train-Low / Compete-High Paradigm

**Vocabulary tradition:** Train-low exercise physiology (Hawley & Burke 2010 coinage)  
**Rationale:** Much of the mechanistic and early-applied literature uses "train low, compete high" as its framing rather than "periodization." These papers would not surface under Angle 1 despite covering identical interventions.

**Queries:**
1. `"train low" "compete high" cycling performance`
2. `"train low" glycogen availability endurance adaptation`
3. `"low glycogen training" mitochondrial adaptation cyclists`

## Angle 3 — Sleep-Low Strategy

**Vocabulary tradition:** Specific named protocol within train-low (Marquet et al. 2016 onwards)  
**Rationale:** Sleep-low is a distinct implementation variant where athletes deplete glycogen in an evening session and train fasted the following morning. It has its own terminology and clinical cohorts distinct from generic train-low or CHO periodization.

**Queries:**
1. `"sleep low" carbohydrate strategy endurance cyclists`
2. `"sleep low train low" protocol performance fat oxidation`
3. `"overnight fasted training" cycling adaptation glycogen`

## Angle 5 — Metabolic Flexibility and Fat Adaptation

**Vocabulary tradition:** Metabolic flexibility / low-carbohydrate high-fat (LCHF) / keto-adaptation  
**Rationale:** A competing literature frames the goal not as glycogen manipulation but as permanently increasing fat oxidation capacity. This vocabulary tradition (Volek, Phinney, et al.) overlaps in intervention but diverges sharply in mechanism and conclusion, so it requires separate search terms.

**Queries:**
1. `"metabolic flexibility" endurance athletes carbohydrate fat oxidation`
2. `"fat adaptation" cyclists performance "carbohydrate availability"`
3. `"low carbohydrate high fat" endurance performance periodization`

## Angle 6 — Molecular Signaling: AMPK / PGC-1alpha Pathway

**Vocabulary tradition:** Molecular exercise physiology  
**Rationale:** Papers that study why train-low produces training adaptations operate at the signaling pathway level. They rarely use the words "periodization" or "cyclist." This angle retrieves the mechanistic bedrock literature that justifies CHO periodization protocols.

**Queries:**

## Angle 7 — Failure-Case / Harm: Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)

**Vocabulary tradition:** RED-S / relative energy deficiency / female athlete triad  
**Rationale:** Carbohydrate restriction interventions can produce RED-S, hormonal disruption, and immunosuppression, particularly in female and lean male cyclists. The harms literature sits in a completely separate vocabulary tradition from the performance literature and is systematically missed by benefits-focused searches.

**Queries:**
1. `"relative energy deficiency" cyclists carbohydrate restriction performance`
2. `"low energy availability" endurance athletes carbohydrate periodization outcomes`
3. `"RED-S" OR "female athlete triad" carbohydrate restriction cycling`

## Angle 8 — Null and Negative Results

**Vocabulary tradition:** Publication-bias corrected literature; training-quality impairment studies  
**Rationale:** Training-quality degradation under glycogen restriction is a documented failure mode. Papers finding no performance benefit or active harm are systematically underrepresented in review literature. Searching explicitly for null and negative outcomes corrects this bias.

**Queries:**
1. `"carbohydrate periodization" "no significant" OR "no effect" performance cyclists`
2. `"train low" performance impairment OR "negative" endurance`
3. `"glycogen restriction" training quality impairment OR "reduced performance" cyclists`

## Angle 9 — Gut Adaptation and Carbohydrate Absorption Capacity

**Vocabulary tradition:** Intestinal physiology / gut training (Jeukendrup gut-training framework)  
**Rationale:** High-carbohydrate competition fueling (90+ g/hr) requires trained intestinal absorption. CHO periodization interacts with gut adaptation in both directions: train-low phases may down-regulate gut transporters while high-carb phases upregulate them. This physiology has its own literature.

**Queries:**
1. `"gut training" carbohydrate absorption endurance athletes`
2. `"intestinal adaptation" high carbohydrate intake cyclists performance`
3. `"multiple transportable carbohydrates" gut training endurance SGLT1`

**Rationale:** Sports scientists apply the training-periodization model (Matveyev / Issurin lineage) to nutrition, using terms like "competition phase nutrition," "macrocycle fueling," and "nutritional periodization" distinct from session-level CHO manipulation. This retrieves applied-coaching and team-sport literature not found by biochemistry searches.

**Queries:**

## Angle 11 — Practical Implementation: Elite and Professional Cycling

**Vocabulary tradition:** Applied sports nutrition / practitioner literature / case studies  
**Rationale:** Peer-reviewed controlled trials use tightly constrained laboratory protocols. Real-world implementation by professional teams involves confounders absent from RCTs. Case-study and practitioner literature (often in IJSN Applied, JSCR, practitioner journals) has separate keyword conventions.

**Queries:**

## Angle 12 — Sex Differences in Carbohydrate Metabolism

**Vocabulary tradition:** Sex-specific sports physiology; female athlete metabolism; menstrual cycle nutrition  
**Rationale:** The majority of train-low and CHO periodization RCTs used male subjects. Female athletes show different baseline fat oxidation rates and different hormonal responses to glycogen restriction. A dedicated search for sex-specific work retrieves a population that is systematically absent from the canonical literature.

**Queries:**
1. `sex differences carbohydrate metabolism endurance exercise periodization`
2. `"female cyclists" carbohydrate periodization performance hormonal`
3. `"menstrual cycle" carbohydrate availability endurance performance`

## Angle 13 — Adjacent Field: Military and Occupational Endurance Performance

**Vocabulary tradition:** Military human performance research; occupational physiology  
**Rationale:** The US Army Research Institute of Environmental Medicine and analogous bodies have published extensively on glycogen manipulation, fasted training, and carbohydrate loading under load-bearing endurance conditions. This literature is rarely cited in cycling contexts but contains well-controlled RCTs on CHO timing and restriction that are methodologically transferable.

**Queries:**
1. `military endurance carbohydrate restriction fasted training performance adaptation`
2. `"occupational athlete" OR "military athlete" glycogen manipulation endurance`
3. `soldier load-bearing carbohydrate periodization nutrition performance`

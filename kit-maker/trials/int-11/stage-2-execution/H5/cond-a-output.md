# Research Angles: CGM for Endurance Sport Performance Optimization

**Topic:** Continuous glucose monitoring for performance optimization in endurance sport.

Generated: 2026-07-07

---

## Angle 1 — Exercise Physiology / Substrate Utilization Vocabulary

Most sports science literature does not use the phrase "continuous glucose monitoring" — it uses substrate, fuel selection, and oxidation terminology. Searching this vocabulary reaches exercise physiology studies that measure blood glucose as a dependent variable without framing it as a monitoring technology.

**Search queries:**
- `"carbohydrate oxidation" AND "endurance" AND ("blood glucose" OR "plasma glucose") AND "performance"`
- `"substrate utilization" AND "exercise intensity" AND "glucose kinetics" AND "athlete"`
- `"glycogen availability" AND "fatigue" AND "blood glucose regulation" AND "endurance sport"`

---

## Angle 2 — Wearable Biosensor / Digital Health Technology Vocabulary

The engineering and digital health literature classifies CGM as a wearable biosensor or real-time physiological monitor rather than a sports tool. This angle finds device validation studies, signal processing work, and feasibility trials in active populations that never appear under sport-science keywords.

**Search queries:**
- `"continuous glucose monitor" AND "wearable" AND ("exercise" OR "physical activity") NOT "diabetes"`
- `"interstitial glucose" AND "biosensor" AND "athletic" OR "sport" OR "endurance training"`
- `"real-time glucose monitoring" AND ("healthy adults" OR "non-diabetic") AND "exercise"`

---

## Angle 3 — Glycemic Variability and Time-in-Range Metrics

Glycemic variability research has its own metric vocabulary (TIR, MAGE, CV%) that is rarely used in sports science papers. This vocabulary tradition surfaces studies on how glucose fluctuation patterns — not just absolute values — predict performance outcomes or recovery quality.

**Search queries:**
- `"glycemic variability" AND ("endurance" OR "ultramarathon" OR "cycling" OR "triathlon") AND "performance"`
- `"time in range" AND "athlete" AND "glucose" AND ("training" OR "competition")`
- `"mean amplitude of glycemic excursions" OR "MAGE" AND "exercise" AND "non-diabetic"`

---

## Angle 4 — Metabolic Flexibility and Fat Adaptation

The metabolic flexibility literature uses distinct vocabulary ("fat oxidation capacity," "Randle cycle," "metabolic switching") and is largely separate from both CGM technology literature and mainstream sports nutrition. It directly bears on whether glucose tracking reveals anything meaningful for athletes who train in low-carbohydrate states.

**Search queries:**
- `"metabolic flexibility" AND "endurance athlete" AND ("glucose" OR "CGM" OR "continuous monitoring")`
- `"fat adaptation" AND "glucose response" AND ("exercise" OR "training") AND "performance"`
- `"low carbohydrate" AND "endurance" AND "blood glucose kinetics" AND ("performance" OR "fatigue")`

---

## Angle 5 — Exercise-Induced Hypoglycemia / Bonking Failure Case

The failure-case literature — what goes wrong when glucose management fails — uses clinical and physiological vocabulary ("exercise-induced hypoglycemia," "central fatigue hypothesis," "glucoregulation failure") largely absent from optimization-framed papers. This angle finds the boundary conditions that CGM-based strategies should protect against.

**Search queries:**
- `"exercise-induced hypoglycemia" AND "endurance" AND ("mechanism" OR "prevention" OR "incidence")`
- `"hypoglycemia" AND "prolonged exercise" AND "glucoregulation" AND ("athlete" OR "healthy")`
- `"central fatigue" AND "glucose" AND "endurance" AND ("brain glucose" OR "CNS" OR "central nervous system")`

---

## Angle 6 — Non-Diabetic Population CGM Validity

Most CGM device validation work is performed on diabetic populations. A separate strand asks whether CGM readings in healthy, insulin-sensitive athletes are accurate enough to act on — device accuracy under exercise conditions (sweating, arm movement, skin temperature). This is a prerequisite question for the entire optimization premise.

**Search queries:**
- `"continuous glucose monitor" AND "accuracy" AND ("healthy" OR "non-diabetic") AND "exercise"`
- `"CGM" AND "interstitial glucose lag" AND "exercise" AND "validation"`
- `"glucose sensor accuracy" AND "physical activity" AND "wearable" AND ("calibration" OR "bias")`

---

## Angle 7 — Carbohydrate Periodization and Nutrient Timing

Sports nutrition research on carbohydrate periodization — "train low, compete high," sleep-low strategies — is mechanistically about glucose availability but rarely uses CGM as a measurement tool. Searching this vocabulary finds studies where CGM could have been used but wasn't, revealing open research gaps and establishing what glucose patterns the fueling strategies actually produce.

**Search queries:**
- `"carbohydrate periodization" AND "endurance" AND ("glucose" OR "glycogen") AND "performance"`
- `"train low" AND "blood glucose" AND "adaptation" AND "endurance athlete"`
- `"nutrient timing" AND "intra-exercise" AND "carbohydrate" AND "glucose response" AND "performance"`

---

## Angle 8 — Interindividual Glycemic Response / Personalized Nutrition

Personalized nutrition research (Zeevi et al. lineage) shows large interindividual variation in postprandial glucose responses to identical foods in non-diabetic people, mediated partly by gut microbiome. This angle asks whether the same variation exists under exercise loads, and whether CGM could identify responder vs non-responder profiles in athletes.

**Search queries:**
- `"postprandial glucose" AND "interindividual variation" AND ("exercise" OR "athlete" OR "sport")`
- `"personalized nutrition" AND "glycemic response" AND "exercise" AND "endurance"`
- `"gut microbiome" AND "glucose response" AND "exercise" AND "athletic performance"`

---

## Angle 9 — Military / Tactical Performance Literature

Military human performance science is a parallel track that studies prolonged exertion, fueling, and cognitive function under load — often with funding and access that civilian sports science lacks. It uses vocabulary like "sustained operations," "load carriage," "cognitive-motor performance under energy deficit." This literature contains glucose monitoring studies that never reach sports science journals.

**Search queries:**
- `"continuous glucose" AND "sustained operations" AND ("military" OR "soldier" OR "tactical")`
- `"glucose monitoring" AND "prolonged physical exertion" AND "cognitive performance" AND ("energy deficit" OR "caloric restriction")`
- `"blood glucose" AND "load carriage" AND "performance" AND "fatigue"`

---

## Angle 10 — Null / Absence-of-Effect Angle

A standard keyword search will systematically under-return studies that found CGM or glucose monitoring did not improve performance, or that glucose tracking is redundant with simpler measures (perceived exertion, heart rate). Explicitly searching for null and critical findings guards against confirmation bias in the synthesis.

**Search queries:**
- `"glucose monitoring" AND "endurance performance" AND ("no effect" OR "no significant difference" OR "not associated")`
- `"CGM" AND "athlete" AND ("limitations" OR "critique" OR "reliability") AND "performance"`
- `"blood glucose" AND "performance outcome" AND "endurance" AND ("confounded" OR "not predictive" OR "poor correlation")`

---

## Angle 11 — Lactate / Multi-Biomarker Context

Lactate threshold testing is the established gold standard in endurance physiology. CGM enters a context where another real-time metabolic marker already dominates. The comparative literature — when glucose adds vs. subtracts from lactate-based interpretation — is found in metabolomics and multi-biomarker studies, not in CGM technology papers.

**Search queries:**
- `"lactate" AND "glucose" AND "endurance" AND "real-time" AND ("monitoring" OR "wearable" OR "sensor")`
- `"blood lactate" AND "blood glucose" AND "training load" AND "athlete" AND ("concurrent" OR "combined")`
- `"multi-biomarker" AND "endurance performance" AND ("glucose" OR "CGM") AND ("metabolic profile" OR "physiological monitoring")`

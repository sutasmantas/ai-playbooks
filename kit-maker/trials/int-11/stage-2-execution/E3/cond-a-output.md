## Angle 1: Cardiovascular Physiology and VO2max

**What it finds:** The canonical sports science literature on maximal oxygen uptake, cardiac output, and aerobic capacity. Core vocabulary of exercise physiology textbooks and classic randomized trial literature.

**Why it would be missed:** Searches using only "endurance running" or "aerobic exercise" miss studies that frame outcomes as VO2max improvements or stroke volume adaptation without naming the sport.

**Search queries:**
- `"maximal oxygen uptake" AND ("training adaptation" OR "endurance performance") AND ("cardiac output" OR "stroke volume")`
- `VO2max AND ("high-intensity interval training" OR "polarized training") AND "longitudinal"`
- `"aerobic capacity" AND "training intervention" AND ("running economy" OR "cycling efficiency")`

## Angle 2: Mitochondrial Biogenesis and Cellular Adaptation

**What it finds:** Molecular and cell biology literature on how endurance training remodels skeletal muscle at the subcellular level. PGC-1alpha signaling, mitochondrial density, and oxidative enzyme activity.

**Why it would be missed:** This literature sits in biochemistry and cell biology journals, not sports science journals, and uses molecular terminology rather than performance terminology.

**Search queries:**
- `"PGC-1alpha" AND ("endurance training" OR "aerobic exercise") AND ("mitochondrial biogenesis" OR "oxidative phosphorylation")`
- `"AMPK" AND "exercise" AND ("mitochondrial density" OR "oxidative capacity") AND "skeletal muscle"`
- `"mitochondrial adaptations" AND "endurance exercise" AND ("citrate synthase" OR "succinate dehydrogenase")`

## Angle 3: Lactate Dynamics and Metabolic Thresholds

**What it finds:** Literature on lactate threshold, lactate turnpoint, MLSS (maximal lactate steady state), and how training shifts the lactate-power curve. Bridges physiology and applied training prescription.

**Why it would be missed:** The vocabulary is inconsistent across traditions — "anaerobic threshold," "lactate threshold," "VT2," "OBLA," and "MLSS" all describe related but not identical constructs. A single-term search misses the others.

**Search queries:**
- `("lactate threshold" OR "maximal lactate steady state" OR "MLSS") AND ("endurance training" OR "training adaptation")`
- `"blood lactate" AND ("training intensity" OR "exercise intensity distribution") AND performance`
- `("anaerobic threshold" OR "ventilatory threshold" OR "OBLA") AND "endurance" AND ("validity" OR "comparison" OR "agreement")`

## Angle 4: Training Load, Dose-Response, and Periodization

**What it finds:** Applied sports science literature on how training volume, intensity, and structure should be organized over time. Includes polarized, pyramidal, and threshold-dominant training distribution models, and the chronic training load/acute training load ratio.

**Why it would be missed:** This literature uses sport-management and coaching science terminology — "training monotony," "training strain," "ATL:CTL ratio," "training load monitoring" — that does not appear in physiology-first searches.

**Search queries:**
- `"training load" AND ("acute:chronic" OR "ATL" OR "CTL") AND ("endurance" OR "running" OR "cycling")`
- `"polarized training" AND ("threshold training" OR "pyramidal") AND ("performance" OR "VO2max" OR "lactate")`
- `"periodization" AND "endurance" AND ("meta-analysis" OR "systematic review") AND ("performance outcome" OR "training adaptation")`

## Angle 5: Central Fatigue and Neuromuscular Mechanisms

**What it finds:** Literature framing endurance limits not as peripheral oxygen delivery failures but as central nervous system fatigue, motor unit recruitment failure, and afferent inhibitory feedback from the muscles. Includes the "central governor" model.

**Why it would be missed:** Standard physiology searches focus on peripheral (cardiovascular/metabolic) mechanisms. Central fatigue research is scattered across neurophysiology, exercise science, and sports medicine and uses distinct vocabulary.

**Search queries:**
- `"central fatigue" AND "endurance exercise" AND ("motor unit" OR "electromyography" OR "neuromuscular")`
- `"central governor" AND ("endurance performance" OR "exercise limitation") AND ("pacing" OR "self-regulation")`
- `"muscle afferent" AND ("group III" OR "group IV") AND "exercise" AND ("fatigue" OR "performance limitation")`

## Angle 6: Overtraining Syndrome and Maladaptation (Failure-Case Angle)

**What it finds:** Literature on what goes wrong when training load exceeds recovery — overtraining syndrome (OTS), non-functional overreaching (NFOR), relative energy deficiency in sport (RED-S). This is the failure mode of endurance training, and it reveals the system's limits.

**Why it would be missed:** The adaptive case and the failure case are almost never reviewed together. Failure-case vocabulary — "overreaching," "RED-S," "underperformance syndrome," "HPA axis suppression" — does not appear in performance optimization searches.

**Search queries:**
- `("overtraining syndrome" OR "non-functional overreaching") AND ("biomarker" OR "diagnosis" OR "prevention") AND endurance`
- `"relative energy deficiency in sport" AND ("endurance athlete" OR "running" OR "cycling") AND ("performance" OR "bone health" OR "hormonal")`
- `"overreaching" AND ("HRV" OR "heart rate variability" OR "cortisol") AND ("endurance training" OR "athletic performance")`

## Angle 7: Skeletal Muscle Fiber Type and Phenotypic Plasticity

**What it finds:** Histology and muscle physiology literature on Type I vs. Type II fiber distribution, how training shifts fiber type proportions, and how fiber type relates to endurance performance.

**Why it would be missed:** This is muscle biology vocabulary, not sports science vocabulary. Studies in this tradition measure percent Type I area, fiber cross-sectional area, and myosin heavy chain isoform shifts.

## Angle 8: Heat Acclimatization and Environmental Physiology

**What it finds:** The literature on how endurance performance degrades in heat and how heat training or acclimatization improves subsequent performance — including at sea level. Also covers plasma volume expansion as a mechanism.

**Why it would be missed:** Environmental physiology is a separate subfield. The performance benefits of heat acclimation protocols are often published in thermal biology or aviation medicine journals, not sports science journals.

**Search queries:**
- `"heat acclimation" AND ("endurance performance" OR "VO2max" OR "time trial") AND ("plasma volume" OR "thermoregulation")`
- `"exercise in heat" AND ("cardiovascular drift" OR "core temperature") AND ("performance" OR "fatigue")`
- `"heat training" AND "sea-level performance" AND ("hemoglobin mass" OR "plasma volume expansion")`

## Angle 9: Altitude Training and Hypoxic Exposure

**What it finds:** The large literature on live high-train low (LHTH/LHTL), intermittent hypoxic training (IHT), and altitude camps as a performance intervention. Includes hematological and non-hematological adaptation mechanisms.

**Why it would be missed:** Altitude research spans altitude medicine, applied sports science, and hematology. It is indexed under both "hypoxia" and "altitude" with no consistent preferred term.

**Search queries:**
- `("live high train low" OR "LHTL") AND ("hemoglobin mass" OR "VO2max" OR "red blood cell") AND endurance`

- `"altitude camp" AND ("elite athlete" OR "endurance runner" OR "cyclist") AND ("performance" OR "hematological")`

**Why it would be missed:** Running economy as a physiological construct sits at the intersection of biomechanics and physiology. Pure physiology searches miss papers that measure force plate kinematics, vertical oscillation, and leg spring stiffness.
